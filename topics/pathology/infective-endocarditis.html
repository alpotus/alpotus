INFECTIVE ENDOCARDITIS

DEFINITION

Infective endocarditis is a microbial infection of the heart valves or endocardium leading to formation of vegetations composed of thrombotic debris and organisms, often associated with destruction of the underlying cardiac tissue.

INTRODUCTION

TABLE: | Point | Description | | Nature of disease | Microbial infection of valves or endocardium | | Lesion formed | Vegetations containing thrombotic debris + organisms | | Tissue damage | Associated with destruction of underlying cardiac tissue | | Other sites | Aorta, aneurysmal sacs, blood vessels, prosthetic devices | | Etiology | Mostly bacterial; rarely fungi, rickettsiae, chlamydia | | Classification basis | Based on tempo and severity | | Spectrum | Overlap exists between acute and subacute forms |

CLASSIFICATION

FLOWCHART: Infective endocarditis is classified based on the virulence of the organism and the condition of the underlying valve -> When highly virulent organisms infect even normal valves, the disease progresses rapidly with severe destruction, leading to acute infective endocarditis -> Acute infective endocarditis is characterized by rapid progression, severe valve destruction, and high morbidity and mortality even with treatment -> When less virulent organisms infect previously damaged or abnormal valves, the disease develops slowly over weeks to months, leading to subacute infective endocarditis -> Subacute infective endocarditis has an insidious onset and prolonged course, and most patients recover with appropriate antibiotic therapy -> Many cases lie between these two extremes, forming a continuous clinical spectrum

RISK FACTORS

TABLE: | Risk Factor | Explanation | | Rheumatic heart disease | Damaged valves predispose to infection | | Mitral valve prolapse | Leading current predisposing lesion | | Bicuspid aortic valve | Structural abnormality | | Calcific valvular stenosis | Provides abnormal surface | | Prosthetic valves | Account for 10–20% of cases | | Pacemaker lines / catheters | Cause endothelial injury and thrombus formation | | IV drug use | Direct introduction of organisms into bloodstream | | Immunodeficiency / neutropenia | Reduced host defense | | Malignancy, diabetes, alcohol use | Increase susceptibility and worsen outcome | | Transient bacteremia | From dental/surgical procedures or infections |

PATHOGENESIS

FLOWCHART: Microorganisms gain entry into the bloodstream through sources such as dental procedures, infections, intravenous drug use, or minor injuries -> This leads to bacteremia, allowing microbes to circulate in the blood -> Predisposing factors such as damaged valves, prosthetic material, or endothelial injury create sites where platelet-fibrin thrombi are deposited -> These sterile thrombi act as a nidus for microbial adherence -> Circulating microorganisms adhere to these sites and begin to proliferate -> Bacteria become embedded within layers of fibrin and platelets, forming vegetations -> These vegetations protect organisms from host immune defenses and antibiotics, allowing persistent infection -> The growing vegetations cause local destruction of valve tissue and may extend into surrounding myocardium forming abscesses -> Fragments of friable vegetations break off and enter circulation as septic emboli -> These emboli lodge in distant organs, causing infarction, abscess formation, and vascular infections such as mycotic aneurysms

ETIOLOGY (IMPORTANT ORGANISMS LINKED TO SETTING)

TABLE: | Organism | Clinical Association | | Streptococcus viridans | Community-acquired, subacute, damaged valves | | Staphylococcus aureus | Acute, highly virulent, normal or damaged valves, IV drug users, healthcare settings | | Enterococci | Part of normal flora, opportunistic | | HACEK group | Oral commensals | | Gram-negative bacilli | Rare | | Fungi | Rare, severe | | Culture-negative cases (10%) | Due to prior antibiotics or difficult isolation |

CLINICAL FEATURES — MECHANISM OF DEVELOPMENT

FLOWCHART: Microbial infection of valves leads to formation of vegetations containing organisms and inflammatory material -> These organisms and inflammatory mediators enter circulation, producing systemic inflammatory response, which causes fever -> In acute infective endocarditis, highly virulent organisms such as Staphylococcus aureus cause rapid tissue destruction, leading to sudden onset of high fever, chills, and severe systemic symptoms such as lassitude -> In subacute infective endocarditis, less virulent organisms produce a slower and less intense inflammatory response, leading to insidious symptoms such as fatigue, weight loss, and a flulike illness -> Chronic immune stimulation in subacute disease leads to enlargement of spleen, resulting in splenomegaly -> Vegetations on valves disrupt normal blood flow and valve function, producing turbulent flow that manifests as cardiac murmurs, especially in left-sided lesions -> Fragments of vegetations break off and form microemboli that travel through circulation -> These microemboli lodge in small vessels of skin and other tissues, causing petechiae -> Embolization to nail bed capillaries produces linear hemorrhages called splinter hemorrhages -> Emboli lodging in retinal vessels cause retinal hemorrhages known as Roth spots -> Septic emboli reaching palms and soles produce painless erythematous lesions called Janeway lesions -> Immune complex deposition and vascular inflammation in fingers lead to painful nodules known as Osler nodes -> Continuous bacteremia allows detection of organisms in blood cultures -> Structural damage to valves and vegetations can be visualized by echocardiography, confirming diagnosis

COMPLICATIONS — MECHANISM OF DEVELOPMENT

FLOWCHART: Vegetations formed on valves are friable and contain large numbers of microorganisms -> Fragments of these vegetations break off and enter circulation as septic emboli -> These emboli lodge in distant organs, leading to infarction and formation of abscesses due to infection at the site -> Infection of arterial walls by these emboli leads to weakening and dilation, forming mycotic aneurysms -> Persistent infection leads to continuous antigen release into circulation -> Antigen-antibody complexes form and deposit in glomeruli, causing glomerulonephritis -> Glomerular injury leads to hematuria, albuminuria, and may progress to renal failure -> Extension of infection into myocardium and conduction system disrupts electrical pathways, leading to arrhythmias -> Ongoing systemic infection leads to septicemia, causing widespread inflammatory response and organ dysfunction -> Valve destruction leads to severe regurgitation and cardiac dysfunction -> Combination of embolization, cardiac damage, renal involvement, and systemic infection worsens prognosis -> If untreated, progressive infection and complications ultimately lead to death

MORPHOLOGY

IMPORTANT EXPLANATORY POINTS

In both acute and subacute infective endocarditis, vegetations are present on heart valves. These vegetations are friable, bulky, and potentially destructive, and consist of fibrin, inflammatory cells, and microorganisms.

The most commonly affected valves are the aortic and mitral valves, although the tricuspid valve is commonly involved in intravenous drug users. Vegetations may be single or multiple and may involve more than one valve.

These vegetations can extend into the underlying myocardium and form abscess cavities called ring abscesses. Because vegetations are friable, embolization is common. Since emboli contain organisms, they frequently produce abscesses, septic infarcts, and mycotic aneurysms at distant sites.

MORPHOLOGY TABLE

TABLE: | Feature | Morphological Description | | Vegetations | Friable, bulky, destructive masses | | Composition | Fibrin + inflammatory cells + microorganisms | | Valve involvement | Aortic and mitral most common | | Tricuspid valve | Common in IV drug users | | Number | Single or multiple | | Multi-valve involvement | Possible | | Myocardial extension | Ring abscess formation | | Embolization | Common due to friable nature | | Embolic outcome | Abscesses, septic infarcts | | Vascular effect | Mycotic aneurysms |

FINAL CORE

  • Infection of valves → vegetation formation
  • Vegetation = fibrin + microbes + inflammatory cells
  • Acute → rapid, destructive, virulent organisms
  • Subacute → slow, insidious, less virulent organisms
  • Key events → bacteremia → adhesion → vegetation → embolization
  • Major dangers → emboli, abscess, glomerulonephritis, heart damage
  • Untreated disease → fatal
