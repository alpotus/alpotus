NEPHRITIC VS NEPHROTIC SYNDROME

Nephritic and nephrotic syndromes are two distinct clinical entities resulting from glomerular disease, distinguished primarily by the nature of the injury to the glomerular filtration barrier and the subsequent laboratory and clinical findings.

KEY DIFFERENCES

TABLE: | Feature | Nephritic Syndrome | Nephrotic Syndrome | | Primary Pathophysiology | Inflammation within the glomeruli causing capillary wall injury | Derangement in capillary walls leading to massive increase in permeability to proteins | | Onset | Abrupt and acute | Insidious and slow-developing | | Hematuria | Gross or microscopic; urine smoky brown, cola-colored, or tea-colored | Rare or absent | | Red Cell Morphology | Dysmorphic red cells and red cell casts | Generally normal | | Proteinuria | Mild to moderate; sub-nephrotic range | Massive (>3.5 g/day adults / >1 g/m²/day children) | | Edema | Mild; periorbital puffiness, facial swelling; usually non-pitting | Severe generalized; pitting edema → anasarca | | Blood Pressure | Hypertension common | Usually normal or mildly increased | | GFR and Azotemia | Diminished GFR → azotemia (↑BUN, creatinine) | Usually normal initially | | Lipid Profile | No significant changes | Hyperlipidemia + lipiduria | | Serum Albumin | Usually normal | Hypoalbuminemia (<3 g/dL) | | Glomerular Pathology | Hypercellularity, mesangial/endothelial proliferation, leukocyte infiltration | Podocyte injury → foot process effacement |

CLINICAL CONTEXT

  • Nephritic Syndrome
    Often follows infection such as post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
    Type III hypersensitivity reaction
    Inflammation → capillary rupture → hematuria
  • Nephrotic Syndrome
    Heavy protein loss exceeds liver synthesis
    ↓ oncotic pressure → massive edema
    Common causes:
    Minimal Change Disease, FSGS, Membranous Nephropathy
