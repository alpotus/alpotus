CHRONIC PYELONEPHRITIS

DEFINITION

Chronic pyelonephritis is a chronic tubulointerstitial kidney disease characterized by chronic inflammation and scarring of the renal parenchyma, specifically involving the renal calyces and pelvis.
  • It is a serious condition that can lead to irreversible kidney destruction
  • It is a major cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) and end-stage renal disease (ESRD), particularly in children with severe urinary tract abnormalities

1. PATHOGENESIS AND TYPES

  • Chronic pyelonephritis is generally categorized into two major forms based on the underlying predisposing factors:
  • Chronic Reflux-Associated Pyelonephritis (Reflux Nephropathy) — This is the more common form.
  • It results from the retrograde flow of infected urine from the bladder into the ureters and then deep into the renal parenchyma (intrarenal reflux).
  • This most often occurs at the upper and lower poles of the kidney where the papillae are flattened or concave, facilitating reflux.
  • Chronic Obstructive Pyelonephritis
  • This occurs when mechanical obstruction (such as stones, prostatic hypertrophy, or congenital anomalies) causes urinary stasis.
  • Stasis allows bacteria to multiply unhindered, leading to repeated bouts of acute inflammation and eventual scarring.

2. MORPHOLOGY (STRUCTURAL CHANGES)

  • The hallmark of chronic pyelonephritis is that it affects the calyces and pelvis, which distinguishes it from most other chronic tubulointerstitial diseases.

Gross Appearance

  • The kidneys are typically asymmetrically contracted and irregularly scarred.
  • The scars are coarse and discrete, often located at the upper and lower poles.
  • The underlying calyces are dilated, blunted, or deformed (a feature known as calyceal clubbing).

Microscopic Appearance

  • Thyroidization
    • Many tubules are dilated and filled with glassy, PAS-positive eosinophilic casts
    • Renal tissue resembles thyroid follicles
  • Tubulointerstitial Injury
    • Tubular atrophy
    • Interstitial fibrosis
    • Chronic inflammatory infiltrate (lymphocytes and plasma cells)
  • Glomerular Changes
    • Glomeruli may initially be spared
    • Eventually undergo secondary focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)
    • Also develop global sclerosis due to progressive loss of nephron mass

3. CLINICAL FEATURES

  • Chronic pyelonephritis is often insidious or silent in its onset, making it difficult to detect early.
  • Symptoms
    • Vague back pain
    • Fever
    • Bouts of pyuria and bacteriuria
    • May mimic other abdominal conditions like peptic ulcers
  • Tubular Dysfunction
    • Earliest defect → inability to concentrate urine
    • Leads to polyuria and nocturia
  • Hypertension
    • Very common manifestation
    • Leads to further renal damage
  • Renal Failure
    • Progression leads to uremia
    • Symptoms: fatigue, malaise, headache

4. INVESTIGATIONS AND DIAGNOSIS

  • Urinalysis
    • Proteinuria (usually mild, but can reach nephrotic range in advanced stages)
    • Microscopic hematuria
    • Leukocyte casts
  • Urine Specific Gravity
    • Isosthenuria (~1.010)
    • Reflects loss of concentrating ability
  • Imaging
    • Intravenous Pyelography (IVP) or CT scans
    • Findings:
      • Asymmetrically contracted kidneys
      • Characteristic coarse scars
      • Blunted, deformed calyces
  • Urine Culture
    • Essential for detecting active infection
    • May be sterile in later chronic stages
