BRONCHIECTASIS

DEFINITION

Bronchiectasis is a disorder in which destruction of smooth muscle and elastic tissue by inflammation stemming from persistent or severe infections leads to permanent dilation of bronchi and bronchioles.

INTRODUCTION

  • Now uncommon due to better control of lung infections
  • Occurs when airway clearance is impaired
  • Leads to persistent infection and chronic inflammation
  • Results in permanent structural damage of airways

RISK FACTORS

TABLE: | Category | Conditions | | Congenital / hereditary | cystic fibrosis, intralobar sequestration, immunodeficiency, primary ciliary dyskinesia, Kartagener syndrome | | Infections | severe necrotizing pneumonia (bacterial, viral, fungal) | | Obstruction | tumor, foreign body, mucus impaction | | Immune disorders | rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, inflammatory bowel disease, posttransplant | | Idiopathic | defective host immunity (≈50%) |

PATHOGENESIS

FLOWCHART: Obstruction and infection are major factors -> Defect in airway clearance develops -> Secretions accumulate distally -> Chronic infection persists -> Inflammation damages airway wall -> Smooth muscle and elastic tissue are destroyed -> Bronchi become permanently dilated -> Smaller bronchioles are obliterated due to fibrosis (bronchiolitis obliterans)

Cystic Fibrosis Mechanism

FLOWCHART: Ion transport defect produces thick viscous secretions -> Mucociliary clearance is impaired -> Airway obstruction develops -> Chronic infection occurs -> Airway wall damage leads to bronchiectasis

Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia

FLOWCHART: Ciliary motor protein defect causes ciliary dysfunction -> Mucus is not cleared -> Recurrent infections occur -> Chronic inflammation damages airways -> Bronchiectasis develops
  • Associated with situs inversus and sinusitis (Kartagener syndrome)
  • Males → infertility due to sperm dysmotility

Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis

FLOWCHART: Aspergillus sensitization activates Th2 cells -> Cytokines recruit eosinophils -> Airway inflammation increases -> Mucus plugs form -> Airway obstruction leads to bronchiectasis

MORPHOLOGY

Distribution

  • Lower lobes commonly involved due to gravity-dependent secretion pooling
  • Distal bronchi and bronchioles more affected due to poor clearance
  • Localized disease in obstruction

Gross

  • Airways dilated up to four times normal due to wall destruction
  • Bronchi visible up to pleural surface due to abnormal dilation
  • Cystic spaces filled with mucopurulent secretions due to infection

Microscopy

Chronic inflammation

  • Lymphocytes and macrophages persist due to ongoing infection
  • Causes continuous tissue injury

Desquamation

  • Shedding of epithelial cells into lumen
  • Loss of ciliated epithelium impairs mucus clearance

Ulceration

  • Complete loss of epithelium
  • Exposes underlying tissue and vessels
  • Leads to bleeding

Squamous metaplasia

  • Ciliated columnar epithelium replaced by squamous epithelium
  • Occurs due to chronic irritation
  • Squamous cells lack cilia → clearance worsens

Necrosis

  • Tissue death due to severe inflammation
  • Leads to destruction of airway wall
  • Can form lung abscess

Fibrosis

  • Collagen deposition during healing
  • Causes thick, rigid airway walls

Peribronchiolar fibrosis

  • Fibrosis around bronchioles
  • Compresses airway externally

Obliteration of smaller bronchioles

  • Lumen closed by fibrotic tissue
  • Called bronchiolitis obliterans
  • Causes airflow obstruction

Microbiology

  • Haemophilus influenzae most common
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa frequent
  • Others include non-tuberculous mycobacteria
  • Occurs due to favorable mucus-rich environment

CLINICAL FEATURES

1. Persistent cough

  • Due to mucus accumulation and airway irritation

2. Foul-smelling sputum

  • Due to pus and bacterial breakdown

3. Hemoptysis

  • Due to erosion of inflamed vessels

4. Dyspnea

  • Due to airway obstruction and poor ventilation

5. Orthopnea (severe cases)

  • Due to impaired lung function in supine position

6. Episodic symptoms

  • Triggered by infections

7. Morning cough

  • Secretions drain when position changes

8. Cyanosis

  • Due to hypoxemia from respiratory insufficiency

COMPLICATIONS

  • Cor pulmonale due to chronic hypoxia
  • Brain abscess due to septic emboli
  • Amyloidosis due to chronic inflammation
  • Now less common due to improved treatment

FINAL CORE

FLOWCHART: Defect in airway clearance -> Pooling of secretions -> Chronic infection -> Persistent inflammation -> Wall destruction -> Bronchi dilate -> Small bronchioles obliterate -> Cycle continues
