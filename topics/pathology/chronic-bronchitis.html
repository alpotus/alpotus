CHRONIC BRONCHITIS

DEFINITION

Chronic bronchitis is defined clinically as persistent cough with sputum production for at least 3 months in at least 2 consecutive years in the absence of any other identifiable cause.
  • Longstanding chronic bronchitis is associated with progressive lung dysfunction, which may be so severe as to lead to hypoxemia, pulmonary hypertension, and cor pulmonale.

PATHOGENESIS

CORE INITIATING FACTOR

FLOWCHART: Exposure to noxious or irritating inhaled substances such as tobacco smoke (90% of those affected are smokers) and dust from grain, cotton, and silica -> Repeated epithelial injury and irritation -> Initiation of chronic bronchitis

MECHANISMS

1. Mucus hypersecretion

FLOWCHART: Hypersecretion of mucus in the large airways -> Enlargement of the submucosal glands in the trachea and bronchi -> Inflammatory mediators such as histamine and IL-13 contribute -> Marked increase in goblet cells in small airways (small bronchi and bronchioles) -> Excessive mucus production -> Airway obstruction
  • The enlargement of submucosal glands and increase in goblet cells are protective reactions against tobacco smoke or other pollutants (sulfur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide)

2. Acquired CFTR dysfunction

FLOWCHART: Smoking leads to acquired CFTR dysfunction -> Secretion of abnormal, dehydrated mucus -> Increased viscosity of mucus -> Exacerbation of severity of chronic bronchitis

3. Inflammation

FLOWCHART: Inhalants cause cellular damage -> Acute and chronic inflammatory responses -> Cells involved: neutrophils, lymphocytes, macrophages -> Long-standing inflammation -> Fibrosis in small airways (bronchi and bronchioles less than 2–3 mm in diameter) -> Chronic airway obstruction

4. Infection

FLOWCHART: Infection does not initiate chronic bronchitis -> Maintains disease -> Produces acute exacerbations

EFFECTS OF CIGARETTE SMOKE

FLOWCHART: Cigarette smoke damages airway-lining cells -> Leads to chronic inflammation -> Interferes with ciliary action of respiratory epithelium -> Prevents clearance of mucus -> Increases risk of infection

CLINICAL CONSEQUENCE MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Mucus hypersecretion + airway inflammation -> Airway narrowing and obstruction -> Impaired ventilation -> Hypoxemia develops -> Chronic hypoxemia causes pulmonary vasoconstriction -> Pulmonary hypertension develops -> Right ventricular strain -> Cor pulmonale

MORPHOLOGY

GROSS

  • Hyperemia, swelling, and edema of the mucous membranes → because chronic irritation causes vasodilation and increased vascular permeability leading to congestion and fluid accumulation
  • Frequently accompanied by excessive mucinous or mucopurulent secretions → because mucus hypersecretion combines with inflammatory exudate and infection
  • Sometimes heavy casts of secretions and pus fill the bronchi and bronchioles → because impaired ciliary clearance leads to accumulation and stagnation of mucus and pus

MICROSCOPIC FEATURES

1. Chronic inflammation of the airways

  • (predominantly lymphocytes and macrophages)
  • → because persistent epithelial injury leads to continuous recruitment of inflammatory cells

2. Thickening of the bronchiolar wall

  • Smooth muscle hypertrophy → because chronic irritation and inflammatory mediators stimulate smooth muscle proliferation as an adaptive response
  • Deposition of extracellular matrix in the muscle layer → because fibroblast activation during chronic inflammation leads to collagen and matrix deposition
  • Peribronchial fibrosis → because repeated injury and healing cycles result in fibrous tissue deposition around airways
  • 👉 These changes together → narrow airway lumen and contribute to chronic airway obstruction

3. Goblet cell hyperplasia

  • → because chronic irritation stimulates epithelial differentiation into mucus-secreting goblet cells as a protective response

4. Enlargement of mucus-secreting glands

  • → because persistent stimulation increases gland size and mucus production

KEY FEATURE

  • Most striking change is an increase in the size of the mucous glands → because chronic stimulation continuously drives mucus production leading to gland enlargement

AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Mucus hypersecretion + goblet cell hyperplasia -> Excess mucus accumulation -> Impaired ciliary clearance -> Mucus plugging -> Inflammation + fibrosis -> Narrowing of bronchioles -> Chronic airway obstruction

ADVANCED CHANGES

  • Mucus plugging, inflammation, and fibrosis may lead to marked narrowing of bronchioles → because luminal obstruction and wall thickening reduce airway diameter
  • In the most severe cases, there may be obliteration of lumen due to fibrosis (bronchiolitis obliterans) → because fibrotic tissue replaces normal airway architecture completely

EPITHELIAL CHANGES

  • Squamous metaplasia → because chronic irritation replaces normal ciliated epithelium with more resistant squamous epithelium
  • Dysplasia → because mutagenic effects of tobacco smoke cause abnormal cellular proliferation
