POLYCYSTIC KIDNEY DISEASE (PKD)

DEFINITION

Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) is a heterogeneous group of hereditary disorders characterized by the formation of multiple expanding cysts in both kidneys that ultimately destroy the renal parenchyma.
  • It is a major cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) and end-stage renal disease (ESRD).
  • There are two primary forms:
    • Autosomal Dominant (Adult) PKD
    • Autosomal Recessive (Childhood) PKD
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) causes cysts to form when inherited genetic mutations (PKD1, PKD2, or PKHD1) disrupt normal kidney tubule structure, causing epithelial cells to overproliferate, separate, and fill with fluid.
  • These fluid-filled sacs grow slowly, destroying healthy kidney tissue and leading to reduced kidney function.

1. AUTOSOMAL DOMINANT POLYCYSTIC KIDNEY DISEASE (ADPKD)

  • ADPKD is a common condition affecting approximately 1 in 400 to 1000 live births and is responsible for 5% to 10% of cases requiring dialysis or transplantation.
  • It typically presents in mid-adult life, around the fourth or fifth decade.

Genetics and Pathogenesis

  • PKD1 Gene: Mutations in this gene (chromosome 16p13.3) account for about 85% of cases.
  • It encodes polycystin-1, a large membrane protein involved in cell-cell and cell-matrix interactions.
  • PKD2 Gene: Mutations in this gene (chromosome 4q21) account for most remaining cases and encode polycystin-2, which functions as a calcium-permeable cation channel.
  • Ciliary Hypothesis: Polycystin-1 and -2 are localized to the primary cilium of tubular epithelial cells.
  • These cilia act as mechanosensors.
  • Defects in their function lead to altered calcium flux and abnormal cellular signaling, resulting in progressive cyst formation which leads to Damage to glomerulus and vessels and interstitial fibrosis.

Morphology

  • The kidneys become bilaterally enlarged, sometimes weighing up to 4 kg each.
  • The surface is replaced by a mass of cysts up to 4 cm in diameter, though functioning nephrons remain dispersed between them.
  • Cysts arise from all segments of nephron example : glomeruli , pct , dct, and CT.
  • Cysts may show clear serious fluid which is red to brown sometimes hemorrhagic fluid

Clinical Features

  • Patients often experience chronic flank pain, hematuria, and a dragging sensation due to the massive kidney size.
  • Hypertension is common (found in over half of patients) and accelerates the decline in renal function.

Extrarenal Manifestations

  • About 40% of patients develop liver cysts.
  • Other systemic associations include intracranial berry aneurysms (causing death in 4%–10% of cases), mitral valve prolapse, and cysts in the pancreas or lungs.

2. AUTOSOMAL RECESSIVE POLYCYSTIC KIDNEY DISEASE (ARPKD)

  • ARPKD is rarer and more severe, often presenting at birth or in early infancy.

Genetics

  • It is caused by mutations in the PKHD1 gene (chromosome 6p21–p23) encoding fibrocystin (or polyductin), another protein localized to the primary cilium.
  • It interacts with polycystin-2 (PC2) to regulate fluid flow sensation, calcium signaling, and maintain proper renal tubular structure, with its dysfunction causing Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD).

    • Morphology

  • The kidneys are enlarged but maintain a smooth external surface.
  • On cut section, the cortex and medulla are replaced by numerous small, cylindrical or saccular dilations of the collecting tubules, giving the kidney a spongelike appearance.

Clinical Features

  • Neonates may present with oliguria, respiratory insufficiency, and palpable abdominal masses.
  • A hallmark of ARPKD is its association with congenital hepatic fibrosis, which can lead to portal hypertension in children who survive the neonatal period.

3. DIAGNOSIS AND INVESTIGATIONS

  • Imaging: Ultrasonography is the gold standard for diagnosis and screening family members.
  • ADPKD shows multiple irregular cysts, while ARPKD presents as bulky, bright (echogenic) kidneys.
  • CT and MRI can provide more detailed anatomical information.
  • Laboratory Tests: Urinalysis may show mild proteinuria or hematuria.
  • Renal function tests demonstrate a gradual decline in GFR.
  • Genetic Testing: Linkage analysis and molecular techniques can be used to identify PKD1 and PKD2 mutations in familial cases.

4. MANAGEMENT

  • Management focuses on controlling symptoms and slowing disease progression:
  • Blood Pressure Control: Strict management of hypertension using ACE inhibitors or Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs) is critical to limit glomerular hyperfiltration and proteinuria.
  • Managing Complications: Treatment of urinary tract infections, renal stones, and pain is essential.
  • Renal Replacement Therapy: For patients progressing to end-stage renal failure, dialysis or renal transplantation are the only definitive treatments.
  • Solid organ transplantation can also address associated complications like severe hepatic fibrosis.
