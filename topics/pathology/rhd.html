RHEUMATIC VALVULAR DISEASE

DEFINITION

Rheumatic valvular disease is the chronic cardiac manifestation of rheumatic fever characterized by inflammation of all parts of the heart with predominant valvular involvement leading to deforming fibrotic mitral stenosis.

INTRODUCTION

TABLE: | Point | Description | | Nature of disease | Rheumatic fever is an acute, immunologically mediated, multisystem inflammatory disease | | Cause | Occurs after group A β-hemolytic streptococcal infections | | Common site of infection | Usually pharyngitis, occasionally skin infections | | Cardiac involvement | Rheumatic heart disease is the cardiac manifestation | | Layers involved | Inflammation of all parts of the heart | | Most important damage | Valvular inflammation and scarring produce major clinical features | | Main valvular lesion | Deforming fibrotic mitral stenosis | | Unique feature | Essentially the only cause of acquired mitral stenosis | | Epidemiology (high-income countries) | Declined due to improved socioeconomic conditions, rapid diagnosis and treatment, and decreased virulence | | Epidemiology (low-income countries) | Remains most important acquired heart disease in children and young adults |

RISK FACTORS

TABLE: | Risk Factor | Explanation | | Group A β-hemolytic streptococcal infection | Preceding infection provides antigenic stimulus | | Genetic susceptibility | Only 1–3% develop cross-reactive immune response | | Low socioeconomic status | Increased exposure and delayed treatment | | Delayed or inadequate treatment | Allows immune-mediated injury to develop | | Virulent streptococcal strains | Certain M proteins strongly induce immune response |

PATHOGENESIS

FLOWCHART: Group A β-hemolytic streptococcal infection introduces antigens such as M proteins into the host -> The host immune system generates antibodies and T cells directed against these streptococcal antigens -> These immune responses cross-react with host proteins present in the myocardium and cardiac valves -> Antibodies activate complement and macrophages, while T cells release cytokines, leading to inflammation -> Inflammation produces Aschoff bodies and involves all layers of the heart (pancarditis) -> Healing leads to fibrosis, especially affecting valves -> Valve thickening, fusion, and deformity develop, causing stenosis and regurgitation, mainly of the mitral valve

CLINICAL FEATURES — MECHANISM OF DEVELOPMENT

FLOWCHART: After streptococcal infection, an immune-mediated inflammatory response develops and affects multiple tissues including joints, heart, skin, and central nervous system -> Fever develops because inflammatory cytokines released during immune activation act on the hypothalamus and raise body temperature -> Migratory polyarthritis occurs because immune-mediated inflammation affects synovial membranes of large joints, and the inflammation shifts from one joint to another as the immune response evolves -> When the heart is involved, pancarditis develops, meaning inflammation affects pericardium, myocardium, and endocardium -> Pericardial inflammation produces fibrin deposition on pericardial surfaces, which rub against each other during cardiac movement, leading to a pericardial friction rub -> Myocardial inflammation damages cardiac muscle fibers and the conduction system, leading to arrhythmias -> Damage to myocardium reduces contractility, causing cardiac dilation due to inability of the heart to pump effectively -> This reduced pumping ability combined with valvular dysfunction leads to accumulation of blood in the heart and lungs, resulting in congestive heart failure -> Inflammation of valves and dilation of cardiac chambers leads to improper closure of mitral valve, producing functional mitral insufficiency -> Immune-mediated inflammation in subcutaneous tissues leads to formation of subcutaneous nodules -> Immune-mediated vascular inflammation in the skin produces erythema marginatum -> Immune-mediated involvement of basal ganglia in the brain leads to Sydenham chorea characterized by involuntary purposeless movements -> The symptoms appear after a delay of 2 to 3 weeks because time is required for the adaptive immune response to develop, and by this time streptococci are no longer present

COMPLICATIONS — MECHANISM OF DEVELOPMENT

FLOWCHART: Chronic inflammation heals by fibrosis, leading to thickening, fusion, and deformation of heart valves, especially the mitral valve -> Mitral valve stenosis develops because fibrotic thickening and commissural fusion narrow the valve opening -> Narrowing of the mitral valve causes obstruction to blood flow from left atrium to left ventricle, resulting in increased pressure in the left atrium -> Sustained pressure overload leads to progressive dilation of the left atrium -> Dilated left atrium undergoes electrical remodeling, leading to atrial fibrillation -> Atrial fibrillation causes ineffective atrial contraction and blood stasis within the atrium -> Blood stasis predisposes to formation of mural thrombi within the left atrium -> These thrombi can detach and travel through circulation, causing thromboembolic events -> Increased left atrial pressure is transmitted backward into pulmonary veins, leading to pulmonary congestion -> Chronic pulmonary congestion causes pulmonary vascular changes and increased pulmonary arterial pressure, resulting in pulmonary hypertension -> Pulmonary hypertension increases resistance against which the right ventricle must pump, leading to right ventricular hypertrophy -> Prolonged pressure overload eventually causes right ventricular failure -> Damaged and deformed valves provide a surface for bacterial colonization, increasing the risk of infective endocarditis -> Combined effects of valvular stenosis, regurgitation, and myocardial dysfunction lead to chronic congestive heart failure

MORPHOLOGY

IMPORTANT EXPLANATORY POINTS

Acute rheumatic fever is characterized by discrete inflammatory foci within a variety of tissues. The myocardial inflammatory lesions called Aschoff bodies are pathognomonic for rheumatic fever. These are collections of lymphocytes (primarily T cells), scattered plasma cells, and plump activated macrophages called Anitschkow cells associated with zones of fibrinoid necrosis.

Anitschkow cells have abundant cytoplasm and nuclei with chromatin centrally condensed into a slender, wavy ribbon producing the characteristic “caterpillar cell” appearance.

Aschoff bodies can be found in pericardium, myocardium, and endocardium including valves, producing pancarditis.

ACUTE RHEUMATIC FEVER — MORPHOLOGY TABLE

TABLE: | Structure | Morphological Features | | Pericardium | Fibrinous exudate that usually resolves without sequelae | | Myocardium | Scattered Aschoff bodies in interstitial connective tissue | | Aschoff bodies | Lymphocytes, plasma cells, Anitschkow cells with fibrinoid necrosis | | Anitschkow cells | Activated macrophages with “caterpillar-like” nuclei | | Endocardium/Valves | Fibrinoid necrosis and fibrin deposition along lines of closure | | Vegetations (verrucae) | 1–2 mm vegetations causing little disturbance in function |

CHRONIC RHEUMATIC HEART DISEASE

Chronic disease results from organization of acute inflammation and subsequent scarring. Aschoff bodies are replaced by fibrous scar and are rarely seen.

Valve cusps and leaflets become permanently thickened and retracted. Commissural fusion and shortening occur, and chordae tendineae become thickened and fused. Fibrous bridging across commissures and calcification produce “fish-mouth” or “buttonhole” stenosis.

Microscopy shows neovascularization and diffuse fibrosis obliterating normal leaflet architecture.

CHRONIC MORPHOLOGY TABLE

TABLE: | Feature | Morphological Change | | Valve cusps/leaflets | Thickened and retracted | | Commissures | Fused | | Chordae tendineae | Thickened, shortened, and fused | | Fibrous bridging | Present across commissures | | Calcification | Produces deformity | | Valve shape | Fish-mouth / buttonhole stenosis | | Microscopy | Neovascularization + diffuse fibrosis | | Architecture | Normal leaflet structure obliterated | | Aschoff bodies | Rare/absent |

VALVE INVOLVEMENT PATTERN

TABLE: | Valve | Frequency | | Mitral valve alone | 70% | | Mitral + Aortic | 25% | | Tricuspid | Less frequent and less severe | | Pulmonic | Almost always spared |

FUNCTIONAL CONSEQUENCES

TABLE: | Change | Why it occurs | | Mitral stenosis | Due to fibrosis, commissural fusion, and calcification | | Left atrial dilation | Due to pressure overload from stenotic valve | | Atrial fibrillation | Due to structural and electrical remodeling | | Mural thrombi | Due to blood stasis | | Pulmonary congestion | Due to backward pressure transmission | | Pulmonary hypertension | Due to chronic elevated pulmonary pressure | | Right ventricular hypertrophy | Due to increased pulmonary resistance | | Right ventricular failure | Due to prolonged overload | | Left ventricle normal | Obstruction is proximal at mitral valve |

FINAL CORE

  • Autoimmune disease following streptococcal infection
  • Molecular mimicry is key
  • Pancarditis in acute stage
  • Aschoff bodies are pathognomonic
  • Chronic fibrosis leads to valvular deformity
  • Mitral stenosis is most important outcome
