CLINICAL FEATURES OF COPD

1. Smoking History

FLOWCHART: Most affected patients have a smoking history of 40 pack-years or greater -> Chronic exposure to tobacco smoke continuously injures airway epithelium and alveoli -> This leads to persistent inflammation, mucus hypersecretion, and alveolar wall destruction -> These structural changes together result in the development of COPD

2. Onset of Disease

FLOWCHART: COPD often presents insidiously with slowly increasing dyspnea on exertion and chronic cough with sputum production, slight at first but increasing over time -> Airway narrowing due to mucus and fibrosis + loss of elastic recoil due to emphysema -> Air trapping and lung hyperinflation progressively develop -> During exertion, oxygen demand increases but ventilation is insufficient -> Dyspnea on exertion appears and gradually worsens -> Simultaneously, mucus accumulation stimulates cough reflex -> Sputum production increases over time

3. Exacerbations

FLOWCHART: Other patients present with exacerbations caused by superimposed infection that can lead to confusion with other disorders, such as asthma (due to wheezing) -> Impaired mucociliary clearance allows microorganisms to persist in airways -> Superimposed infection increases inflammation, mucus production, and airway edema -> Airway lumen becomes more narrowed -> Airflow becomes turbulent producing wheezing -> Clinical picture may resemble asthma during exacerbations

4. Spirometry

FLOWCHART: The most important diagnostic test is spirometry, which typically shows an FEV1/FVC ratio of less than 0.7 -> Airflow obstruction reduces the amount of air that can be forcefully exhaled in one second (FEV1) -> Total forced vital capacity (FVC) is relatively less affected -> FEV1 decreases disproportionately compared to FVC -> The ratio falls below 0.7, confirming obstructive lung disease

5. Symptom Pattern

FLOWCHART: Once COPD appears, symptoms often wax and wane over time and are generally worse in the morning -> During sleep, coughing and mucociliary clearance are reduced -> Mucus accumulates in the airways overnight -> On waking, airway obstruction is increased -> Symptoms such as cough and dyspnea are worse in the morning

6. Clinical Spectrum

FLOWCHART: The clinical picture varies according to the severity of the disease and the relative contributions of emphysematous and bronchitic changes -> COPD consists of two main pathological processes: airway disease (bronchitis) and alveolar destruction (emphysema) -> The dominance of one process over the other determines the clinical phenotype

7. Emphysema Dominant — “Pink Puffers”

FLOWCHART: At one extreme end of the spectrum lie “pink puffers”, patients in whom emphysema dominates -> Alveolar wall destruction leads to loss of elastic recoil -> Air cannot be expelled efficiently during expiration -> Air trapping causes lung hyperinflation -> Chest becomes barrel-shaped and expiration is prolonged -> Patients adopt forward posture and breathe through pursed lips to prevent airway collapse -> Overdistention of lungs is severe and diffusion capacity is reduced due to loss of alveolar surface area -> Blood gas values remain relatively normal at rest due to compensatory hyperventilation -> Increased work of breathing leads to weight loss

8. Chronic Bronchitis Dominant — “Blue Bloaters”

FLOWCHART: At the other end lie patients with pure chronic bronchitis, referred to as “blue bloaters” -> Mucus hypersecretion and airway inflammation cause significant airway obstruction -> Ventilation becomes inadequate -> Carbon dioxide accumulates leading to hypercapnia -> Oxygen levels fall leading to hypoxemia -> Hypoxemia results in cyanosis (blue appearance) -> Chronic hypoxia leads to pulmonary hypertension and right heart failure -> Fluid retention occurs resulting in edema (bloater) -> Persistent cough with sputum remains the dominant symptom

9. Mixed Type

FLOWCHART: Most patients are somewhere in the middle, with signs and symptoms stemming from both bronchitic and emphysematous changes -> Both airway obstruction and alveolar destruction contribute simultaneously -> Patients exhibit a combination of dyspnea, cough, sputum, and hypoxemia

10. Treatment

FLOWCHART: Treatment options include smoking cessation, oxygen therapy, bronchodilators, corticosteroids, antibiotics, physical therapy, and surgical interventions -> Smoking cessation removes the primary cause of injury -> Bronchodilators reduce airway resistance and improve airflow -> Corticosteroids reduce inflammation -> Oxygen therapy corrects hypoxemia -> Antibiotics treat superimposed infections -> Surgical options reduce hyperinflation or replace diseased lung

11. Complications

Pulmonary Hypertension → Cor Pulmonale

FLOWCHART: Long-standing COPD causes chronic hypoxemia -> Hypoxemia induces pulmonary vasoconstriction -> Pulmonary vascular resistance increases -> Pulmonary hypertension develops -> Right ventricle undergoes strain and hypertrophy -> Cor pulmonale (right heart failure) occurs

Respiratory Failure

FLOWCHART: Acute infection superimposed on COPD -> Sudden increase in airway obstruction and inflammation -> Severe impairment of gas exchange -> Respiratory failure develops

Pneumothorax

FLOWCHART: In patients with emphysematous changes, subpleural blebs form -> Blebs rupture -> Air enters pleural space -> Lung collapses -> Pneumothorax, which may be fatal

FINAL CORE

FLOWCHART: Chronic smoking exposure -> Airway inflammation + mucus hypersecretion + alveolar destruction -> Airflow obstruction + air trapping + hyperinflation -> Dyspnea + cough + sputum production -> Hypoxemia + hypercapnia -> Pulmonary hypertension + cor pulmonale + respiratory failure
