Atherosclerosis is a specific form of arteriosclerosis because it is characterized by intimal-based lesions called atheromas or atherosclerotic plaques that protrude into vascular lumens.
FLOWCHART: Atheroma / Atherosclerotic plaque -> Consists of a raised lesion -> Contains a soft grumous core of lipid -> The lipid is mainly cholesterol and cholesterol esters -> The lipid core is covered by a fibrous cap. FLOWCHART: Atherosclerosis acts as a chronic inflammatory and healing response to injury of the vessel wall because endothelial damage initiates inflammation, lipid accumulation and repair -> Therefore it causes progressive mechanical obstruction of blood flow -> It may also produce catastrophic vascular thrombosis because plaque disruption exposes thrombogenic material.

Commonly Affected Vessels

  1. The abdominal aorta, especially the lower portion of the abdominal aorta and the iliac arteries, is affected most frequently because these vessels are more exposed to turbulent flow and pressure.
  2. The coronary arteries are commonly involved, especially the first several centimeters of the major branches because these regions experience marked turbulence.
  3. The popliteal arteries are frequently affected and therefore disease in these vessels compromises the blood supply to the lower legs.
  4. The internal carotid arteries are commonly involved, especially at the origin of the artery or at the carotid bifurcation because turbulence is greatest there.
  5. The circle of Willis is commonly involved because it forms the polygonal arterial anastomosis at the base of the brain.

Risk Factors

Constitutional Risk Factors
  • Increasing age is an important risk factor because plaque development is progressive and usually becomes clinically apparent in middle age or later. Between the ages of 40 and 60 years, the incidence of myocardial infarction increases fivefold.
  • Male gender is a risk factor because premenopausal women are relatively protected when compared with age-matched men.
FLOWCHART: Premenopausal women -> Relative protection compared with age-matched men -> This protection is lost if the woman has diabetes mellitus or hyperlipidemia -> After menopause, the risk in women increases -> Eventually becomes greater than that in men.
  • Genetics and family history are the most important independent risk factors because susceptibility is usually polygenic. The predisposition results from the cumulative effect of many shared alleles.
Modifiable Major Risk Factors
  • Hyperlipidemia, especially hypercholesterolemia, is the most important modifiable risk factor because excess cholesterol enters the arterial wall.
TABLE: | Lipoprotein | Characteristic | Effect on Atherosclerosis | | LDL cholesterol | Called “bad cholesterol” because it delivers cholesterol to peripheral tissues | Increased LDL strongly promotes atherosclerosis | | HDL cholesterol | Called “good cholesterol” because it mobilizes cholesterol from tissues and returns it for excretion | HDL protects against atherosclerosis |
  • Hypertension increases the risk of atherosclerosis because chronic high blood pressure damages the endothelium. Therefore the risk of ischemic heart disease increases by approximately 60%.
  • Cigarette smoking increases the risk because smoking produces endothelial injury and promotes thrombosis. Prolonged smoking doubles the death rate from ischemic heart disease.
  • Diabetes mellitus is an important risk factor because it induces hypercholesterolemia and accelerates atherosclerosis. Therefore the incidence of myocardial infarction becomes approximately twice as high.
Additional Risk Factors
  • Inflammation is an additional risk factor because atherosclerosis is fundamentally an inflammatory process. C-reactive protein is a stable and independent marker of risk for myocardial infarction and stroke.
  • Hyperhomocysteinemia is associated with increased risk because elevated serum homocysteine correlates with coronary atherosclerosis and peripheral vascular disease.
  • Metabolic syndrome increases the risk because it is a cluster of central obesity, insulin resistance, hypertension and dyslipidemia.
  • Lipoprotein(a) is an altered form of LDL and therefore it is associated with increased coronary and cerebrovascular risk.
  • Lack of exercise contributes to cardiovascular risk because physical inactivity promotes obesity and metabolic abnormalities.
  • Stress and Type A personality are linked to increased risk because competitive and stressful lifestyles may aggravate cardiovascular disease.

Etiopathogenesis

💡
The response-to-injury hypothesis explains atherosclerosis because the disease is considered a chronic inflammatory and healing response of the arterial wall to endothelial injury.
FLOWCHART: Hyperlipidemia + Hypertension + Cigarette smoking + Hemodynamic disturbances such as turbulence at arterial branch points -> Produce endothelial injury and dysfunction -> The injured endothelium becomes more permeable -> Lipoproteins enter the intima more easily -> The dysfunctional endothelium expresses adhesion molecules -> Leukocyte adhesion increases. FLOWCHART: LDL enters the intima -> LDL accumulates within the arterial wall -> Inflammatory cells generate free radicals -> LDL becomes oxidized -> Oxidized LDL is more atherogenic because it stimulates inflammation and is readily taken up by macrophages. FLOWCHART: Monocytes adhere to the endothelium -> Monocytes migrate into the intima -> Monocytes transform into macrophages -> Macrophages ingest modified LDL through scavenger receptors -> Macrophages become filled with lipid -> Foam cells are formed. FLOWCHART: Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) + Fibroblast growth factor (FGF) -> Recruit smooth muscle cells from the media or from circulating precursors -> Smooth muscle cells migrate into the intima -> Smooth muscle cells proliferate. FLOWCHART: Proliferating smooth muscle cells -> Synthesize extracellular matrix -> Produce collagen + Elastin + Proteoglycans -> Extracellular matrix stabilizes the growing plaque. FLOWCHART: Endothelial injury + Oxidized LDL + Foam cells + Smooth muscle proliferation + Extracellular matrix deposition -> Formation of an atherosclerotic plaque -> Mature plaque contains -> [Necrotic lipid core | Fibrous cap]

Development of Atherosclerotic Plaque

FLOWCHART: Fatty streak -> Earliest visible lesion -> Composed primarily of lipid-filled foamy macrophages -> Appears as flat yellow macules -> Found in virtually all adolescents. FLOWCHART: Fatty streak -> Progressive intimal thickening + Increasing lipid accumulation -> Atheromatous plaque develops.

The mature plaque contains three major components.

ATHEROMATOUS PLAQUE │ ├── Fibrous cap │ └── Superficial portion containing smooth muscle cells and dense collagen │ ├── Necrotic lipid core │ └── Deeper portion containing cholesterol, cell debris, foam cells and calcium │ └── Shoulder region └── Area at the edge of the cap containing numerous macrophages, T lymphocytes and smooth muscle cells
FLOWCHART: Progressive enlargement of plaque -> Calcification + Rupture + Erosion + Intraplaque hemorrhage -> Complicated plaque develops.

Morphology of Atherosclerotic Plaque

Gross Morphology
  • Atherosclerotic plaques appear as yellow-tan raised lesions because lipid accumulates within the arterial wall.
  • The lesions are usually patchy and eccentric because they involve only a portion of the circumference of the vessel wall rather than the entire circumference.
Microscopic Morphology
MICROSCOPIC COMPONENTS │ ├── Fibrous cap │ └── Composed of smooth muscle cells and dense collagen │ ├── Necrotic lipid core │ └── Contains lipid and necrotic debris │ ├── Foam cells │ └── Lipid-laden macrophages and smooth muscle cells │ ├── Cholesterol clefts │ └── Empty needle-shaped spaces remain because cholesterol crystals dissolve during tissue processing │ ├── Calcification │ └── Occurs in advanced lesions │ └── Neovascularization └── Small new blood vessels proliferate at the periphery of the plaque

Stable Versus Vulnerable Plaque

TABLE: | Feature | Stable Plaque | Vulnerable / Unstable Plaque | | Fibrous cap | Thick | Thin | | Necrotic lipid core | Small | Large | | Inflammation | Minimal | Marked with numerous macrophages | | Risk of rupture | Low | High | | Main manifestation | Stable angina due to gradual luminal narrowing | Myocardial infarction, stroke or sudden death due to plaque rupture and thrombosis |

Clinical Features

Atherosclerosis usually remains clinically silent for decades because plaques enlarge slowly.

FLOWCHART: Luminal narrowing reaches approximately 70% to 75% -> Critical stenosis develops -> Blood flow becomes insufficient -> Tissue ischemia occurs.
  • Gradual narrowing of coronary arteries produces stable angina because exertional chest pain develops when oxygen demand exceeds blood supply during exercise.
  • Gradual narrowing of arteries supplying the legs produces intermittent claudication because muscle pain or cramps develop during walking and disappear with rest.
⚠️
Acute changes in plaques such as rupture, erosion or thrombosis may suddenly obstruct blood flow and therefore produce myocardial infarction, stroke or sudden cardiac death.

Complications

  • Plaque rupture occurs because the fibrous cap is focally disrupted. This usually occurs in vulnerable plaques with thin caps and marked inflammation.
  • Fissuring occurs because the plaque develops cracks or splits and therefore the thrombogenic core becomes exposed to circulating blood.
  • Ulceration or erosion occurs because the endothelial lining is lost and therefore the underlying basement membrane and plaque contents become exposed.
  • Hemorrhage into the plaque occurs because either the fibrous cap ruptures or fragile neovessels break. Therefore the plaque suddenly enlarges because blood accumulates within it.
  • Superimposed thrombosis is the most important complication because thrombus forms over a disrupted plaque and may partially or completely occlude the vessel.
  • Atheroembolism or cholesterol embolism occurs because fragments of plaque or cholesterol crystals break away and travel to distal vessels.
  • Calcification occurs in advanced plaques because dystrophic calcification develops in areas of necrosis. Therefore the vessel becomes rigid and brittle.
  • Weakening of the vessel wall occurs because pressure atrophy and ischemic destruction damage the underlying media.
FLOWCHART: Weakening of the media -> Predisposes to aneurysm formation -> Especially in the abdominal aorta -> Abdominal aortic aneurysm becomes a major complication of atherosclerosis.

Consequences of Atherosclerotic Disease

ATHEROSCLEROTIC DISEASE │ ├── Coronary Artery Involvement │ └── Stable angina + Unstable angina + Myocardial infarction + Sudden cardiac death │ ├── Cerebral Artery Involvement │ └── Transient ischemic attack + Cerebral infarction / Stroke │ ├── Peripheral Artery Involvement │ └── Intermittent claudication + Gangrene of the lower limbs │ └── Abdominal Aortic Involvement └── Abdominal aortic aneurysm
