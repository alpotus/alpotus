MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION

DEFINITION

Myocardial infarction (MI), also commonly referred to as “heart attack,” is the death of cardiac muscle due to prolonged ischemia.

EPIDEMIOLOGY

TABLE: | Point | Description | | Incidence | Roughly 1.5 million individuals in the United States suffer an MI each year | | Mortality | Approximately 610,000 deaths annually | | Major underlying cause | The major underlying cause of IHD is atherosclerosis | | Age distribution | 10% of MIs occur in people younger than 40 years of age | | Age distribution | 45% occur in people younger than 65 years of age | | Age trend | The frequency rises progressively with increasing age and with increasing atherosclerotic risk factors | | Gender effect | Through middle age, male gender increases the relative risk of MI | | Female protection | Women are generally protected against MI during their reproductive years | | Postmenopausal effect | Postmenopausal decline in estrogen production is usually associated with accelerated CAD | | Outcome in women | IHD is the most common cause of death in older women | | Hormone therapy | Postmenopausal hormonal replacement therapy has not been shown to be protective and in some cases may be detrimental |

ETIOLOGY (CAUSES OF REDUCED CORONARY BLOOD FLOW)

TABLE: | Cause | Description | | Atherosclerosis | The major underlying cause of IHD leading to myocardial infarction | | Vasospasm | With or without coronary atherosclerosis, perhaps in association with platelet aggregation or due to drug ingestion (e.g., cocaine or ephedrine) | | Emboli | From the left atrium in association with atrial fibrillation | | Emboli | From a left-sided mural thrombus | | Emboli | From vegetations of infective endocarditis | | Emboli | From intracardiac prosthetic material | | Paradoxical emboli | From the right side of the heart or peripheral veins traversing a patent foramen ovale into coronary arteries | | Small vessel disorders | Disorders of small intramural coronary vessels (e.g., vasculitis) | | Hematologic abnormalities | Such as sickle cell disease | | Amyloid deposition | In vascular walls | | Vascular dissection | Disruption of coronary vessels | | Hypertrophy | Marked hypertrophy (e.g., due to aortic stenosis) | | Hypotension | Lowered systemic blood pressure (e.g., shock) | | Cardiac surgery | Inadequate myocardial protection during cardiac surgery |

PATHOGENESIS

1. CORONARY ARTERIAL OCCLUSION — CORE MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: An atheromatous plaque is eroded or suddenly disrupted by endothelial injury, intraplaque hemorrhage, or mechanical forces -> This exposes subendothelial collagen and necrotic plaque contents to the blood -> Platelets adhere, aggregate, and are activated -> Activated platelets release thromboxane A2, adenosine diphosphate (ADP), and serotonin -> These mediators cause further platelet aggregation and vasospasm -> Activation of coagulation by tissue factor and other mechanisms adds to the growing thrombus -> Within minutes, the thrombus can evolve to completely occlude the coronary artery lumen -> This results in abrupt reduction of coronary blood flow and myocardial ischemia

2. EVIDENCE AND TEMPORAL RELATION

FLOWCHART: Autopsy studies of patients dying of acute myocardial infarction demonstrate a high frequency of thrombotic occlusion -> Imaging studies show coronary thrombosis in almost 90% of cases when angiography is performed within 4 hours of onset -> When angiography is performed 12 to 24 hours after onset, thrombosis is seen in only 60% of patients -> This reduction occurs due to spontaneous lysis of thrombus or relaxation of vasospasm -> This explains why some occlusions may clear without intervention

3. THERAPEUTIC IMPLICATION

FLOWCHART: Early thrombolysis or angioplasty can restore coronary blood flow -> Restoration of blood flow limits the extent of myocardial necrosis -> This is because myocardium in the area at risk can be salvaged if perfusion is re-established early

4. NON-ATHEROTHROMBOTIC CAUSES

FLOWCHART: In approximately 10% of cases, myocardial infarction occurs in the absence of coronary atherothrombosis -> Reduced coronary blood flow may occur due to vasospasm, embolism, or other mechanisms -> These mechanisms include disorders of small intramural vessels such as vasculitis -> Hematologic abnormalities such as sickle cell disease may impair blood flow -> Amyloid deposition in vascular walls can reduce lumen size -> Vascular dissection can disrupt blood flow -> Marked hypertrophy (e.g., due to aortic stenosis) increases myocardial demand -> Lowered systemic blood pressure (e.g., shock) reduces coronary perfusion -> Inadequate myocardial protection during cardiac surgery can also cause ischemia -> All these mechanisms result in myocardial ischemia and infarction

5. EMBOLIC CAUSES

FLOWCHART: Emboli may arise from the left atrium in association with atrial fibrillation -> Emboli may originate from a left-sided mural thrombus -> Vegetations of infective endocarditis may embolize -> Intracardiac prosthetic material may act as a source of emboli -> Paradoxical emboli may arise from the right side of the heart or peripheral veins -> These emboli can traverse a patent foramen ovale and enter coronary arteries -> These emboli obstruct coronary blood flow and cause myocardial infarction

6. VASOSPASM

FLOWCHART: Coronary vasospasm may occur with or without atherosclerosis -> Vasospasm may be associated with platelet aggregation -> Vasospasm may also be induced by drugs such as cocaine or ephedrine -> Vasospasm reduces coronary blood flow -> This reduction leads to myocardial ischemia and infarction

7. MYOCARDIAL RESPONSE TO ISCHEMIA

FLOWCHART: Coronary arterial obstruction diminishes blood flow to a region of myocardium -> This region is referred to as the area at risk -> Ischemia leads to rapid myocardial dysfunction -> With prolonged vascular compromise, myocyte death occurs -> The outcome depends predominantly on severity and duration of flow deprivation

8. BIOCHEMICAL CHANGES

FLOWCHART: Within seconds of ischemia, aerobic metabolism ceases -> This leads to inadequate production of high-energy phosphates such as creatine phosphate and ATP -> Potentially noxious metabolites such as lactic acid accumulate -> This metabolic disturbance impairs myocardial function

9. FUNCTIONAL CHANGES

FLOWCHART: Myocardial contractility is highly dependent on oxygen and nutrients -> Within less than one minute of severe ischemia, myocardial contractility ceases -> This loss of contractility leads to decreased systolic function -> This occurs before myocyte death

10. ULTRASTRUCTURAL CHANGES

FLOWCHART: Within minutes of ischemia, ultrastructural changes develop -> These include myofibrillar relaxation -> Glycogen depletion occurs -> Cell and mitochondrial swelling develop -> These changes are initially reversible

11. REVERSIBLE VS IRREVERSIBLE INJURY

FLOWCHART: Experimental evidence shows that only severe ischemia with blood flow 10% or less of normal leads to irreversible damage -> This damage occurs when ischemia lasts for 20 to 30 minutes or longer -> Before this time, injury is potentially reversible -> This delay provides an opportunity for early intervention

12. ONSET OF NECROSIS

FLOWCHART: Irreversible injury results in disruption of sarcolemmal membrane integrity -> Intracellular macromolecules leak into interstitium and circulation -> These proteins form the basis of diagnostic blood tests -> This marks the onset of myocyte necrosis

13. MICROVASCULAR INJURY

FLOWCHART: With prolonged severe ischemia, injury to microvasculature follows injury to cardiac myocytes -> Microvascular damage contributes to progression of myocardial injury

14. SPATIAL PROGRESSION OF NECROSIS

FLOWCHART: Irreversible injury first occurs in the subendocardial zone -> This region is most vulnerable because it is the last to receive blood from epicardial vessels -> It is also exposed to high intramural pressures -> With continued ischemia, a wavefront of necrosis spreads outward -> This progression moves from subendocardium toward epicardium -> This process is driven by edema, reactive oxygen species, and inflammatory mediators

15. FINAL EXTENT OF INFARCTION

FLOWCHART: An infarct usually achieves its full extent within 3 to 6 hours -> In the absence of intervention, infarction may involve the entire wall thickness -> This produces a transmural infarct -> Early intervention within this window can reduce infarct size

CLASSIFICATION & PATTERNS OF INFARCTS

CLASSIFICATION OF INFARCTS

FLOWCHART: Myocardial infarction can be classified based on the extent and distribution of myocardial necrosis -> When there is occlusion of an epicardial coronary artery without intervention, the infarction involves nearly the entire thickness of the ventricular wall -> This produces a transmural infarction -> When there is partial or transient obstruction of a coronary artery, myocardial necrosis does not extend across the full thickness of the wall -> This produces a subendocardial (nontransmural) infarction -> When there is global hypotension or severe reduction in systemic blood pressure, myocardial damage occurs in a circumferential pattern involving the subendocardial region -> When there is involvement of small intramural vessels, multiple small areas of necrosis develop -> This produces multifocal microinfarctions

TYPES OF INFARCTS TABLE

TABLE: | Type of Infarct | Mechanism | Distribution | Key Features | | Transmural infarction | Occlusion of epicardial coronary artery (without intervention) | Full thickness of ventricular wall in distribution of affected coronary artery | Associated with chronic atherosclerosis, acute plaque change, and superimposed thrombosis | | Subendocardial (nontransmural) infarction | Partial or transient coronary obstruction OR global hypotension | Inner layer (subendocardial region), may be circumferential | Does not extend full thickness | | Multifocal microinfarction | Involvement of small intramural vessels | Multiple small areas of myocardium | Seen in microembolization, vasculitis, or vasospasm |

PATTERNS OF INFARCTION

FLOWCHART: The distribution of myocardial necrosis correlates with the location and nature of decreased perfusion -> When there is permanent occlusion of the left anterior descending artery -> Infarction involves anterior wall of left ventricle, anterior septum, and apex -> When there is permanent occlusion of the right coronary artery -> Infarction involves inferior/posterior wall of left ventricle, posterior septum, and sometimes right ventricular wall -> When there is permanent occlusion of the left circumflex artery -> Infarction involves lateral wall of left ventricle -> When there is transient or partial obstruction -> Subendocardial infarction occurs -> When there is global hypotension -> Circumferential subendocardial infarction occurs -> When there is obstruction of small intramural vessels -> Multifocal microinfarctions occur

CORRELATION OF ARTERY WITH INFARCT LOCATION

TABLE: | Coronary Artery | Infarct Location | | Left anterior descending (LAD) | Anterior LV wall, anterior septum, apex | | Right coronary artery (RCA) | Inferior/posterior LV wall, posterior septum, RV wall | | Left circumflex (LCX) | Lateral LV wall |

TRANSMURAL VS NON-TRANSMURAL

TABLE: | Feature | Transmural Infarct | Subendocardial Infarct | | Cause | Complete occlusion | Partial/transient occlusion OR hypotension | | Extent | Full thickness | Inner layer only | | Distribution | Territory of artery | Circumferential or regional | | Severity | More severe | Less severe | | Association | Atherosclerosis + thrombosis | Hypotension / partial block |

MICROINFARCTION

FLOWCHART: Small intramural vessel obstruction occurs due to microembolization, vasculitis, or vasospasm -> Catecholamines or drugs such as cocaine or ephedrine may induce vasospasm -> Catecholamines increase heart rate and myocardial contractility -> This increases oxygen demand and worsens ischemia -> This leads to multiple small infarcts (microinfarctions) -> Severe outcomes may include sudden cardiac death due to arrhythmia or ischemic dilated cardiomyopathy

MORPHOLOGY

INTRODUCTION

TABLE: | Point | Description | | Infarct type | Nearly all transmural infarcts involve at least a portion of the left ventricle (free wall + ventricular septum) | | Extent | They encompass nearly the entire perfusion zone of the occluded coronary artery | | Exception | A narrow rim (~0.1 mm) of viable subendocardial myocardium is preserved by diffusion from ventricular lumen |

CORONARY ARTERY INVOLVEMENT

TABLE: | Artery | Frequency | Infarct Distribution | | Left anterior descending (LAD) | 40–50% | Anterior LV wall near apex, anterior septum, apex circumferentially | | Right coronary artery (RCA) | 30–40% | Inferior/posterior LV wall, posterior septum, inferior/posterior RV wall (in some cases) | | Left circumflex (LCX) | 15–20% | Lateral LV wall except apex |

ADDITIONAL POINTS

  • Other arteries involved:
  • Left main coronary artery
  • Diagonal branches of LAD
  • Marginal branches of LCX
  • RCA infarcts:
  • 15–30% extend into right ventricular wall
  • Rare infarctions:
  • Isolated right ventricular infarction → 1–3%
  • Atrial infarction → rare

PATHOLOGICAL PROCESS

FLOWCHART: Damaged myocardium undergoes a progressive sequence of morphologic changes -> The predominant mechanism of cell death is ischemic coagulative necrosis -> Apoptosis may also occur but is not the dominant mechanism -> This is followed by a stereotypical inflammatory response -> Subsequently, tissue repair occurs -> The sequence parallels responses to injury in other tissues

CHANGES OF GROSS AND MICROSCOPY

TABLE: | Time | Gross Features | Light Microscopy | Electron Microscopy | | 0–1/2 hour | None | None | Relaxation of myofibrils; glycogen loss; mitochondrial swelling | | 1/2–4 hours | None | Usually none; variable waviness of fibers at border | Sarcolemmal disruption; mitochondrial amorphous densities | | 4–12 hours | Dark mottling (occasional) | Early coagulative necrosis; edema; hemorrhage | — | | 12–24 hours | Dark mottling | Ongoing coagulative necrosis; pyknosis of nuclei; myocyte hypereosinophilia; marginal contraction band necrosis; early neutrophilic infiltrate | — | | 1–3 days | Mottling with yellow-tan infarct center | Coagulative necrosis, with loss of nuclei and striations; brisk interstitial infiltrate of neutrophils | — | | 3–7 days | Hyperemic border; central yellow-tan softening | Beginning disintegration of dead myofibers; dying neutrophils; early macrophage phagocytosis | — | | 7–10 days | Maximally yellow-tan and soft, with depressed red-tan margins | Well-developed phagocytosis; granulation tissue at margins | — | | 10–14 days | Red-gray depressed infarct borders | Well-established granulation tissue with new blood vessels and collagen deposition | — | | 2–8 weeks | Gray-white scar (progressive) | Increased collagen deposition; decreased cellularity | — | | >2 months | Complete scarring | Dense collagenous scar | — |

EARLY MORPHOLOGICAL RECOGNITION

FLOWCHART: Early recognition of acute MI is difficult when death occurs within a few hours -> MIs less than 12 hours old are usually not apparent grossly -> Triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC) stain is used -> Intact myocardium stains brick-red due to preserved lactate dehydrogenase activity -> Infarcted myocardium appears pale because enzymes leak out -> Thus infarct appears as unstained pale zone

GROSS MORPHOLOGY

FLOWCHART: <12 hours -> Usually no visible gross changes -> 2–3 hours -> Necrosis detectable using TTC stain -> 12–24 hours -> Reddish-blue discoloration due to congestion and extravasated blood -> 3–7 days -> Hyperemic border due to early granulation tissue -> Later -> Infarct becomes sharply defined, yellow-tan, and soft -> Weeks -> Injured region evolves into fibrous scar

MICROSCOPIC MORPHOLOGY

FLOWCHART: 6–12 hours -> Coagulative necrosis becomes detectable -> Wavy fibers appear at periphery due to stretching by viable myocardium -> Myocyte vacuolization occurs due to accumulation of salt and water in sarcoplasmic reticulum -> 1–3 days -> Acute inflammation with neutrophilic infiltrate -> 3–7 days -> Macrophages remove necrotic cells and neutrophil debris -> 7–10 days -> Granulation tissue forms with new vessels -> Later -> Collagen deposition increases -> No regeneration occurs because cardiac myocytes are terminally differentiated -> Final outcome is fibrous scar

HEALING PROCESS — DIRECTION

FLOWCHART: Healing requires migration of inflammatory cells and ingrowth of new vessels -> Repair begins from infarct margins -> Progresses toward center of infarct -> Large infarcts heal slower than small infarcts -> Healing efficiency depends on host factors -> Malnutrition, poor vasculature, and steroids impair healing -> After complete healing, scar age cannot be determined

CLINICAL FEATURES & COMPLICATIONS

CLINICAL FEATURES — INDIVIDUAL MECHANISMS

1. Chest Pain

FLOWCHART: Myocardial ischemia leads to prolonged lack of oxygen supply to cardiac muscle -> Ischemic myocardium releases metabolites and stimulates sensory nerve endings -> This produces prolonged chest pain lasting more than 30 minutes -> Pain is described as crushing, stabbing, or squeezing

2. Rapid, Weak Pulse

FLOWCHART: Myocardial infarction reduces effective cardiac output due to loss of contractile myocardium -> Reduced stroke volume leads to compensatory sympathetic activation -> Sympathetic activation increases heart rate but weakens pulse volume -> This produces a rapid, weak pulse

3. Diaphoresis (Profuse Sweating)

FLOWCHART: Myocardial ischemia activates the sympathetic nervous system -> Sympathetic stimulation activates sweat glands -> This leads to profuse sweating (diaphoresis)

4. Nausea and Vomiting

FLOWCHART: Infarction involving the posterior-inferior ventricle stimulates the vagus nerve -> Vagal stimulation activates gastrointestinal reflex pathways -> This produces nausea and vomiting

5. Dyspnea

FLOWCHART: Loss of myocardial contractility reduces left ventricular pumping function -> This leads to increased pressure in left atrium and pulmonary veins -> Pulmonary congestion and edema develop -> Pulmonary edema impairs gas exchange -> This results in dyspnea

6. Asymptomatic (Silent MI)

FLOWCHART: In some patients, especially those with diabetic neuropathy, sensory nerve function is impaired -> Pain signals from ischemic myocardium are not perceived -> Myocardial infarction occurs without chest pain -> Disease is detected only by electrocardiographic changes or laboratory evidence of myocardial damage

ECG FEATURES

1. ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)

FLOWCHART: Transmural myocardial infarction involves full thickness of ventricular wall -> This produces characteristic ST-segment elevation on electrocardiogram -> This is termed ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI)

2. Non–ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (NSTEMI)

FLOWCHART: Subendocardial infarction involves only inner layer of myocardium -> This does not produce ST-segment elevation -> This is termed non–ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI)

3. Microinfarctions

FLOWCHART: Small areas of myocardial necrosis occur due to involvement of small vessels -> Electrical changes are minimal or absent -> Electrocardiographic findings are nonspecific or silent

BIOMARKERS

1. Troponin Elevation

FLOWCHART: Irreversible myocardial injury disrupts sarcolemmal membrane -> Intracellular proteins such as cardiac-specific troponins T and I leak into circulation -> Troponins regulate calcium-mediated contraction of cardiac muscle -> Their levels rise in blood within 2 to 4 hours -> They peak at 24 to 48 hours -> Elevation above threshold confirms myocardial injury

2. Troponin Rise with Reperfusion

FLOWCHART: Reperfusion restores blood flow to infarcted myocardium -> This causes rapid washout of intracellular troponins into circulation -> Troponin levels rise earlier and reach higher peaks

3. Troponin Elevation in Other Conditions

FLOWCHART: Conditions such as myocarditis, myocardial trauma, congestive heart failure, pulmonary embolism, renal failure, and sepsis cause myocyte injury -> This leads to low-level troponin elevation -> However, the pattern is gradual and differs from acute MI -> Serial measurements help distinguish causes

COMPLICATIONS

1. Contractile Dysfunction (Left Ventricular Failure)

FLOWCHART: Myocardial infarction causes loss of viable myocardium -> Reduced contractility decreases left ventricular pump function -> This leads to hypotension, pulmonary congestion, and pulmonary edema -> Severe loss of function results in cardiogenic shock

2. Papillary Muscle Dysfunction

FLOWCHART: Ischemia affects papillary muscles -> Papillary muscles become poorly contractile -> Mitral valve closure becomes ineffective -> This leads to postinfarct mitral regurgitation -> Later fibrosis and ventricular dilation worsen regurgitation

3. Right Ventricular Infarction

FLOWCHART: Right coronary artery occlusion affects right ventricle -> Right ventricular contractility decreases -> Blood pools in venous circulation -> Systemic hypotension develops -> Right-sided heart failure occurs

4. Myocardial Rupture

FLOWCHART: Necrotic myocardium becomes soft and weakened during healing phase (3–7 days) -> Mechanical stress causes rupture of ventricular wall, septum, or papillary muscle -> Free wall rupture leads to hemopericardium and cardiac tamponade -> Septal rupture causes ventricular septal defect -> Papillary muscle rupture causes severe mitral regurgitation

5. Arrhythmias

FLOWCHART: Ischemic and necrotic myocardium disrupts electrical conduction pathways -> This creates myocardial irritability -> Abnormal impulses develop -> This leads to arrhythmias such as ventricular fibrillation, tachycardia, or heart block -> Serious arrhythmias are most common in first hour

6. Pericarditis

FLOWCHART: Transmural infarction extends to epicardium -> Inflammation spreads to pericardium -> Fibrinohemorrhagic pericarditis develops -> This causes chest pain and pericardial friction rub -> Later, immune-mediated Dressler syndrome may occur

7. Chamber Dilation

FLOWCHART: Necrotic myocardium loses structural integrity -> Affected region becomes thin and weak -> Stretching and dilation occur -> This leads to chamber dilation

8. Mural Thrombus

FLOWCHART: Reduced contractility causes blood stasis -> Endocardial damage creates thrombogenic surface -> Chamber dilation worsens stasis -> Mural thrombus forms -> Thrombi may embolize causing systemic complications

9. Ventricular Aneurysm

FLOWCHART: Large transmural infarct heals with thin fibrous scar -> Scarred wall bulges outward during contraction -> Ventricular aneurysm forms -> This leads to arrhythmias, thrombus formation, and heart failure -> Aneurysm does not rupture

10. Progressive Heart Failure

FLOWCHART: Loss of myocardium and ventricular remodeling reduce cardiac efficiency -> Compensatory mechanisms fail over time -> Chronic heart failure develops
