OBSTRUCTIVE LUNG DISEASES — FULL ORGANIZATION (100% PRESERVED TEXT)

OVERVIEW

Common obstructive lung diseases include chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, and bronchiectasis (Table 15.3).
  • COPD has two major clinicopathologic manifestations, emphysema and chronic bronchitis, which are often found together in the same patient, almost certainly because they share the same major etiologic factor—cigarette smoking.
  • While asthma is distinguished from chronic bronchitis and emphysema by the presence of reversible bronchospasm, some patients with otherwise typical asthma also develop an irreversible component.
  • Conversely, some patients with otherwise typical COPD have a reversible component, and such patients are labeled as having COPD/asthma.

CHRONIC OBSTRUCTIVE PULMONARY DISEASE (COPD)

COPD, a major public health problem, is defined by the World Health Organization (WHO) as
👉 “a common, preventable and treatable disease that is characterized by persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow limitation that is due to airway and/or alveolar abnormalities caused by exposure to noxious particles or gases.”

EPIDEMIOLOGY

  • It is currently the fourth leading cause of death in the world
  • Projected to rank third by 2020 due to increases in cigarette smoking
  • There is a strong association between heavy cigarette smoking and COPD
  • Overall, 35% to 50% of heavy smokers develop COPD
  • Conversely about 80% of COPD is attributable to smoking
  • Women and African Americans who smoke heavily are more susceptible

RISK FACTORS

  • Poor lung development early in life
  • Exposure to environmental pollutants
  • Occupational pollutants
  • Airway hyperresponsiveness
  • Certain genetic polymorphisms

OVERLAP OF DISEASES

FLOWCHART: Chronic injury (e.g., smoking) -> EMPHYSEMA → Alveolar wall destruction → Overinflation -> CHRONIC BRONCHITIS → Productive cough → Airway inflammation -> ASTHMA → Reversible obstruction → Bronchial hyperresponsiveness triggered by allergens, infection, etc.

EMPHYSEMA

DEFINITION

Emphysema is defined by irreversible enlargement of the airspaces distal to the terminal bronchiole, accompanied by destruction of their walls.
  • Subtle but functionally important small airway fibrosis (distinct from chronic bronchitis) is also present and is a significant contributor to airflow obstruction.

CLASSIFICATION

FLOWCHART: Emphysema is classified according to its anatomic distribution within the lobule -> Based on the segments of the respiratory units that are involved -> Emphysema is subdivided into four major types -> (1) centriacinar -> (2) panacinar -> (3) paraseptal -> (4) irregular

TYPES OF EMPHYSEMA

Centriacinar (centrilobular) emphysema

  • The most common form, constituting more than 95% of clinically significant cases
  • Occurs predominantly in heavy smokers with COPD
  • The central or proximal parts of the acini, formed by respiratory bronchioles, are affected
  • Distal alveoli are spared
  • Lesions are more common and more pronounced in the upper lobes, particularly in the apical segments
  • In severe cases, distal acinus may also be involved

Panacinar (panlobular) emphysema

  • Associated with α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • Exacerbated by smoking
  • Acini are uniformly enlarged from respiratory bronchiole to terminal alveoli
  • Occurs more commonly in lower zones and anterior margins
  • Usually most severe at the bases

Distal acinar (paraseptal) emphysema

  • Underlies many cases of spontaneous pneumothorax in young adults
  • Proximal acinus normal, distal part involved
  • More striking adjacent to pleura and septa
  • Occurs adjacent to fibrosis, scarring, or atelectasis
  • More severe in upper half of lungs
  • Characteristic enlarged airspaces (0.5–2.0 cm) forming cyst-like structures

Irregular emphysema

  • Airspace enlargement with fibrosis
  • Acinus irregularly involved
  • Associated with scarring
  • Usually clinically insignificant

PATHOGENESIS

FLOWCHART: Smoking or air pollutant + genetic predisposition -> Inflammatory cells → release of inflammatory mediators -> Protease-antiprotease imbalance -> Oxidative stress → increased apoptosis and senescence -> Alveolar wall destruction

MECHANISMS

  • Toxic injury and inflammation
  • Inhaled cigarette smoke and other noxious particles damage respiratory epithelium
  • Cause inflammation and parenchymal destruction
  • Mediators: leukotriene B4, IL-8, TNF
  • Effects: Attract inflammatory cells, amplify inflammation, induce structural changes
  • Chronic inflammation → accumulation of T and B cells

Protease-antiprotease imbalance

  • Proteases break down connective tissue
  • Relative deficiency of antiproteases
  • Sometimes genetic

Oxidative stress

  • Tobacco smoke, inflammatory cells produce oxidants
  • Causes tissue damage, endothelial dysfunction, inflammation
  • NRF2 gene protects against oxidants

Infection

  • Does not initiate disease
  • Exacerbates existing disease

α1-ANTITRYPSIN DEFICIENCY

  • Major inhibitor of proteases (especially elastase)
  • Encoded on chromosome 14
  • ZZ genotype → very low levels
  • > 80% develop panacinar emphysema
  • Earlier and more severe with smoking
FLOWCHART: Increased neutrophil activation and influx -> Protease release increases -> Lack of α1-antitrypsin activity -> Excessive elastic tissue digestion -> Emphysema develops

AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Loss of elastic tissue in alveolar walls -> Reduced radial traction on bronchioles -> Collapse during expiration -> Functional airflow obstruction -> Small airway inflammation contributes further

MORPHOLOGY

GROSS

  • Voluminous lungs
  • Overlap heart anteriorly
  • Upper two-thirds more affected
  • Large alveoli visible
  • Apical blebs or bullae present

MICROSCOPY

  • Enlarged alveoli with thin septa
  • Focal centriacinar fibrosis
  • Loss of alveolar attachments
  • Enlarged pores of Kohn
  • Septa appear floating or club-shaped
  • Reduced capillary bed area
  • Advanced disease: Larger airspaces, blebs or bullae, compression of bronchioles

ASSOCIATED CHANGES

  • Small airway inflammation
  • Vascular changes due to pulmonary hypertension
  • Caused by hypoxemia and loss of capillary beds
