ANTIEPILEPTIC DRUGS

Epilepsy is derived from a Greek word meaning “convulsions”. It is a disorder of brain function characterized by paroxysmal cerebral dysrhythmia.
FLOWCHART: Abnormal electrical discharge develops in the brain -> The discharge spreads through a group of neurons -> Normal brain function becomes disturbed temporarily -> A seizure occurs

MAJOR TYPES OF EPILEPSY

Epilepsy │ ├── Generalized seizures │ │ │ ├── Generalized tonic-clonic seizures │ ├── Absence seizures │ └── Myoclonic seizures │ └── Partial seizures │ ├── Simple partial seizures └── Complex partial seizures
FLOWCHART: Epilepsy -> [Generalized seizures | Partial seizures] -> [Generalized tonic-clonic seizures | Absence seizures | Myoclonic seizures | Simple partial seizures | Complex partial seizures]

GENERALIZED SEIZURES

1. Generalized Tonic-Clonic Seizures (GTCS, Grand Mal Epilepsy)

Generalized tonic-clonic seizure is characterized by a typical sequence.

FLOWCHART: Aura appears -> Loss of consciousness occurs -> The patient falls to the ground -> Tonic phase develops with generalized rigidity -> Clonic phase develops with repeated jerking movements -> A period of relaxation follows -> Postictal CNS depression and drowsiness occur
Mnemonic
"A Fall To Clumsy Relaxed Sleep"
  • A = Aura
  • F = Fall
  • T = Tonic phase
  • C = Clonic phase
  • R = Relaxation
  • S = Sleep or CNS depression

2. Absence Seizures (Petit Mal Epilepsy)

FLOWCHART: Sudden onset of blank staring occurs -> The patient stops all activity -> There is momentary unresponsiveness -> Brief loss of consciousness occurs -> The patient rapidly returns to normal

Absence seizures do not produce a fall or postictal confusion.

Mnemonic
"ABSENCE = A Brief Staring Episode, No Collapse"

3. Myoclonic Seizures

FLOWCHART: Sudden discharge occurs in the motor system -> Single or multiple brief shock-like contractions develop -> The patient experiences sudden jerking movements

PARTIAL SEIZURES

1. Simple Partial Seizures (SPS)

In simple partial seizures, the manifestations depend on the part of the cerebral cortex involved.

FLOWCHART: A small localized area of the cortex becomes overactive -> Focal symptoms develop -> [Motor symptoms may appear as localized convulsions | Sensory symptoms may appear as paraesthesia] -> Consciousness remains intact

2. Complex Partial Seizures (CPS, Temporal Lobe Epilepsy, Psychomotor Epilepsy)

FLOWCHART: Aura appears -> Consciousness becomes impaired -> The patient develops amnesia -> Abnormal behaviour and automatisms occur

Examples of automatisms include lip smacking, chewing movements and purposeless hand movements.

Mnemonic
"Complex Partial = A3"
  • A = Aura
  • A = Amnesia
  • A = Automatism

Mnemonic
"E for Empty staring"

Ethosuximide is the drug of choice for absence seizures because the patient appears empty and staring.

MECHANISM OF ACTION OF ANTIEPILEPTIC DRUGS

Most antiepileptic drugs act either by blocking sodium channels or by increasing GABA-mediated inhibition.

FLOWCHART: Antiepileptic Drugs -> [Sodium Channel Blocking Drugs | GABA Facilitating Drugs]

Sodium Channel Blocking Drugs

Examples are phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine and lacosamide.

FLOWCHART: The antiepileptic drug binds to voltage-gated sodium channels -> The inactivated state of the channel is prolonged -> Further entry of sodium into the neuron is prevented -> Generation of repetitive action potentials decreases -> High-frequency firing of neurons is suppressed -> Spread of seizure discharge is prevented

GABA Facilitating Drugs

Examples are benzodiazepines, phenobarbitone, sodium valproate, vigabatrin, gabapentin and tiagabine.

FLOWCHART: The antiepileptic drug increases the activity of GABA -> Chloride entry into the neuron increases -> The neuron becomes hyperpolarized -> Neuronal excitability decreases -> Seizure activity is suppressed TABLE: | Drug | Main GABA-Related Action | | Benzodiazepines | Facilitate GABA activity | | Phenobarbitone | Facilitates GABA activity and has GABA-mimetic action | | Sodium valproate | Increases GABA by increasing GAD and inhibiting GABA-T | | Vigabatrin | Inhibits GABA transaminase | | Tiagabine | Blocks uptake of GABA into neurons | | Gabapentin | Promotes GABA release |
Mnemonic
"Benzos Bar the Brain, Valproate Values GABA"

PHENYTOIN (DIPHENYLHYDANTOIN)

Phenytoin is one of the most commonly used antiepileptic drugs. It has a selective antiepileptic effect and does not produce significant drowsiness.

MECHANISM OF ACTION OF PHENYTOIN

FLOWCHART: Voltage-gated sodium channels normally open repeatedly during seizure activity -> Phenytoin binds to sodium channels in the inactivated state -> Recovery of the channel back to the resting state becomes delayed -> Further entry of sodium into the neuron decreases -> The neuronal membrane becomes stabilized -> Repetitive high-frequency firing is inhibited -> Spread of seizure discharge is prevented

At higher concentrations, phenytoin also produces additional effects.

FLOWCHART: High concentration of phenytoin is reached -> [Calcium entry into neurons decreases | Glutamate release decreases | Response to GABA increases] -> Antiepileptic action becomes stronger
Mnemonic
"Phenytoin Puts Sodium channels to Permanent Pause"

PHARMACOKINETICS OF PHENYTOIN

TABLE: | Property | Details | | Absorption | Slowly absorbed through GIT | | Distribution | Widely distributed | | Protein binding | About 90% bound to plasma proteins | | Metabolism | Almost completely metabolized in liver by hydroxylation and glucuronide conjugation | | Enzyme induction | Repeated administration induces hepatic enzymes |

Phenytoin shows dose-dependent elimination.

FLOWCHART: Low dose of phenytoin is administered -> Metabolizing enzymes are not saturated -> Elimination follows first-order kinetics -> Half-life remains about 10–24 hours FLOWCHART: The dose of phenytoin is increased -> Metabolizing enzymes become saturated -> Elimination changes to zero-order kinetics -> Half-life increases up to about 60 hours -> Even a small increase in dose causes a marked rise in plasma concentration -> Toxicity may develop

Therefore, therapeutic drug monitoring is essential.

Mnemonic
"Phenytoin is Fine till the enzymes Fill"

Once the enzymes become saturated, toxicity appears suddenly.

USES OF PHENYTOIN

TABLE: | Use | Explanation | | Generalized tonic-clonic seizures | One of the most important indications | | Partial seizures | Effective in both simple and complex partial seizures | | Trigeminal neuralgia and other neuralgias | Useful because it decreases nerve excitability | | Status epilepticus | Given intravenously |

When phenytoin is used intravenously in status epilepticus, it must be administered only in normal saline.

FLOWCHART: Intravenous phenytoin is mixed with glucose solution -> Phenytoin precipitates -> The preparation becomes unsuitable FLOWCHART: Phenytoin for status epilepticus -> Use only normal saline
Mnemonic
"Phenytoin and Saline are Fine"

ADVERSE EFFECTS OF PHENYTOIN

The important adverse effects of phenytoin can be remembered by the “H” mnemonic.

Mnemonic
"The Seven Hs of Phenytoin"
  • H = Hypertrophy and hyperplasia of gums
  • H = Hypersensitivity reactions
  • H = Hirsutism
  • H = Hyperglycaemia
  • H = Hypocalcaemia
  • H = Hypovitaminosis folate causing megaloblastic anaemia
  • H = Hydantoin syndrome in fetus
TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Cause | | Hypertrophy and hyperplasia of gums | Defect in collagen catabolism | | Hypersensitivity reactions | Skin rash, neutropenia and rarely hepatic necrosis | | Hirsutism | Increased androgen secretion | | Hyperglycaemia | Decreased insulin release | | Megaloblastic anaemia | Folate deficiency | | Osteomalacia | Increased metabolism of vitamin D | | Hypocalcaemia | Decreased calcium absorption from gut | | Fetal hydantoin syndrome | Cleft lip, cleft palate and digital hypoplasia |

Gingival hyperplasia develops step by step.

FLOWCHART: Chronic phenytoin therapy is continued -> Collagen breakdown in gums decreases -> Fibrous tissue accumulates in the gums -> Gingival hyperplasia develops

Proper oral hygiene can reduce this complication.

TOXICITY AT HIGH CONCENTRATION

CNS Toxicity

FLOWCHART: The plasma concentration of phenytoin becomes high -> Vestibulocerebellar function becomes impaired -> [Nystagmus | Vertigo | Ataxia] develop -> Tremor, headache and psychological disturbances may also occur TABLE: | CNS Toxicity | Manifestation | | Vestibular toxicity | Vertigo | | Cerebellar toxicity | Ataxia, nystagmus | | Other CNS effects | Tremor, headache, psychological disturbances |
Mnemonic
"Phenytoin Toxicity = NAVTH"
  • N = Nystagmus
  • A = Ataxia
  • V = Vertigo
  • T = Tremor
  • H = Headache

Cardiovascular Toxicity

FLOWCHART: Intravenous phenytoin is administered rapidly -> Cardiac conduction becomes depressed -> Hypotension and arrhythmias may occur

Extravasation causes local tissue injury.

FLOWCHART: Phenytoin leaks outside the vein during intravenous administration -> The surrounding tissue becomes damaged -> Local necrosis may occur

Gastrointestinal Toxicity

FLOWCHART: Phenytoin irritates the gastrointestinal tract -> Nausea, vomiting and dyspepsia occur

Giving phenytoin after food decreases these symptoms.

IMPORTANT PRACTICAL POINTS

TABLE: | Important Point | Explanation | | Do not stop phenytoin suddenly | Abrupt withdrawal may precipitate seizures | | Monitor blood level regularly | Because of dose-dependent kinetics | | Give after food | Reduces gastric irritation | | Use normal saline for IV administration | Phenytoin precipitates in glucose | | Maintain oral hygiene | Prevents gingival hyperplasia |
