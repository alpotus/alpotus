PLASMA VOLUME EXPANDERS

  • Plasma volume expanders are therapeutic agents used to restore and maintain circulating blood volume in patients experiencing hypovolemia, such as those in shock or with significant blood loss.
  • They are primarily divided into two categories: crystalloids and colloids.

1. CRYSTALLOIDS

  • Crystalloids are typically isotonic salt solutions.
  • While effective for immediate volume replacement, they are rapidly lost from the circulation as they leak into the interstitial space.

Examples

  • Normal saline (0.9% NaCl)
  • Ringer’s lactate

Dosing

  • Because they do not stay in the vascular compartment long, large volumes are often required.
  • For example, replacing 1 liter of plasma volume may require up to 6 liters of crystalloids.

2. COLLOIDS (THE PRIMARY "EXPANDERS")

  • Colloids contain large molecules that cannot easily cross the capillary membrane.
  • They remain in the vascular tree longer than crystalloids.
  • They draw fluid from the interstitial space into the bloodstream via osmotic pressure.

Human Albumin Solutions (5%, 20%, 25%)

  • Albumin is a natural plasma protein that accounts for half of the total plasma protein.
  • 5% Albumin is used for volume replacement.
  • Concentrated Albumin (20% or 25%) is used to rapidly expand volume.
  • For instance, 50g of 25% albumin can increase blood volume by approximately 500 mL by pulling fluid into the vessels.

Polygelatin Solutions (e.g., Hemaccel, Gelofusine)

  • These are iso-osmotic with plasma and do not interfere with the body's coagulation system.
  • They are often preferred in obstetric emergencies like placental abruption because they promote osmotic diuresis.
  • They are safer for blood cross-matching than other options.

Dextrans

  • These are polymolecular polysaccharides.
  • While effective at expanding volume, they are generally avoided in modern practice.
  • They interfere with blood cross-matching.
  • They can adversely affect platelet function.
  • Specific formulations like Hyskon (32% Dextran 70) have been associated with severe anaphylactic reactions and are no longer recommended.

3. CLINICAL INDICATIONS

  • Hemorrhagic Shock: Used to stabilize a patient immediately until whole blood or packed red cells become available.
  • Septic Shock: Helps maintain mean arterial pressure (MAP) and tissue perfusion during severe infection.
  • Obstetric Complications: Used in cases of placental abruption, severe preeclampsia, or eclampsia to manage hypovolemia, though they must be used cautiously to avoid overloading the heart.
  • Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS): Colloids or albumin are used to manage the fluid shift from the vascular to the extracellular compartment.

4. PRECAUTIONS AND MONITORING

  • Circulatory Overload: Rapid infusion can lead to pulmonary edema and Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS), especially in patients with brittle cardiac function or preeclampsia.
  • Monitoring: Best practice involves using Central Venous Pressure (CVP) monitoring to ensure the circulating volume is adequate without causing overload.
  • Anaphylaxis: Certain expanders, particularly dextrans and some gelatins, can trigger severe allergic reactions.
  • Electrolyte Imbalance: Excessive use can lead to distortions in sodium and potassium levels.
