ANTIANGINAL DRUGS

❤️
Angina pectoris is a symptom of ischaemic heart disease. It occurs when the oxygen requirement of the myocardium becomes greater than the oxygen supplied to it.

TYPES OF ANGINA PECTORIS

TABLE: | Type of Angina | Explanation | | Stable (Classical) Angina | Characterized by episodes of chest pain usually precipitated by exertion or emotional stress | | Unstable Angina | Characterized by increasing severity, frequency and duration of anginal attacks due to rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque and progressive coronary thrombosis | | Prinzmetal (Variant) Angina | Occurs at rest and is caused by coronary artery spasm | FLOWCHART: Angina Pectoris -> [Stable (Classical) Angina | Unstable Angina | Prinzmetal (Variant) Angina] -> [Chest pain precipitated by exertion or emotional stress | Rupture of atherosclerotic plaque + progressive coronary thrombosis | Coronary artery spasm at rest] FLOWCHART: Stable angina develops during exertion -> The myocardium requires more oxygen -> Coronary blood flow cannot increase sufficiently -> Chest pain develops FLOWCHART: An atherosclerotic plaque ruptures in a coronary artery -> Platelets adhere to the damaged area -> Coronary thrombosis gradually develops -> Coronary blood flow decreases further -> Unstable angina occurs FLOWCHART: Coronary artery spasm occurs suddenly -> Coronary blood flow falls despite rest -> Myocardial ischaemia develops -> Prinzmetal angina occurs

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY OF ANGINA

TABLE: | Factors Decreasing Oxygen Supply | Factors Increasing Oxygen Demand | | Coronary atherosclerosis | Increased heart rate | | Coronary vasospasm | Increased ventricular contractility | | Coronary thrombosis | Ventricular hypertrophy | | Reduced coronary blood flow | Increased ventricular wall tension | FLOWCHART: Myocardial Oxygen Imbalance -> [Coronary atherosclerosis | Coronary vasospasm | Coronary thrombosis | Reduced coronary blood flow] + [Increased heart rate | Increased ventricular contractility | Ventricular hypertrophy | Increased ventricular wall tension] -> Myocardial oxygen demand exceeds oxygen supply -> Anginal pain develops FLOWCHART: Coronary blood flow becomes inadequate -> The myocardium receives less oxygen -> At the same time the heart continues to work harder -> Myocardial oxygen demand exceeds oxygen supply -> Anginal pain develops

PRINCIPLES OF TREATMENT OF ANGINA

TABLE: | Method of Increasing Oxygen Supply | Method of Decreasing Oxygen Demand | | Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stenting | Reduction of preload by nitrates | | Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) | Reduction of afterload by calcium channel blockers and potassium channel openers | | Relief of coronary vasospasm by nitrates and calcium channel blockers | Reduction of heart rate and contractility by beta blockers | | Thrombolytic therapy to dissolve thrombus | Reduction of cardiac work | | Antiplatelet drugs to prevent thrombosis | Reduction of myocardial oxygen consumption | FLOWCHART: Treatment of Angina -> [Increase oxygen supply | Decrease oxygen demand] -> [PCI / CABG / Relief of vasospasm / Thrombolysis / Antiplatelet drugs | Nitrates / Beta blockers / Calcium channel blockers / Potassium channel openers] -> The imbalance between oxygen supply and oxygen demand becomes less -> Myocardial ischaemia decreases -> Anginal pain is relieved

CLASSIFICATION OF ANTIANGINAL DRUGS

ORGANIC NITRATES

💊
Organic nitrates are prodrugs that release nitric oxide. Nitric oxide relaxes vascular smooth muscle. Nitrates predominantly dilate veins, but they also dilate arteries and coronary vessels.
TABLE: | Important Nitrates | Common Use | | Nitroglycerin (Glyceryl trinitrate) | Acute anginal attack | | Isosorbide dinitrate | Acute attack and chronic prophylaxis | | Isosorbide mononitrate | Long-term prophylaxis |

MECHANISM OF ACTION OF NITRATES

FLOWCHART: Nitrate enters the vascular smooth muscle cell -> The nitrate is denitrated inside the cell -> Nitric oxide is released -> Nitric oxide stimulates guanylyl cyclase -> Guanylyl cyclase converts GTP into cyclic GMP -> The concentration of cyclic GMP increases -> Protein kinase G becomes activated -> Myosin light chain phosphatase is activated and intracellular calcium decreases -> Vascular smooth muscle relaxes -> Venodilatation and arteriolar dilatation occur FLOWCHART: Nitrate Administration -> [Predominant venodilatation | Arteriolar dilatation] -> [Blood pools in peripheral veins -> Venous return decreases -> Preload decreases | Peripheral vascular resistance decreases -> Afterload decreases] -> The work done by the heart decreases -> Myocardial oxygen requirement decreases -> Anginal pain is relieved

PHARMACOLOGICAL ACTIONS OF NITRATES

TABLE: | Action of Nitrates | Result | | Venodilatation | Decreases preload | | Arteriolar dilatation | Decreases afterload | | Dilatation of large coronary arteries and collateral vessels | Improves blood flow to ischaemic myocardium | | Redistribution of coronary blood flow | Increases oxygen delivery to the ischaemic area | FLOWCHART: Nitrate causes venodilatation -> Peripheral pooling of blood occurs -> Venous return to the heart decreases -> Left ventricular end-diastolic volume decreases -> Cardiac work falls -> Myocardial oxygen demand decreases FLOWCHART: Nitrate causes arteriolar dilatation -> Peripheral vascular resistance decreases -> Afterload decreases -> The left ventricle ejects blood more easily -> Myocardial oxygen demand decreases further FLOWCHART: Large coronary vessels and collateral vessels dilate -> Coronary blood flow is redistributed toward the ischaemic region -> Oxygen delivery to the ischaemic myocardium increases -> Anginal pain is relieved TABLE: | Smooth Muscle Relaxed by Nitrates | | Bronchial smooth muscle | | Oesophageal smooth muscle | | Biliary tract smooth muscle |

PHARMACOKINETICS OF NITRATES

Organic nitrates are rapidly absorbed through the buccal mucosa, gastrointestinal tract and skin.

TABLE: | Route | Characteristic | | Sublingual route | Very rapid onset within 2–5 minutes but short duration | | Oral route | Useful for prophylaxis but undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism | | Transdermal route | Slow absorption with prolonged action | TABLE: | Drug | First-Pass Metabolism | | Nitroglycerin | Extensive | | Isosorbide dinitrate | Extensive | | Isosorbide mononitrate | Minimal or absent | FLOWCHART: Nitroglycerin is swallowed -> The drug enters the liver before reaching the circulation -> Extensive first-pass metabolism occurs -> Very little active drug reaches the blood FLOWCHART: Nitroglycerin tablet is placed under the tongue -> The drug is absorbed rapidly through the buccal mucosa -> The liver is bypassed -> Rapid relief of anginal pain occurs within 2 to 3 minutes

ADVERSE EFFECTS OF NITRATES

TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Cause | | Headache | Dilatation of cranial blood vessels | | Postural hypotension | Excessive venodilatation and fall in blood pressure | | Palpitation | Reflex tachycardia secondary to vasodilatation | | Dizziness | Reduced cerebral perfusion due to hypotension | | Methaemoglobinaemia | Occurs in severe overdose | FLOWCHART: Nitrate produces marked vasodilatation -> Blood pressure falls excessively -> Baroreceptor reflex becomes activated -> Heart rate rises -> Palpitation occurs
⚠️
Nitroglycerin should be avoided if there is hypotension or if sildenafil or tadalafil has been taken within the previous 24 hours.

TOLERANCE TO NITRATES

FLOWCHART: Nitrate is given continuously by oral route, transdermal patch or intravenous infusion -> Nitric oxide generation gradually decreases -> The vascular response to nitrates becomes weaker -> The antianginal effect decreases -> Tolerance develops

Tolerance may be due to depletion of sulfhydryl groups, reduced nitric oxide formation or increased free radical generation.

TABLE: | Method of Preventing Tolerance | Explanation | | Nitrate-free interval of 8–10 hours daily | Restores sensitivity to nitrates | | Removal of transdermal patch for a few hours daily | Prevents continuous exposure | FLOWCHART: The transdermal nitrate patch is removed for at least 8 hours each day -> Continuous exposure to nitrate stops -> Tolerance does not develop

THERAPEUTIC USES OF NITRATES

Acute Attack of Angina

Nitroglycerin is the drug of choice for an acute attack.

TABLE: | Drug | Dose and Important Instructions | | Nitroglycerin | 0.5 mg sublingually | FLOWCHART: Acute anginal pain develops -> A nitroglycerin tablet of 0.5 mg is placed under the tongue -> Pain is relieved within 2 to 3 minutes -> The tablet should be spit out once pain disappears to reduce adverse effects FLOWCHART: Pain persists after the first tablet -> A second tablet may be taken after 5 minutes -> If necessary, a third tablet may be taken after another 5 minutes -> No more than three tablets should be used within 15 minutes

Nitroglycerin buccal spray may also be used.

Isosorbide dinitrate can also be used sublingually, but its action is slower.

Prophylaxis of Stable Angina

TABLE: | Drug Used for Long-Term Prophylaxis | Route | | Isosorbide mononitrate | Oral | | Isosorbide dinitrate | Oral | | Nitroglycerin sustained-release preparation | Oral | | Nitroglycerin patch, ointment or disc | Transdermal | FLOWCHART: Long-acting nitrate is taken regularly -> The frequency of anginal attacks decreases -> Exercise tolerance improves

Sublingual nitroglycerin may also be taken immediately before exercise or emotional stress.

Variant (Prinzmetal) Angina

TABLE: | Drug Useful in Variant Angina | Use | | Nitroglycerin | Relief of acute coronary spasm | | Nitrates + Calcium channel blockers | Prophylaxis | | Amlodipine, Nifedipine SR, Diltiazem | Commonly used calcium channel blockers | FLOWCHART: Coronary vasospasm develops -> Nitroglycerin or a calcium channel blocker relaxes the coronary artery -> Coronary blood flow improves -> Anginal pain disappears

Unstable Angina

TABLE: | Drug Group | Drugs Used | | Antiplatelet drugs | Aspirin, Clopidogrel, Prasugrel | | Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa antagonists | Tirofiban, Eptifibatide, Abciximab | | Anticoagulants | Low molecular weight heparin, Unfractionated heparin, Fondaparinux | | Nitrates | Sublingual or intravenous nitroglycerin | | Beta blockers | Atenolol, Metoprolol | | Calcium channel blockers | Amlodipine, Nifedipine SR, Diltiazem, Verapamil | | Statins | Used to improve outcome | FLOWCHART: Atherosclerotic plaque ruptures in a coronary artery -> Platelet aggregation and thrombosis occur -> Coronary blood flow decreases rapidly -> Multiple drugs are used to reduce thrombosis, relieve vasospasm and reduce oxygen demand

Myocardial Infarction

FLOWCHART: Myocardial infarction is accompanied by persistent or recurrent ischaemic pain -> Intravenous nitroglycerin is administered -> Preload and myocardial oxygen demand decrease -> Pain and left ventricular failure improve

Congestive Cardiac Failure

FLOWCHART: Acute heart failure causes increased venous return and pulmonary congestion -> Intravenous nitroglycerin dilates veins -> Preload falls -> Pulmonary congestion decreases

Hypertensive Emergency

FLOWCHART: Intravenous nitroglycerin is administered in hypertensive emergency -> Rapid venodilatation occurs -> Blood pressure decreases quickly

Biliary Colic

FLOWCHART: Spasm of the biliary tract causes pain -> Sublingual nitroglycerin relaxes the biliary smooth muscle -> Pain decreases

CYANIDE POISONING

☠️
Cyanide poisoning produces histotoxic anoxia because oxygen cannot be utilized by tissues.
FLOWCHART: Cyanide binds to cytochrome oxidase inside cells -> Oxidative phosphorylation stops -> Cells cannot utilize oxygen -> All tissues become hypoxic despite normal oxygen content in blood FLOWCHART: Treatment of Cyanide Poisoning -> [Amyl nitrite or sodium nitrite administered | Intravenous sodium thiosulphate given | Hydroxocobalamin administered] -> [Haemoglobin converted into methaemoglobin | Cyanomethaemoglobin converted into sodium thiocyanate | Cyanocobalamin formed] -> [Methaemoglobin binds cyanide | Sodium thiocyanate excreted in urine | Cyanocobalamin safely eliminated] FLOWCHART: Amyl nitrite or sodium nitrite is administered -> Haemoglobin is converted into methaemoglobin -> Methaemoglobin binds cyanide -> Cyanomethaemoglobin is formed -> Intravenous sodium thiosulphate is given -> Cyanomethaemoglobin is converted into sodium thiocyanate -> Sodium thiocyanate is rapidly excreted in urine TABLE: | Step in Treatment of Cyanide Poisoning | Drug Used | Result | | Step 1 | Amyl nitrite or sodium nitrite | Converts haemoglobin into methaemoglobin | | Step 2 | Methaemoglobin | Binds cyanide and forms cyanomethaemoglobin | | Step 3 | Sodium thiosulphate | Converts cyanide into sodium thiocyanate for urinary excretion | FLOWCHART: Hydroxocobalamin is administered -> Hydroxocobalamin binds cyanide -> Cyanocobalamin is formed -> The cyanocobalamin is safely eliminated from the body
