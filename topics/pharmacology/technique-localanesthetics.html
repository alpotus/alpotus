TECHNIQUES OF LOCAL ANAESTHESIA

Different techniques of local anaesthesia are used depending upon the area to be anaesthetized and the type of procedure.

TABLE: | Technique | Main Site of Action | Common Drugs | | Surface (Topical) Anaesthesia | Skin and mucous membrane | Lignocaine, Benzocaine, Tetracaine | | Infiltration Anaesthesia | Local tissue and sensory nerve endings | Lignocaine, Procaine, Bupivacaine | | Field Block and Nerve Block | Peripheral nerves and nerve plexuses | Most local anaesthetics | | Spinal Anaesthesia | Spinal nerve roots in subarachnoid space | Lignocaine, Tetracaine, Bupivacaine | | Epidural Anaesthesia | Spinal nerve roots in epidural space | Lignocaine, Bupivacaine, Ropivacaine | | Intravenous Regional Anaesthesia (Bier's Block) | Veins of isolated limb | Lignocaine, Prilocaine |
Mnemonic
"Some Intelligent Nerves Speak Easily Inside"
  • S = Surface anaesthesia
  • I = Infiltration anaesthesia
  • N = Nerve block
  • S = Spinal anaesthesia
  • E = Epidural anaesthesia
  • I = Intravenous regional anaesthesia

SURFACE (TOPICAL) ANAESTHESIA

In surface anaesthesia, the local anaesthetic is applied directly to the skin or mucous membrane.

FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic is applied to the surface of skin or mucous membrane -> The drug penetrates only superficial sensory nerve endings -> Pain sensation is lost locally -> Motor function remains intact TABLE: | Site Where Topical Anaesthesia is Used | Examples | | Eye | Tonometry, foreign body removal | | Nose and throat | Endoscopy, nasal procedures | | Mouth | Dental procedures | | Upper respiratory tract | Bronchoscopy | | Oesophagus and urethra | Endoscopy and catheterization | | Ulcers, burns and fissures | Relief of local pain | TABLE: | Drug Used for Topical Anaesthesia | Concentration | | Lignocaine | 2%–10% | | Benzocaine | 1%–2% | | Tetracaine | 2% | | Oxethazaine | Used for gastric mucosa |

Topical anaesthesia is also useful during diagnostic procedures.

FLOWCHART: Topical anaesthetic is applied to the eye -> Corneal sensation disappears -> Tonometry or removal of a foreign body can be performed painlessly

EMLA can be used to anaesthetize intact skin.

FLOWCHART: EMLA cream is applied on intact skin one hour before the procedure -> The mixture penetrates the superficial tissues slowly -> The skin becomes numb -> Venepuncture or skin grafting can be performed painlessly

Addition of adrenaline does not significantly prolong surface anaesthesia because penetration of the drug is poor.

Mnemonic
"Topical = Touch only"

Surface anaesthesia blocks only superficial sensation

Motor power remains normal

INFILTRATION ANAESTHESIA

In infiltration anaesthesia, the local anaesthetic is injected directly into the tissues that are to be operated upon.

FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic is injected into the tissues around the area of operation -> The drug blocks sensory nerve endings in that area -> The area becomes numb -> The procedure can be performed without pain TABLE: | Drug Used | Concentration | | Lignocaine | 0.5%–1% | | Procaine | 0.5%–1% | | Bupivacaine | 0.125%–0.25% |

Adrenaline is often added to prolong the duration of action.

FLOWCHART: Adrenaline is added to the local anaesthetic -> Local blood flow decreases -> Absorption of the drug becomes slower -> The duration of anaesthesia becomes longer TABLE: | Procedure Commonly Done Under Infiltration Anaesthesia | | Drainage of abscess | | Excision of small swelling or lipoma | | Suturing of wounds | | Episiotomy |

Infiltration anaesthesia is suitable only for small areas.

FLOWCHART: A large area has to be anaesthetized -> Large amounts of local anaesthetic would be required -> The risk of toxicity increases -> Infiltration anaesthesia becomes unsuitable TABLE: | Contraindication | Reason | | Local infection | Acidic pH reduces the effectiveness of the local anaesthetic | | Clotting disorder | Risk of bleeding into tissues |
Mnemonic
"INFILTRATION = Inject In the Field"

CONDUCTION BLOCK

Conduction block prevents transmission of impulses through larger nerves.

There are two main types.

TABLE: | Type | Site of Injection | | Field block | Around the area to be anaesthetized | | Nerve block | Around a peripheral nerve or nerve plexus | FLOWCHART: Conduction Block -> [Field block | Nerve block] -> [Around the area to be anaesthetized | Around a peripheral nerve or nerve plexus]

FIELD BLOCK ANAESTHESIA

FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic is injected subcutaneously around the area of surgery -> The sensory nerves entering that region are blocked -> The area distal to the injection becomes anaesthetized

Field block is useful when a larger area must be anaesthetized with a smaller quantity of drug.

TABLE: | Common Uses of Field Block | | Minor procedures on scalp | | Procedures on anterior abdominal wall | | Minor surgery on upper limb | | Minor surgery on lower limb |
Mnemonic
"Field block Fences the area"

NERVE BLOCK ANAESTHESIA

In nerve block anaesthesia, the local anaesthetic is injected close to a major nerve or nerve plexus.

FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic is injected near a nerve or nerve plexus -> Impulse conduction in the entire nerve is blocked -> A large area supplied by that nerve becomes anaesthetized TABLE: | Nerve Block | Common Use | | Brachial plexus block | Surgery on upper limb | | Cervical plexus block | Surgery of the neck | | Intercostal nerve block | Surgery of anterior abdominal wall | | Sciatic and femoral nerve block | Surgery below the knee |

Nerve block requires a smaller quantity of local anaesthetic than infiltration anaesthesia.

Mnemonic
"BICS for Nerve Blocks"
  • B = Brachial plexus block
  • I = Intercostal nerve block
  • C = Cervical plexus block
  • S = Sciatic and femoral nerve block

SPINAL ANAESTHESIA

Spinal anaesthesia is one of the most commonly used forms of regional anaesthesia.

In this technique, the local anaesthetic is injected into the subarachnoid space.

FLOWCHART: A needle is inserted between L2 and L3 or between L3 and L4 -> The needle enters the subarachnoid space below the end of the spinal cord -> Local anaesthetic is injected into the cerebrospinal fluid -> The drug acts on spinal nerve roots -> The lower part of the body becomes anaesthetized TABLE: | Common Drugs Used in Spinal Anaesthesia | | Lignocaine | | Tetracaine | | Bupivacaine |

The level of anaesthesia depends on several factors.

TABLE: | Factor Affecting Level of Spinal Anaesthesia | Effect | | Site of injection | Higher injection produces higher block | | Amount of drug injected | Larger amount produces wider block | | Force of injection | Faster injection may spread the drug more | | Density of solution | Hyperbaric, hypobaric or isobaric solution affects spread | | Position of patient | Head-down or lateral position changes the level of block | FLOWCHART: A hyperbaric solution is injected -> The patient is placed head down -> The solution moves upward with gravity -> A higher spinal level is produced TABLE: | Use of Spinal Anaesthesia | | Surgery on lower limb | | Caesarean section | | Obstetric procedures | | Prostate surgery | | Surgery on perineum | | Appendicectomy |

Advantages of spinal anaesthesia:

TABLE: | Advantage | Explanation | | Consciousness is maintained | The patient remains awake | | Good muscle relaxation | Helps surgery | | Excellent analgesia | Effective pain relief | | Better tolerated in heart, lung and kidney disease | Safer than general anaesthesia in many patients |

COMPLICATIONS OF SPINAL ANAESTHESIA

Headache

FLOWCHART: The needle punctures the dura -> Cerebrospinal fluid leaks out -> Intracranial pressure decreases -> Post-spinal headache develops

Use of a very fine needle reduces the risk.

Hypotension

FLOWCHART: Spinal anaesthesia blocks sympathetic vasoconstrictor fibres -> Blood vessels dilate -> Venous return to the heart decreases -> Blood pressure falls

Hypotension is treated step by step.

FLOWCHART: Hypotension during spinal anaesthesia -> [The foot end of the bed is raised | If necessary, sympathomimetic drugs such as ephedrine, mephentermine or phenylephrine are given] -> [Venous return to the heart increases | Blood pressure improves]

Respiratory Paralysis

FLOWCHART: The spinal block spreads too high -> Intercostal muscles become paralysed -> Breathing becomes difficult -> Respiratory failure may occur

Other Complications

TABLE: | Complication | Explanation | | Urinary retention | Due to blockade of bladder reflexes | | Septic meningitis | Very rare with modern aseptic technique | | Nerve injury | Rare |
Mnemonic
"Spinal causes HURT"
  • H = Headache
  • U = Urinary retention
  • R = Respiratory paralysis
  • T = Tension (hypotension) of blood pressure falls

CONTRAINDICATIONS TO SPINAL ANAESTHESIA

TABLE: | Contraindication | Reason | | Young children | Difficult and unsafe | | Vertebral abnormality | Needle placement becomes difficult | | Infection at puncture site | Risk of meningitis | | Hypotension and shock | May become worse |

EPIDURAL ANAESTHESIA

In epidural anaesthesia, the local anaesthetic is injected into the epidural space.

FLOWCHART: A needle is inserted into the epidural space in the lumbar, thoracic or sacral region -> Local anaesthetic surrounds the spinal nerve roots -> Pain sensation is blocked -> The patient becomes anaesthetized below the level of injection TABLE: | Common Drugs Used in Epidural Anaesthesia | | Lignocaine | | Bupivacaine | | Ropivacaine |

Epidural anaesthesia differs from spinal anaesthesia.

TABLE: | Feature | Epidural Anaesthesia | Spinal Anaesthesia | | Site of injection | Epidural space | Subarachnoid space | | Onset | Slower | Faster | | Drug requirement | Larger amount | Smaller amount | | Safety | Safer | Less safe |

Epidural analgesia is especially useful in labour.

FLOWCHART: A low concentration of bupivacaine or ropivacaine is injected into the epidural space -> [Pain fibres are blocked | Motor fibres are relatively spared] -> [Labour pain decreases | The mother can still move]

Ropivacaine is preferred because it produces less motor block and is less cardiotoxic.

Mnemonic
"Epidural = External to the dura"

INTRAVENOUS REGIONAL ANAESTHESIA (BIER'S BLOCK)

This technique is mainly used for operations on the upper limb.

FLOWCHART: A tourniquet is applied to the limb -> Blood flow to and from the limb is blocked -> Local anaesthetic is injected into a vein of that limb -> The drug diffuses into the nerves of the isolated limb -> The limb becomes anaesthetized TABLE: | Common Drugs Used in Bier's Block | | Lignocaine 0.5% | | Prilocaine 0.5% | TABLE: | Main Uses of Bier's Block | | Minor surgery on upper limb | | Surgery on lower limb in selected cases |
Mnemonic
"Bier's Block = Blood Is Excluded Regionally"

DRUG INTERACTIONS

Lignocaine and Propranolol

FLOWCHART: Propranolol reduces hepatic blood flow -> The metabolism of lignocaine decreases -> The concentration of lignocaine in blood increases -> Lignocaine toxicity may occur

Procaine and Sulfonamides

FLOWCHART: Procaine is metabolized to para-aminobenzoic acid (PABA) -> PABA antagonizes the action of sulfonamides -> The antibacterial effect of sulfonamides decreases
Mnemonic
"Procaine Produces PABA and Prevents sulfonamides"
