HEPARIN vs WARFARIN

Heparin and warfarin are anticoagulant drugs used to prevent and treat thromboembolic disorders, but they differ markedly in their source, route of administration, mechanism of action, onset of action, monitoring, and clinical uses.

BASIC DIFFERENCE

HEPARIN

  • Parenteral anticoagulant
  • Rapid acting
  • Mainly used to initiate anticoagulant therapy

WARFARIN

  • Oral anticoagulant
  • Slow acting
  • Mainly used for long-term maintenance therapy

SOURCE

HEPARIN

  • Heparin is a naturally occurring anticoagulant obtained from animal sources such as:
    • Ox lung
    • Pig intestine

WARFARIN

  • Warfarin is:
    • A synthetic anticoagulant

SITE OF ACTION

HEPARIN

  • Heparin acts:
    • Both in vivo and in vitro
  • This means:
    • It can anticoagulate blood inside the body
    • It can also prevent clotting in collected blood samples outside the body

WARFARIN

  • Warfarin acts:
    • Only in vivo
  • It has no anticoagulant action outside the body because it works by inhibiting synthesis of clotting factors in the liver.

ROUTE OF ADMINISTRATION

HEPARIN

  • Heparin is not absorbed orally and therefore is given:
    • Intravenously (IV)
    • Subcutaneously (SC)

WARFARIN

  • Warfarin is well absorbed orally and is given:
    • By mouth

MECHANISM OF ACTION

HEPARIN

  • Heparin acts by activating antithrombin III.

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Heparin -> Binds to antithrombin III -> Forms heparin–antithrombin III complex -> Rapid inactivation of clotting factors: Xa IIa (thrombin) IXa XIa XIIa XIIIa -> Anticoagulant effect
  • Thus, heparin directly enhances destruction of already formed clotting factors.

WARFARIN

  • Warfarin inhibits synthesis of vitamin K–dependent clotting factors in the liver.

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Warfarin -> Inhibits vitamin K action -> Decreased synthesis of clotting factors: II VII IX X -> Blood coagulation decreases
  • Warfarin does not destroy existing clotting factors. It only prevents formation of new clotting factors.

ONSET AND DURATION OF ACTION

HEPARIN

FEATURES

  • Rapid onset of action
  • Short duration of action

DURATION

  • Approximately 3–6 hours

WHY RAPID?

  • Because it acts on already circulating clotting factors.

WARFARIN

FEATURES

  • Delayed onset of action
  • Long duration of action

DURATION

  • Approximately 3–6 days

WHY DELAYED?

  • Because previously synthesized clotting factors must first disappear from circulation before anticoagulation becomes evident.

MONITORING OF THERAPY

HEPARIN

  • Therapy is monitored using:
    • Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)
  • This assesses the intrinsic coagulation pathway.

WARFARIN

  • Therapy is monitored using:
    • INR (International Normalized Ratio)
  • This reflects the effect on the extrinsic pathway.

TREATMENT OF OVERDOSAGE

HEPARIN OVERDOSE

ANTIDOTE

  • Protamine sulphate
  • Protamine binds heparin and neutralizes its action.

WARFARIN OVERDOSE

TREATMENT

  • Vitamin K
  • Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)
  • Vitamin K restores synthesis of clotting factors.
  • FFP supplies already formed clotting factors immediately.

USE IN PREGNANCY

HEPARIN

  • Heparin:
    • Does not cross the placental barrier
    • Is safe during pregnancy

REASON

  • Large molecular size prevents placental transfer.

WARFARIN

  • Warfarin:
    • Crosses the placenta
    • Has teratogenic potential
  • Hence, it is contraindicated during pregnancy.

CLINICAL ROLE

HEPARIN

  • Mainly used:
    • To initiate anticoagulant therapy

REASON

  • Rapid onset of action

WARFARIN

  • Mainly used:
    • For maintenance anticoagulant therapy

REASON

  • Oral effectiveness and long duration of action

COST

HEPARIN

  • Expensive

WARFARIN

  • Less expensive

COMPLETE FLOW UNDERSTANDING

HEPARIN

FLOWCHART: Parenteral administration -> Activates antithrombin III -> Rapid destruction of clotting factors -> Immediate anticoagulant effect -> Used for emergency/initial therapy

WARFARIN

FLOWCHART: Oral administration -> Inhibits vitamin K–dependent clotting factor synthesis -> No effect on existing clotting factors -> Gradual anticoagulant effect -> Used for long-term maintenance therapy
