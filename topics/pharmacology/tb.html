Tuberculosis is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis because the organism produces slowly progressive granulomatous lesions with central caseation and fibrosis.
FLOWCHART: Tuberculosis -> Produces excessive fibrous tissue with central necrosis -> Vascularity of the lesion becomes poor -> Penetration of drugs into the lesion decreases -> Therefore prolonged treatment is required.

Classification

ANTITUBERCULAR DRUGS │ ├── 1. First-Line Drugs │ └── Isoniazid (H) + Rifampin (R) + Pyrazinamide (Z) + Ethambutol (E) │ These drugs are cheap, more effective, routinely used and less toxic. │ └── 2. Second-Line Drugs └── Para-aminosalicylic acid + Cycloserine + Terizidone + Ethionamide + Prothionamide + Kanamycin + Capreomycin + Amikacin + Levofloxacin + Moxifloxacin + Ofloxacin + Clarithromycin + Thiacetazone + Rifabutin + Rifapentine + Streptomycin These drugs are less effective, more expensive and more toxic and therefore are reserved for drug-resistant tuberculosis.

ISONIAZID (INH, H)

Isoniazid is the most widely used first-line antitubercular drug because it is orally effective, inexpensive and tuberculocidal.

It is active against both intracellular and extracellular bacilli because it penetrates into all infected sites.

FLOWCHART: Isoniazid -> Inhibits KasA and InhA enzymes -> Mycolic acid synthesis is inhibited -> Mycobacterial cell wall synthesis decreases -> Death of tubercle bacilli occurs -> Therefore isoniazid is tuberculocidal.

It also inhibits folate reductase and therefore DNA synthesis is reduced.

Pharmacokinetics of Isoniazid

  • Isoniazid is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract because it is orally active.
  • It is widely distributed throughout the body, including tubercular cavities and cerebrospinal fluid, because it penetrates tissues effectively.
FLOWCHART: Isoniazid -> Metabolized in the liver by acetylation -> Metabolites are excreted in urine -> Rate of acetylation is genetically determined -> Therefore patients may be rapid acetylators or slow acetylators.

Uses of Isoniazid

  • Isoniazid is a first-line drug for treatment of tuberculosis because it is highly effective.
  • It is also used for chemoprophylaxis of tuberculosis because it prevents development of active disease in high-risk individuals.

Adverse Effects of Isoniazid

TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Explanation | | Hepatotoxicity | Hepatic damage is more common in chronic alcoholics, elderly patients and rapid acetylators because toxic metabolites accumulate. The toxicity is reversible after discontinuation of the drug. | | Peripheral neuritis | Isoniazid is structurally similar to pyridoxine and therefore interferes with pyridoxine utilization and increases its excretion. Peripheral neuritis is more common in slow acetylators. | | Other effects | Fever, skin rashes, arthralgia, anaemia, gastrointestinal disturbances, optic neuritis and rarely convulsions may occur. | FLOWCHART: Isoniazid -> Interferes with pyridoxine utilization -> Pyridoxine deficiency develops -> Peripheral neuritis occurs.

Pyridoxine 10 mg/day is usually given along with isoniazid because it reduces the risk of peripheral neuritis, especially in alcoholics, diabetic patients, children and HIV-positive patients.

Pyridoxine 100 mg/day is used for treatment of established isoniazid-induced neuritis.

Drug Interactions of Isoniazid

FLOWCHART: Isoniazid -> Inhibits metabolism of phenytoin, carbamazepine and warfarin -> Plasma levels of these drugs increase -> Toxicity may occur.

RIFAMPIN (RIFAMPICIN, R)

Rifampin is a first-line antitubercular drug derived from rifamycin because it possesses rapid bactericidal action against all types of tubercle bacilli.

It acts against intracellular bacilli, extracellular bacilli and persisters because it penetrates caseous lesions.

FLOWCHART: Rifampin -> Binds to bacterial DNA-dependent RNA polymerase -> RNA synthesis is inhibited -> Mycobacterial protein synthesis stops -> Therefore rifampin is bactericidal.

Rifampin is also active against Neisseria meningitidis, Haemophilus influenzae, Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli and Pseudomonas.

Pharmacokinetics of Rifampin

Rifampin is given orally and is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract, although food decreases its absorption.

FLOWCHART: Rifampin -> Distributed widely throughout the body -> Metabolized in the liver -> Active deacetylated metabolite formed -> Excreted in bile -> Undergoes enterohepatic circulation -> Remaining drug is excreted in urine.

Uses of Rifampin

TABLE: | Condition | Role of Rifampin | | Tuberculosis | Rifampin is used with isoniazid and other antitubercular drugs because it rapidly kills bacilli and persisters. | | Chemoprophylaxis of TB | Rifampin is used to prevent active disease in high-risk individuals. | | Leprosy | Rifampin is an important drug in multidrug therapy for leprosy. | | Meningococcal and H. influenzae meningitis prophylaxis | Rifampin eradicates the carrier state because it reaches high concentrations in the nasopharynx. | | Brucellosis | Rifampin is used with doxycycline because the combination is more effective. | | Staphylococcal infections | Rifampin is combined with beta-lactam antibiotics in endocarditis and osteomyelitis and is also active against MRSA. |

Adverse Effects of Rifampin

TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Explanation | | Hepatitis | Hepatotoxicity is the most important adverse effect and is more common in alcoholics and elderly patients. | | Flu-like syndrome | Fever, chills, headache, muscle pain and joint pain may occur. | | Gastrointestinal effects | Nausea, vomiting and abdominal discomfort may occur. | | Skin reactions | Skin rashes, itching and flushing may occur. | | Orange-red discoloration | Urine, tears, saliva, sweat and sputum become orange-red because rifampin is excreted in body fluids. This effect is harmless. |

Drug Interactions of Rifampin

FLOWCHART: Rifampin -> Potent microsomal enzyme inducer -> Metabolism of oral contraceptives, anticoagulants, oral antidiabetic drugs and HIV drugs increases -> Plasma concentration of these drugs decreases -> Therapeutic failure may occur.
⚠️
Because rifampin reduces the efficacy of oral contraceptives, either a high-dose estrogen preparation or an alternative contraceptive method should be used.

PYRAZINAMIDE (Z)

Pyrazinamide is a synthetic analogue of nicotinamide because it possesses sterilizing activity against intracellular bacilli in acidic environments.

FLOWCHART: Pyrazinamide -> Converted to pyrazinoic acid -> Fatty acid synthase is inhibited -> Mycolic acid synthesis decreases -> Tubercle bacilli are destroyed.

Pyrazinamide is especially effective against intracellular bacilli because it acts well in acidic pH.

It is given orally, absorbed well from the gastrointestinal tract and distributed widely throughout the body including cerebrospinal fluid.

It is metabolized in the liver and excreted in urine.

TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Explanation | | Hepatotoxicity | Hepatotoxicity is dose-dependent and is the most important adverse effect. | | Hyperuricaemia | Pyrazinamide impairs urate excretion and therefore serum uric acid increases. | | Gout | Acute attacks of gout may occur in susceptible individuals because of hyperuricaemia. | | Other effects | Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, fever and skin rashes may occur. |

ETHAMBUTOL (E)

Ethambutol is a first-line antitubercular drug because it is useful in combination therapy and also acts against Mycobacterium avium complex.

FLOWCHART: Ethambutol -> Enters mycobacterial cell -> Inhibits arabinosyl transferase -> Arabinogalactan synthesis decreases -> Linkage between mycolic acids and peptidoglycan is impaired -> Cell wall synthesis is inhibited -> Therefore ethambutol is bacteriostatic.

Ethambutol is mainly used in combination with other antitubercular drugs because it prevents emergence of resistance and hastens sputum conversion.

There is no cross-resistance with other antitubercular drugs because its mechanism of action is different.

It is well absorbed after oral administration, widely distributed in the body, metabolized in the liver and excreted in urine.

It crosses the blood-brain barrier during meningitis.

TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Explanation | | Optic neuritis | Optic neuritis is the most important adverse effect and causes decreased visual acuity and red-green colour blindness. | | Hyperuricaemia | Ethambutol decreases clearance of urates and therefore raises serum uric acid. | | Other effects | Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, skin rashes, itching and joint pain may occur. | FLOWCHART: Ethambutol -> Optic neuritis develops -> Visual acuity decreases -> Red-green colour vision defect appears -> Therefore periodic eye examination is necessary.

STREPTOMYCIN

Streptomycin is an aminoglycoside and is now considered a second-line antitubercular drug because it is less commonly used.

It is bactericidal and is especially active against extracellular bacilli in alkaline pH.

Because streptomycin is not absorbed orally, it must be given intramuscularly.

It is mainly used in tuberculosis meningitis, when a first-line drug has to be replaced due to toxicity or as a substitute for amikacin.

The main adverse effects are ototoxicity, nephrotoxicity and neuromuscular blockade because these are characteristic toxicities of aminoglycosides.

PARA-AMINOSALICYLIC ACID (PAS)

Para-aminosalicylic acid is structurally similar to sulfonamides because it inhibits folate synthesis.

FLOWCHART: PAS -> Competitively inhibits folate synthetase -> Tetrahydrofolic acid synthesis decreases -> Growth and multiplication of mycobacteria are inhibited -> Therefore PAS is bacteriostatic.

PAS is rapidly absorbed orally, metabolized in the liver by acetylation and excreted in urine.

FLOWCHART: PAS -> Inhibits acetylation of isoniazid -> Plasma level of isoniazid increases.

PAS is now reserved for multidrug-resistant tuberculosis because it is less effective and more toxic.

The most common adverse effects are anorexia, nausea, vomiting and abdominal discomfort because it irritates the gastrointestinal tract.

Other adverse effects include hepatic damage, drug fever, skin rashes and thrombocytopenia.

ETHIONAMIDE

Ethionamide is structurally similar to isoniazid but is less effective.

FLOWCHART: Ethionamide -> Inhibits mycolic acid synthesis -> Mycobacterial cell wall synthesis decreases -> Therefore ethionamide is bacteriostatic.

The common adverse effects are nausea, vomiting and epigastric pain.

Other effects include hepatitis, headache, blurred vision and paraesthesia.

CYCLOSERINE

Cycloserine is a second-line bacteriostatic antitubercular drug.

FLOWCHART: Cycloserine -> Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis -> Growth of mycobacteria is inhibited.

Cycloserine is widely distributed in the body including cerebrospinal fluid.

The most important adverse effects involve the central nervous system and include headache, tremor, psychosis and convulsions.

TERIZIDONE

Terizidone has a mechanism of action similar to cycloserine because it also inhibits cell wall synthesis.

It is effective in both pulmonary and extrapulmonary tuberculosis.

Because it achieves good urinary concentration, it is especially useful in tuberculosis of the urinary tract.

Terizidone is better tolerated than cycloserine because it produces fewer central nervous system adverse effects.

OTHER ANTITUBERCULOSIS AGENTS

TABLE: | Group | Drugs | Important Feature | | Fluoroquinolones | Moxifloxacin, Levofloxacin, Ofloxacin, Ciprofloxacin | These drugs are bactericidal and are given orally. | | Aminoglycosides | Amikacin, Kanamycin | These drugs are bactericidal and are administered parenterally. Amikacin is less toxic than kanamycin. | | Capreomycin | Capreomycin | This drug is given intramuscularly and may produce nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity. | | Macrolides | Azithromycin, Clarithromycin | These drugs are given orally. | | Rifamycins | Rifabutin, Rifapentine | These drugs are bactericidal and are given orally. |

Rifabutin

Rifabutin is preferred to rifampin in HIV-positive patients on protease inhibitors because it is a weaker enzyme inducer.

It is also used in Mycobacterium avium complex infection together with clarithromycin and ethambutol.

Rifapentine

Rifapentine is an analogue of rifampin and is a potent enzyme inducer.

Bedaquiline

Bedaquiline is a diarylquinoline used for MDR-TB, RR-TB and XDR-TB because it acts against resistant and dormant bacilli.

FLOWCHART: Bedaquiline -> Inhibits mycobacterial ATP synthase -> Energy production stops -> Mycobacteria die -> Therefore bedaquiline is bactericidal.

Bedaquiline has a very long half-life of about 160 days and is given orally.

⚠️
It may cause hepatotoxicity and prolongation of QT interval.

Delamanid

FLOWCHART: Delamanid -> Inhibits mycolic acid synthesis + Releases nitric oxide -> Mycobacteria are damaged -> Therefore delamanid is bactericidal.

Delamanid has a half-life of approximately 36 hours.

Treatment of Tuberculosis

FLOWCHART: Revised National Tuberculosis Control Programme (1997) -> DOTS implemented -> In 2020 renamed as National Tuberculosis Elimination Programme (NTEP).

Directly observed treatment is important because a treatment supporter watches the patient take every dose and therefore improves compliance.

Objectives of Multidrug Therapy

MULTIDRUG THERAPY │ ├── Makes the patient noninfectious early ├── Uses synergistic drugs and shortens duration of therapy ├── Prevents emergence of drug resistance ├── Prevents relapse └── Decreases mortality

Bacterial Subpopulations in Tuberculosis

TABLE: | Bacillary Population | Site | Drugs Effective | | Extracellular rapidly multiplying bacilli | Wall of cavities with neutral pH and high oxygen tension | Isoniazid, Rifampin, Ethambutol and Streptomycin | | Extracellular intermittently multiplying bacilli | Caseous material | Rifampin | | Intracellular and extracellular slowly multiplying bacilli | Macrophages and acidic inflammatory sites | Pyrazinamide is most effective; Rifampin, Isoniazid and Ethambutol are less effective | | Dormant bacilli | Dormant lesions | Bedaquiline |

Pyrazinamide and rifampin possess good sterilizing activity because they act against persisters.

Ethambutol is primarily bacteriostatic because it mainly prevents development of resistance.

Daily Dosage of First-Line Drugs

TABLE: | Drug | Adult Dose | Children Dose | Maximum in Children | | Isoniazid | 5 mg/kg/day | 10 mg/kg/day | 300 mg | | Rifampin | 10 mg/kg/day | 15 mg/kg/day | 600 mg | | Pyrazinamide | 25 mg/kg/day | 35 mg/kg/day | 2000 mg | | Ethambutol | 15 mg/kg/day | 20 mg/kg/day | 1500 mg |

Regimen for Drug-Sensitive Tuberculosis

FLOWCHART: Drug-Sensitive Tuberculosis -> 2HRZE/4HRE -> Intensive Phase for 2 months -> H + R + Z + E -> Continuation Phase for 4 months -> H + R + E

The intensive phase uses four drugs because rapidly multiplying bacilli and naturally resistant bacilli must be killed.

The continuation phase uses three drugs because persisters must be eliminated and relapse prevented.

Weight-Based Daily FDC Dosing for Adults

TABLE: | Weight Category | HRZE Tablets | HRE Tablets | | 25–34 kg | 2 | 2 | | 35–49 kg | 3 | 3 | | 50–64 kg | 4 | 4 | | 65–75 kg | 5 | 5 | | >75 kg | 6 | 6 |

If body weight increases by more than 5 kg and enters the next weight band, the higher dose should be used because adequate drug exposure is required.

Drug-Resistant Tuberculosis

TABLE: | Type | Definition | | Monoresistant TB | Resistance to only one first-line drug | | Poly-drug-resistant TB | Resistance to more than one first-line drug but not both isoniazid and rifampin | | MDR-TB | Resistance to both isoniazid and rifampin with or without resistance to other drugs | | XDR-TB | MDR-TB with additional resistance to a fluoroquinolone and either bedaquiline or linezolid |

Grouping of Drugs for MDR-TB

TABLE: | Group | Drugs | | Group A | Levofloxacin or Moxifloxacin + Bedaquiline + Linezolid | | Group B | Clofazimine + Cycloserine or Terizidone | | Group C | Ethambutol + Delamanid + Pyrazinamide + Imipenem-cilastatin or Meropenem + Amikacin or Streptomycin + Ethionamide or Prothionamide + PAS |

MDR-TB Regimens

FLOWCHART: H Mono-/Poly-Drug Resistant TB -> (6-9) Levofloxacin + Rifampin + Ethambutol + Pyrazinamide FLOWCHART: Shorter Oral Bedaquiline-Containing MDR/RR-TB Regimen -> Initial Phase for 4-6 months -> Bedaquiline + Levofloxacin + Clofazimine + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol + High-Dose Isoniazid + Ethionamide -> Continuation Phase for 5 months -> Levofloxacin + Clofazimine + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol FLOWCHART: Longer Oral M/XDR-TB Regimen -> 18-20 months of Bedaquiline + Levofloxacin + Linezolid + Clofazimine + Cycloserine

Pyridoxine should be continued throughout treatment because it reduces neurotoxicity.

TB in HIV-Positive Patients

Treatment of tuberculosis in HIV-positive patients is generally similar to treatment in non-HIV patients because the same daily short-course chemotherapy is effective.

Rifabutin is preferred to rifampin in HIV-positive patients receiving protease inhibitors because rifabutin is a weaker enzyme inducer.

Tuberculosis in Pregnancy

First-line drugs such as isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide and ethambutol can be used in pregnancy because they are considered relatively safe.

Rifampin is restarted first, followed by isoniazid after 7 days. Pyrazinamide should not be restarted if both are tolerated. | | Skin rash | Drugs should be restarted one by one at low doses because the responsible drug must be identified. | | Visual disturbance | Ethambutol should be discontinued immediately because optic neuritis may become permanent. |

Chemoprophylaxis of TB

Chemoprophylaxis is indicated in household contacts of patients with tuberculosis and in people planned for organ transplantation because these individuals are at high risk of developing active disease.

FLOWCHART: High-risk patient without active TB -> Isoniazid 5 mg/kg/day for 6 months -> Maximum dose 300 mg/day. FLOWCHART: Alternative regimen -> Isoniazid + Rifapentine once weekly for 3 months.

Role of Glucocorticoids in Tuberculosis

Glucocorticoids are relatively contraindicated in tuberculosis because they may suppress immunity.

However, they may be used together with effective antitubercular therapy in selected situations.

TABLE: | Situation | Role of Glucocorticoids | | TB of pleura, pericardium and meninges | Glucocorticoids reduce fibrosis and its complications. | | Hypersensitivity reactions to anti-TB drugs | Glucocorticoids suppress allergic reactions. | | TB of eye, larynx and genitourinary tract | Glucocorticoids prevent fibrosis and scar formation. |

Prednisolone is preferred in most situations because it is effective and safe.

Dexamethasone is preferred in tuberculous meningitis because it lacks mineralocorticoid activity.

⚠️
Glucocorticoids are contraindicated in intestinal tuberculosis because they may increase the risk of perforation.

MYCOBACTERIUM AVIUM COMPLEX (MAC) INFECTIONS

FLOWCHART: MAC Infection -> Clarithromycin or Azithromycin + Ethambutol + Rifabutin

A combination of drugs is used because prolonged therapy is necessary and resistance must be prevented.

The duration of therapy is usually 18-24 months because MAC infection responds slowly.

For prophylaxis, azithromycin, clarithromycin or rifabutin may be used because these drugs prevent MAC disease in high-risk patients.
