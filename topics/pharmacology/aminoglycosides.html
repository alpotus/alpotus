Aminoglycosides include streptomycin, gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, kanamycin, sisomicin, neomycin, framycetin, netilmicin and paromomycin because all of them contain amino sugars linked by glycosidic bonds and possess similar antibacterial properties.

COMMON PROPERTIES OF AMINOGLYCOSIDES

1. STRUCTURE

  • Aminoglycosides contain two or more amino sugars attached by glycosidic linkage to a hexose ring because this structural feature is common to the entire group.

2. PHARMACOKINETICS

Absorption

FLOWCHART: Highly polar compounds -> Poor absorption from gastrointestinal tract -> Therefore administered by intramuscular or intravenous route for systemic effect

Distribution

FLOWCHART: Distribution -> Distributed mainly into extracellular fluid -> Poor penetration into cerebrospinal fluid

Metabolism

FLOWCHART: Metabolism -> Not metabolized in the body -> Remain unchanged

Excretion

FLOWCHART: Excretion -> Excreted unchanged in urine -> Mainly by glomerular filtration

3. ANTIBACTERIAL ACTION

FLOWCHART: Antibacterial Properties -> Aminoglycosides -> Inhibit protein synthesis + Disrupt bacterial cell membrane -> Therefore produce bactericidal action -> Mainly effective against gram-negative aerobic organisms

Activity

FLOWCHART: Activity -> Increased at alkaline pH -> Because alkaline environment facilitates antibacterial action

4. OXYGEN DEPENDENCE

  • Aminoglycosides require oxygen for transport into bacterial cells because their entry mechanism is oxygen dependent.
  • Therefore anaerobic organisms are resistant because the drug cannot enter these bacteria.

5. RESISTANCE

  • Aminoglycosides exhibit partial cross-resistance because resistance to one aminoglycoside may produce resistance to other drugs of the same group.

6. TOXICITY

Ototoxicity

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycosides accumulate in inner ear -> Damage to cochlear/vestibular structures -> Ototoxicity

Nephrotoxicity

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycosides accumulate in kidney -> Tubular damage -> Nephrotoxicity
  • Aminoglycosides possess a narrow safety margin because the toxic dose is close to the therapeutic dose.

Mechanism of Action

Aminoglycosides are bactericidal agents because they inhibit protein synthesis and also damage the bacterial cell membrane.

FLOWCHART: Entry of aminoglycoside into bacterial cell requires oxygen -> Aminoglycoside enters the bacterial cell -> Binds to the 30S ribosomal subunit -> Inhibits initiation of protein synthesis -> Causes misreading of codons -> Produces premature termination of protein synthesis -> Wrong amino acids are inserted into the growing peptide chain -> Defective proteins are formed -> Defective proteins become incorporated into the bacterial cell membrane -> Membrane permeability becomes altered -> Bacterial cell membrane is disrupted -> Therefore aminoglycosides produce a bactericidal effect.

Mechanisms of Bacterial Resistance

FLOWCHART: Bacterial resistance to aminoglycosides -> Occurs because bacteria produce enzymes that inactivate the drug -> Or because entry of the drug into the bacterial cell is decreased -> Or because the affinity of the drug for the ribosome is reduced.

Special Pharmacodynamic Properties

  • Aminoglycosides produce concentration-dependent killing because a higher plasma concentration kills a larger number of bacteria more rapidly.
  • Aminoglycosides also produce a postantibiotic effect because bactericidal activity continues even when the serum concentration falls below the minimum inhibitory concentration.
  • Because of concentration-dependent killing and postantibiotic effect, once-daily dosing is effective.

Dosing Regimens

Once-Daily Dosing Regimen

  • In the once-daily dosing regimen, the total daily dose is given as a single injection because aminoglycosides act better when a high peak concentration is achieved.
  • This regimen is preferred because it is as effective as multiple-dose regimens.
  • It is safer because the trough concentration remains below the threshold for toxicity for a longer period of time.
  • It is more convenient because only one injection is required each day.

Multiple-Daily Dosing Regimen

  • In the multiple-daily dosing regimen, the total daily dose is divided into two or three equal doses because smaller doses are administered at regular intervals.
  • Once-daily dosing is not preferred in bacterial endocarditis, in children and in patients with renal impairment because these conditions require more careful control of blood levels.
  • Dose adjustment is necessary according to body weight and creatinine clearance because aminoglycosides are eliminated by the kidney.

Adverse Effects and Precautions

1. Ototoxicity

Aminoglycosides may produce vestibular and cochlear dysfunction because they accumulate in the perilymph and endolymph of the inner ear and progressively damage vestibular and cochlear hair cells.

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycoside accumulates in endolymph and perilymph -> Progressive damage to vestibular and cochlear hair cells -> VIII cranial nerve dysfunction -> Ototoxicity develops.

Streptomycin and gentamicin mainly affect vestibular function because they produce greater damage to the vestibular apparatus.

  • Vestibular dysfunction causes intense headache, which is usually the earliest symptom, because the vestibular system is affected first.
  • Other manifestations include dizziness, nausea, vomiting, vertigo, nystagmus and ataxia because balance is disturbed.

Amikacin and kanamycin mainly affect auditory function because they produce greater cochlear damage.

  • Cochlear dysfunction initially causes tinnitus because the auditory apparatus becomes irritated.
  • Tinnitus is reversible on discontinuation of the drug, but deafness is permanent because cochlear hair cell damage becomes irreversible.

The important risk factors for ototoxicity include elderly age, repeated courses of aminoglycosides, persistently increased plasma concentration, pre-existing auditory impairment and concurrent use of other ototoxic drugs such as vancomycin, minocycline and loop diuretics because these factors increase damage to the ear.

2. Nephrotoxicity

Aminoglycosides may produce nephrotoxicity because they become concentrated in the renal cortex and damage the kidney.

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycosides accumulate in renal cortex -> Renal tubular cells become damaged -> Nephrotoxicity develops -> Urinary concentrating capacity decreases and albuminuria may occur.
  • Nephrotoxicity is usually reversible because kidney function improves after the drug is discontinued.

SEVERITY AMONG DRUGS

Neomycin

FLOWCHART: Neomycin -> Produces highest incidence of nephrotoxicity -> Most nephrotoxic aminoglycoside

Streptomycin

FLOWCHART: Streptomycin -> Produces least nephrotoxicity

RISK FACTORS

Elderly Patients

FLOWCHART: Increased susceptibility -> Elderly patients

Pre-existing Renal Disease

FLOWCHART: Pre-existing renal disease -> Reduced renal reserve -> Increased toxicity

Concurrent Nephrotoxic Drugs

FLOWCHART: Concurrent nephrotoxic drugs -> Amphotericin B Vancomycin Cisplatin Cyclosporine -> These increase renal damage -> Therefore risk of nephrotoxicity increases

3. Neuromuscular Blocking Effect

Aminoglycosides may produce neuromuscular blockade because they inhibit the release of acetylcholine from motor nerve endings.

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycoside inhibits release of acetylcholine from motor nerve endings -> Neuromuscular transmission becomes impaired -> Muscular paralysis and apnoea may occur.

This effect may be reversed by administration of calcium salts because calcium improves acetylcholine release.

⚠️
Patients with myasthenia gravis are more susceptible because they already have impaired neuromuscular transmission. Therefore aminoglycosides should be avoided in such patients.

4. Hypersensitivity

Hypersensitivity reactions are uncommon because aminoglycosides are relatively non-allergenic.

  • Occasionally skin rashes, itching, drug fever and eosinophilia may occur because of allergic reaction.
  • Cross-sensitivity between aminoglycosides may occur because these drugs are structurally related.

5. Use During Pregnancy

⚠️
Aminoglycosides should be avoided during pregnancy because they may produce ototoxicity in the fetus.

6. Pharmaceutical Interaction

Aminoglycosides are not compatible with many other drugs because they may undergo pharmaceutical interaction in the same syringe or infusion bottle.

⚠️
Therefore aminoglycosides should never be mixed with other drugs in the same syringe or infusion bottle.

IMPORTANT AMINOGLYCOSIDES

Streptomycin

Discovery

  • Streptomycin was the first aminoglycoside discovered in 1944 because it was the earliest member of this group to be identified.

Uses

  • Streptomycin is one of the first-line drugs for tuberculosis because it is effective against Mycobacterium tuberculosis when combined with other antitubercular drugs.
  • It is also useful in tularaemia, plague and brucellosis because these organisms are sensitive to streptomycin.

Gentamicin

General

  • Gentamicin is the most commonly used aminoglycoside because it is inexpensive and highly effective against aerobic gram-negative bacilli.

Spectrum

  • It is effective against infections due to Escherichia coli, Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterobacter and Pseudomonas aeruginosa because these organisms are highly susceptible.
  • It is also effective against gram-positive organisms such as enterococci, Streptococcus viridans and staphylococci, but it is not effective against Mycobacterium tuberculosis.

Administration

  • Gentamicin is available for both parenteral and topical administration because it is used in both systemic and local infections.

Neomycin

General

  • Neomycin is highly nephrotoxic and therefore it is never used for systemic administration.

Use

  • It is used only for local effect because oral absorption is poor and systemic toxicity is marked.

Topical Uses

  • Infections of the skin and mucous membranes such as ulcers, wounds and burns because it is active against surface organisms.
  • Infections of the eye and external ear because it can be applied locally.

Combination

  • Neomycin is often combined with bacitracin or polymyxin B because combination therapy broadens the antibacterial spectrum.

Oral Uses

  • Neomycin sulphate is useful with metronidazole for preparation of the bowel before abdominal surgery because it destroys intestinal bacteria.
  • It may also be used in hepatic encephalopathy because it reduces blood ammonia by destroying ammonia-producing colonic bacteria.

Note

  • However, neomycin has largely been replaced by oral lactulose because neomycin is highly toxic.

Framycetin (Soframycin)

General

  • Framycetin (Soframycin) is similar to neomycin because it is also highly nephrotoxic and therefore is not used systemically.

Use

  • It is widely used topically for infections of the skin, eye and ear because it is effective when applied locally.

Amikacin

General

  • Amikacin has the broadest spectrum of activity among the aminoglycosides because it is resistant to many aminoglycoside-inactivating enzymes.

Uses

  • It is useful in serious nosocomial gram-negative infections and tuberculosis because it remains active against many resistant organisms.

Tobramycin

General

  • Tobramycin is similar to gentamicin in most respects because it has a similar antibacterial spectrum.

Special Feature

  • However, tobramycin is superior to gentamicin against Pseudomonas aeruginosa because it has stronger antipseudomonal activity.

Use

  • Therefore it is particularly useful in serious infections caused by Pseudomonas aeruginosa.

Paromomycin

General

  • Paromomycin is unusual because it also possesses activity against protozoa.

Uses

  • It is useful in intestinal amoebiasis, giardiasis, vaginal trichomoniasis, visceral leishmaniasis and hepatic encephalopathy because it acts locally in the intestine and against certain protozoal organisms.

Netilmicin

General

  • Netilmicin is resistant to aminoglycoside-inactivating enzymes because its structure prevents destruction by these enzymes.

Use

  • Therefore it is effective against many bacteria that have become resistant to gentamicin.

THERAPEUTIC USES OF GENTAMICIN AND OTHER AMINOGLYCOSIDES

GENERAL

  • Gentamicin is the most commonly used aminoglycoside because it is cheap and effective against most aerobic gram-negative bacilli.

1. SEVERE AEROBIC GRAM-NEGATIVE BACILLARY INFECTIONS

  • Aminoglycosides are useful in:
  • Urinary tract infections with pyelonephritis because they are highly active against gram-negative bacilli
  • Pneumonia, meningitis, osteomyelitis, septicaemia, peritonitis and infected burns because these infections are often caused by susceptible gram-negative organisms
  • These infections are commonly caused by Pseudomonas, Klebsiella, Escherichia coli and Proteus because these organisms are major aerobic gram-negative pathogens.

Antipseudomonal Activity

TABLE: | Drug | Feature | | Gentamicin | Effective against Pseudomonas aeruginosa | | Tobramycin | Superior antipseudomonal activity | | Amikacin | Active against resistant strains | | Netilmicin | Active against resistant strains |
  • Amikacin and netilmicin are particularly useful in serious nosocomial infections because they remain effective against resistant gram-negative bacilli.

Combination Therapy

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycosides Combination -> Aminoglycosides + Penicillins / 3rd generation cephalosporins -> Broadens antibacterial spectrum -> Produces synergistic effect -> Decreases emergence of resistance

2. BACTERIAL ENDOCARDITIS

  • Gentamicin is used in bacterial endocarditis caused by Streptococcus viridans and Enterococcus because it acts synergistically when combined with other antibiotics.

Regimens

  • Streptococcus viridans endocarditis → Penicillin G + Gentamicin
  • Enterococcal endocarditis → Ampicillin + Gentamicin
  • Enterococcal endocarditis (β-lactam allergy) → Vancomycin + Gentamicin
FLOWCHART: Combination -> Broadens spectrum -> Produces synergistic effect -> Decreases emergence of resistance

Prophylaxis

FLOWCHART: High-risk patients before surgical procedures -> Ampicillin + Gentamicin -> Broad antibacterial coverage

3. TUBERCULOSIS

FLOWCHART: Aminoglycosides in Tuberculosis -> Streptomycin / Kanamycin / Amikacin -> Used with other antitubercular drugs -> Effective against Mycobacterium tuberculosis

4. OTHER GRAM-NEGATIVE INFECTIONS

TABLE: | Disease | Drug | | Plague | Streptomycin / Gentamicin | | Brucellosis | Streptomycin / Gentamicin + Doxycycline | | Tularaemia | Streptomycin / Gentamicin (drug of choice) | | Alternatives | Fluoroquinolones, Tetracyclines |

5. TOPICAL USES

  • Gentamicin, tobramycin, neomycin, sisomicin and framycetin are used topically in:
  • Skin infections
  • Eye infections
  • Ear infections
  • Because they are effective against gram-negative organisms at local sites.
