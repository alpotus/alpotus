B-Lactam antibiotics include penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems and monobactams because all of them possess a B-lactam ring in their chemical structure; hence, they are called B-lactam antibiotics.

General Overview

FLOWCHART: B-Lactam antibiotics -> Contain a B-lactam ring in the chemical structure -> Include -> [Penicillins | Cephalosporins | Carbapenems | Monobactams] -> All are generally safe for use during pregnancy, lactation and in children unless the patient is allergic to them. Structure of penicillin
A = Thiazolidine ring
B = B-Lactam ring
FLOWCHART: Thiazolidine ring + B-Lactam ring -> Produces the characteristic structure of penicillin -> The B-lactam ring is the site destroyed by penicillinase.

PENICILLINS

Penicillin was the first antibiotic developed and used clinically because it was discovered accidentally by Alexander Fleming. The source of penicillin is the high-yielding Penicillium chrysogenum.

Mechanism of Action

FLOWCHART: Bacterial cell wall -> Contains peptidoglycan strands -> Peptidoglycan contains amino sugars -> N-acetylmuramic acid (NAM) + N-acetylglucosamine (NAG) -> The enzyme transpeptidase, which is a penicillin-binding protein (PBP), removes the terminal alanine of one strand -> The strand becomes linked with glycine of the adjacent strand -> Cross-linking occurs -> Because of cross-linking, the cell wall becomes rigid and stable.

B-Lactam antibiotics are structural analogues of D-alanine because they resemble the terminal D-alanine portion of peptidoglycan.

FLOWCHART: B-Lactam antibiotic -> Binds to transpeptidase (PBP) -> Inhibits transpeptidase -> Prevents cross-linking of peptidoglycan strands -> Prevents synthesis of bacterial cell wall -> Produces cell wall-deficient forms -> The bacteria undergo lysis -> Hence, B-lactam antibiotics produce a bactericidal effect.
💡
B-Lactams exert their bactericidal effect only when the bacteria are actively multiplying and synthesizing cell wall because inhibition of cell wall synthesis has no effect on resting organisms.

PBPs consist of transpeptidase, other enzymes and related proteins because they are located in the bacterial cell membrane and participate in cell wall synthesis.

Gram-Positive vs Gram-Negative

FLOWCHART: Gram-positive bacteria -> Contain highly cross-linked peptidoglycan -> The peptidoglycan is 50–100 layers thick and lies near the cell surface -> Therefore gram-positive organisms are highly susceptible to penicillin. FLOWCHART: Gram-negative bacteria -> Contain only 1–2 layers of peptidoglycan -> In addition, there is an outer lipopolysaccharide layer -> Therefore the drug penetrates less effectively -> Hence gram-negative organisms are less susceptible to penicillin.

Mechanism of Bacterial Resistance

  • 1. Resistance occurs because bacteria produce B-lactamases which destroy the B-lactam ring, as seen in Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, gonococci and Haemophilus influenzae.
  • 2. Resistance occurs because bacteria develop altered PBPs which have less affinity for B-lactam antibiotics, as seen in Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • 3. Resistance occurs because the drug is unable to penetrate adequately to its site of action.

Penicillin G (Benzyl Penicillin)

Pharmacokinetics: FLOWCHART: Penicillin G is acid labile because most of the orally administered drug is destroyed by gastric acid. -> Therefore penicillin G is usually administered by the intravenous route.
  • Penicillin G can also be administered by the intramuscular route, but intramuscular injection is painful.
  • Penicillin G is widely distributed in body tissues, but it crosses the blood–brain barrier poorly.
FLOWCHART: However, during meningitis the blood–brain barrier becomes inflamed -> Adequate amounts of penicillin G reach the cerebrospinal fluid. FLOWCHART: Penicillin G is rapidly excreted in urine mainly by active tubular secretion. -> Because renal function is not completely developed in infants and neonates -> Excretion of penicillin is slow in these age groups. FLOWCHART: Probenecid competes with penicillin for active tubular secretion -> Therefore probenecid augments and prolongs the action of penicillin.

Preparations of Penicillin G

The duration of action of penicillin G is increased because it can be combined with poorly water-soluble compounds such as procaine or benzathine.

FLOWCHART: These preparations form aqueous suspensions -> Therefore they act as repository or depot penicillins. 1. Crystalline penicillin G: FLOWCHART: Given by intravenous route in a dose of 20–24 million units daily -> Acts for 4–8 hours because it has a rapid onset and reaches high plasma concentration -> Therefore it is used in severe infections such as meningitis, endocarditis and pneumonia. 2. Procaine penicillin G: FLOWCHART: Given intramuscularly in a dose of 600,000–1,200,000 units daily -> Acts for 12–24 hours because the procaine component slows absorption -> Produces moderate plasma concentration -> Therefore it is used in mild-to-moderate infections and is less painful because of the procaine component. 3. Benzathine penicillin G: FLOWCHART: Given intramuscularly in a dose of 600,000–2,400,000 units once a month -> Acts for 3–4 weeks because benzathine releases penicillin very slowly -> Therefore it has the longest duration of action among penicillins -> Hence it is used in syphilis and prophylaxis of rheumatic fever. 4. Fortified procaine penicillin G: FLOWCHART: Contains 300,000 units of procaine penicillin G + 100,000 units of penicillin G -> Given intramuscularly -> Produces both rapid onset and longer duration of action because of the combination of crystalline and repository forms.

Adverse Reactions

Penicillins are relatively safe, but they may produce hypersensitivity reactions because they act as allergens. The manifestations include skin rashes, urticaria, fever, dermatitis, bronchospasm, angioedema, joint pain, serum sickness and anaphylactic reaction.

⚠️
Anaphylactic shock due to penicillin:
• Occurs because of IgE-mediated immediate hypersensitivity reaction (Type I).
• The reaction is not dose related; therefore it can occur with any dosage form of penicillin.
• Major manifestations: Severe hypotension + Bronchospasm + Laryngeal oedema.
FLOWCHART: Cross-reactivity occurs among penicillins because all of them possess a similar basic structure. -> Cross-reactivity can also occur among other B-lactam antibiotics.

Treatment of Anaphylactic Shock

  1. Adrenaline should be given in a dose of 0.3–0.5 mL of 1:1000 solution intramuscularly because adrenaline rapidly reverses hypotension and bronchospasm.
  2. Hydrocortisone 200 mg should be given intravenously because corticosteroids reduce inflammation and prevent recurrence.
  3. Diphenhydramine 50–100 mg should be given intravenously or intramuscularly because antihistamines reduce histamine-mediated symptoms.

Precautions

  • Before giving penicillin, a history of previous administration and allergic manifestations must be obtained because previous exposure increases the risk of hypersensitivity.
  • Penicillin should be avoided in patients with asthma, allergic rhinitis and hay fever.
  • A sensitivity test should be performed by intradermal injection on the ventral aspect of the forearm because itching, erythema and wheal formation indicate sensitivity.
  • However, a negative skin test does not ensure complete safety because anaphylaxis can still occur. Therefore adrenaline and hydrocortisone should always be kept ready.

Other effects: Pain and sterile abscess at IM site; Thrombophlebitis with prolonged IV administration.

Jarisch–Herxheimer Reaction

FLOWCHART: Jarisch–Herxheimer reaction occurs during penicillin therapy for syphilis because endotoxins are released from dead organisms. -> This causes an acute exacerbation of the signs and symptoms of syphilis.

Manifestations: Fever + Chills + Myalgia + Hypotension + Circulatory collapse. Treated with aspirin and corticosteroids.

Limitations of Penicillin G

  1. It is acid labile and therefore is not effective orally.
  2. It has a short duration of action.
  3. It has a narrow spectrum of activity (Mainly Gram +).
  4. It is destroyed by penicillinase.
  5. It may produce anaphylaxis.

Semisynthetic Penicillins

Developed to overcome the limitations of penicillin G. They may be acid resistant, penicillinase resistant or extended spectrum.

Ampicillin vs Amoxicillin

TABLE: | Feature | Ampicillin | Amoxicillin | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | Absorption | Incomplete | Complete | | Effect of Food | Decreases absorption | Does not decrease | | Diarrhoea | More common | Less common | | Oral Contraceptives | Reduces effectiveness | Does not reduce | | Frequency | Four times daily | Three times daily |

Therapeutic Uses

  • 1. Streptococcal infections: treated with amoxicillin (Pharyngitis, sinusitis, otitis media).
  • 2. Rheumatic fever: Benzathine penicillin G is ideal for prophylaxis (1.2 million units IM monthly).
  • 3. Subacute bacterial endocarditis: Amoxicillin for prophylaxis; Ampicillin + Gentamicin for treatment.
  • 4. UTI: Ampicillin + Gentamicin for E. coli pyelonephritis.
  • 5. Meningitis: Ampicillin is effective against Listeria monocytogenes.
  • 6. Bacillary dysentery: Ampicillin used in some responsive cases.
  • 7. Typhoid fever: Ampicillin may be used for carrier state eradication.
  • 8. Syphilis: Best treated with Penicillin G. Drug of choice during pregnancy.
  • 9. Diphtheria: Penicillin G eliminates carrier state.
  • 10. Clostridial infections: Penicillin G used as adjunct in Gas Gangrene.
  • 11. Gonococcal infections: Previously DOC, now limited due to resistance.
  • 12. Other: Leptospirosis, anthrax, Lyme disease, actinomycosis.
  • 13. Anaerobic infections: trench mouth (combined with Metronidazole).
  • 14. H. pylori: Amoxicillin used in combination therapy.
  • 15. Serious Infections: Piperacillin or Ticarcillin for Pseudomonas.

Drug Interaction

FLOWCHART: Probenecid competes with penicillins for active tubular secretion -> Therefore probenecid retards their excretion -> Therefore plasma concentration increases -> Therefore duration of action increases -> Hence enhances therapeutic efficacy in endocarditis and gonococcal infections.
