LOCAL ANAESTHETICS

💉
Local anaesthetics are drugs which, when applied topically or injected locally, block nerve conduction and produce reversible loss of sensation in the area supplied by the nerve.

The order of loss of function is always predictable.

FLOWCHART: Loss of Function -> [Pain sensation disappears first | Temperature sensation disappears next | Touch sensation is lost | Pressure sensation disappears | Skeletal muscle power is lost last]
Mnemonic
"Painful Temperatures Touch Pressure Muscles"
  • P = Pain
  • T = Temperature
  • T = Touch
  • P = Pressure
  • M = Motor power

CHEMISTRY OF LOCAL ANAESTHETICS

Local anaesthetics are weak bases. Every local anaesthetic molecule has three parts.

TABLE: | Part of Molecule | Function | | Hydrophilic amine group | Makes the drug water soluble | | Lipophilic aromatic group | Helps penetration into nerve membrane | | Ester or amide linkage | Determines metabolism and allergy potential |
Mnemonic
"A Local Anaesthetic Has AEA"
  • A = Aromatic lipophilic group
  • E = Ester or amide linkage
  • A = Amine group

Mnemonic
"Esters have One i, Amides have Two i's"
  • Esters contain only one 'i' in their name
  • Examples: procaine, tetracaine, cocaine
  • Amides contain two 'i's in their name
  • Examples: lignocaine, prilocaine, bupivacaine

MECHANISM OF ACTION

Local anaesthetics act mainly on voltage-gated sodium channels.

At physiological pH, local anaesthetics exist partly in ionized form and partly in unionized form.

TABLE: | Form of Drug | Property | | Unionized form | Lipid soluble and can penetrate the nerve membrane | | Ionized form | Binds to and blocks sodium channels | FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic is injected near the nerve -> The unionized lipid-soluble form penetrates the nerve sheath and axon membrane -> Inside the axon, where the pH is lower, the drug becomes ionized again -> The ionized form binds to the sodium channel from inside -> Sodium entry into the neuron is prevented -> Depolarization cannot occur -> Action potential is not generated -> Nerve conduction stops -> Local anaesthesia occurs

The local anaesthetic binds more strongly to the open and inactivated states of the sodium channel.

FLOWCHART: A nerve fires repeatedly -> More sodium channels remain in the open or inactivated state -> Local anaesthetic binds more effectively -> Blockade becomes greater

This is called frequency-dependent block.

Mnemonic
"Unionized enters, Ionized inhibits"

EFFECT OF pH ON LOCAL ANAESTHETIC ACTION

The action of local anaesthetics depends strongly on tissue pH.

FLOWCHART: Tissue pH becomes acidic due to infection or inflammation -> More of the local anaesthetic remains in the ionized form -> The drug cannot cross the nerve membrane effectively -> Less drug reaches the sodium channel inside the neuron -> Local anaesthetic action becomes weak or fails TABLE: | Tissue Condition | Effect on Local Anaesthetic | | Alkaline pH | More unionized form is present and action is stronger | | Acidic pH | More ionized form is present and action is weaker |
Mnemonic
"Infected tissue Is Ionized and Ineffective"

ORDER OF BLOCKADE OF NERVE FIBRES

Local anaesthetics do not block all nerve fibres equally.

FLOWCHART: Nerve Fibre Blockade -> [Small nerve fibres are blocked first | Large nerve fibres are blocked later] -> [Myelinated fibres are blocked earlier than nonmyelinated fibres of the same size | Sensory fibres are blocked before motor fibres] TABLE: | Fibre Type | Time of Blockade | | Pain fibres | Earliest | | Temperature fibres | Early | | Touch and pressure fibres | Later | | Motor fibres | Last |

Fibres at the outer part of a nerve bundle are blocked before those in the centre.

FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic reaches the outer part of the nerve bundle first -> Peripheral fibres are blocked early -> Central fibres are blocked later

FACTORS AFFECTING LOCAL ANAESTHETIC ACTION

1. pKa of the Drug

The pKa of the drug determines how much of the drug is in the unionized form.

FLOWCHART: The pKa of the local anaesthetic is high -> Less unionized drug is present at physiological pH -> Penetration into the nerve is slower -> Onset of action becomes slow TABLE: | Drug | pKa | Onset | | Procaine | 9.1 | Slow | | Lignocaine | 7.7 | Rapid | | Chloroprocaine | 9.1 | Rapid despite high pKa |
Mnemonic
"Lower pKa = Less waiting"

2. Plasma Protein Binding

FLOWCHART: The drug binds strongly to plasma proteins -> The drug remains longer at the site of action -> Duration of action becomes longer TABLE: | Drug | Protein Binding and Duration | | Procaine | Poor protein binding and short duration | | Bupivacaine | High protein binding and long duration |

3. Lipid Solubility

FLOWCHART: The drug is highly lipid soluble -> It enters the nerve membrane more easily -> The potency of the drug increases TABLE: | Drug | Relative Potency | | Procaine | Less potent | | Lignocaine | More potent |

4. Concentration of Drug

FLOWCHART: A higher concentration of local anaesthetic is injected -> The concentration gradient becomes greater -> The drug diffuses more rapidly to the nerve -> The onset of action becomes faster

5. Presence of Vasoconstrictor

FLOWCHART: Adrenaline is added to the local anaesthetic -> Local blood flow decreases -> The local anaesthetic is absorbed more slowly into the circulation -> The drug remains longer at the site of action -> The duration of action increases

COMBINATION OF ADRENALINE WITH LOCAL ANAESTHETICS

Adding adrenaline to a local anaesthetic has important advantages.

TABLE: | Advantage | Explanation | | Prolongs duration of action | Slows absorption from the local site | | Decreases bleeding | Causes vasoconstriction in the surgical field | | Reduces systemic toxicity | Less drug reaches the circulation |
Mnemonic
"Adrenaline gives the 3 P's"
  • P = Prolonged action
  • P = Prevents bleeding
  • P = Prevents systemic toxicity

However, adrenaline should not be used in areas supplied by end arteries.

FLOWCHART: Adrenaline causes intense vasoconstriction -> Blood supply to tissues with end arteries decreases markedly -> Ischaemia develops -> Gangrene may occur TABLE: | Site Where Adrenaline Must Not Be Used | Reason | | Fingers | Risk of gangrene | | Toes | Risk of gangrene | | Penis | Risk of gangrene | | Ear lobule | Risk of gangrene | | Tip of nose | Risk of gangrene |
Mnemonic
"Never add Adrenaline to Fingers, Toes, Penis, Ear and Nose"
  • F = Fingers
  • T = Toes
  • P = Penis
  • E = Ear
  • N = Nose
TABLE: | Contraindication | Reason | | Hypertension | Adrenaline may raise blood pressure | | Ischaemic heart disease | May precipitate angina | | Arrhythmias | May worsen arrhythmia | | Congestive cardiac failure | May worsen cardiac function | | Uncontrolled hyperthyroidism | Adrenaline sensitivity is increased |

PHARMACOLOGICAL ACTIONS

On Peripheral Nerves

FLOWCHART: Autonomic fibres are blocked first -> Pain sensation disappears -> Temperature sensation is lost -> Touch and pressure are blocked -> Motor function is lost last

On Central Nervous System

Most local anaesthetics cross the blood-brain barrier.

FLOWCHART: A high concentration of local anaesthetic enters the CNS -> [Initial stimulation occurs | Further increase in concentration causes CNS depression] -> [Restlessness, tremor and convulsions appear | Respiratory depression, coma and death may occur] TABLE: | CNS Effect | Manifestation | | Early stimulation | Excitement, tremor, restlessness, convulsions | | Later depression | Drowsiness, respiratory depression, coma |
Mnemonic
"Local Anaesthetic CNS Toxicity first Excites, then Ends"

On Cardiovascular System

FLOWCHART: Local anaesthetic blocks sodium channels in the heart -> Automaticity and conduction decrease -> Heart rate and contractility decrease -> Cardiac output decreases

Bupivacaine is especially dangerous because it is highly cardiotoxic.

FLOWCHART: Bupivacaine blocks cardiac sodium channels strongly -> Severe arrhythmias may occur -> Cardiovascular collapse and death may occur
Mnemonic
"Bupivacaine Breaks the Beating heart"

Lignocaine is unique because it is useful in ventricular arrhythmias.

FLOWCHART: Most local anaesthetics cause vasodilatation -> Peripheral vascular resistance decreases -> Blood pressure falls

PHARMACOKINETICS

TABLE: | Ester Local Anaesthetics | Amide Local Anaesthetics | | Metabolized rapidly by plasma cholinesterase | Metabolized mainly in the liver | | Shorter duration of action | Usually longer duration | | More allergic reactions | Less allergic reactions |
Mnemonic
"Ester = Enzyme in plasma"
Mnemonic
"Amide = Acts in liver"

ADVERSE EFFECTS

TABLE: | System | Adverse Effects | | CNS | Restlessness, tremor, headache, confusion, convulsions, respiratory depression | | CVS | Hypotension, bradycardia, arrhythmia, cardiovascular collapse | | Allergy | Rash, itching, urticaria, bronchospasm, anaphylaxis | | Others | Methaemoglobinaemia with prilocaine, mucosal irritation with cocaine |

Allergic reactions are more common with ester local anaesthetics.

FLOWCHART: Ester local anaesthetic is metabolized -> Para-aminobenzoic acid derivatives are formed -> Allergic reaction becomes more likely

COMPARISON OF PROCAINE AND LIGNOCAINE

TABLE: | Feature | Procaine | Lignocaine | | Type | Ester | Amide | | Duration | Short acting | Intermediate acting | | Tissue penetration | Poor | Good | | Onset | Slow | Rapid | | Metabolism | Plasma cholinesterase | Liver | | Allergy | Common | Rare | | Use | Infiltration and nerve block; rarely used now | Used for all types of anaesthesia |
Mnemonic
"Procaine is Poor and Primitive, Lignocaine is Leading and Loved"

IMPORTANT LOCAL ANAESTHETICS

TABLE: | Drug | Important Feature | | Cocaine | Excellent surface anaesthetic; causes euphoria, tachycardia and addiction | | Tetracaine | Long acting; useful in spinal and corneal anaesthesia | | Lignocaine | Most widely used local anaesthetic; also used in ventricular arrhythmias | | Bupivacaine | Long acting and highly cardiotoxic | | Levobupivacaine | Similar to bupivacaine but less cardiotoxic | | Ropivacaine | Less cardiotoxic and more sensory selective | | Prilocaine | Can cause methaemoglobinaemia | | Dibucaine | Most potent and longest acting but highly toxic | | Benzocaine | Surface anaesthetic with minimal systemic toxicity | | Oxethazaine | Used for gastric mucosa in gastritis | | Proparacaine | Used for corneal anaesthesia |

SPECIAL DRUGS

BUPIVACAINE

FLOWCHART: Bupivacaine produces greater sensory than motor blockade -> Pain sensation is lost but motor function is relatively preserved -> The drug becomes useful in obstetric analgesia

However, bupivacaine is highly cardiotoxic.

Mnemonic
"BUPI is Best for Baby pain, but Bad for the heart"

ROPIVACAINE

FLOWCHART: Ropivacaine blocks sensory fibres more than motor fibres -> Pain relief occurs with less motor paralysis -> The drug is useful in labour analgesia
Mnemonic
"ROPI Relieves pain Only, Preserves movement"

PRILOCAINE

Prilocaine can cause methaemoglobinaemia.

Mnemonic
"PRIloCAINE Produces Rust-colored blood"

EMLA

EMLA is a eutectic mixture of lignocaine 2.5% and prilocaine 2.5%.

TABLE: | Composition of EMLA | Use | | Lignocaine 2.5% + Prilocaine 2.5% | Dermal anaesthesia before venesection or skin grafting | FLOWCHART: EMLA cream is applied to intact skin -> The mixture penetrates the skin gradually -> Dermal anaesthesia develops after about one hour TABLE: | Important Precaution with EMLA | | Should not be applied on mucous membranes | | Should not be used on abraded skin | | Contraindicated in infants and in methaemoglobinaemia |
Mnemonic
"EMLA = Easy Masking of Local Ache"

SURFACE ANAESTHETICS

TABLE: | Drug | Main Use | | Benzocaine and Butylaminobenzoate | Haemorrhoids, anal fissure, sore throat | | Oxethazaine | Gastritis and gastric pain | | Dyclonine | Oral mucositis due to chemotherapy or radiation | | Proparacaine | Corneal anaesthesia, tonometry, foreign body removal | FLOWCHART: Oxethazaine is taken with antacids -> The gastric mucosa becomes anaesthetized -> Pain due to gastritis decreases

HIGH-YIELD MNEMONICS FOR LOCAL ANAESTHETICS

"Local Anaesthetic order of block = PTTPM"
  • P = Pain
  • T = Temperature
  • T = Touch
  • P = Pressure
  • M = Motor function
"Esters are One-eyed, Amides wear Spectacles"
  • Esters have one 'i' in the name
  • Examples: cocaine, procaine, tetracaine
  • Amides have two 'i's in the name
  • Examples: lignocaine, prilocaine, bupivacaine
"LIP before LIVE"

Local anaesthetic must be Lipid-soluble first

Then it enters the axon and becomes Ionized

Then it Prevents sodium entry

"Unionized Unlocks the nerve, Ionized Inhibits the channel"
"Acidic area = Anaesthesia Absent"

In infected or inflamed tissue the pH is low

More drug becomes ionized

Less drug enters the nerve

Local anaesthetic fails

"Small fibres Surrender Sooner"

Small fibres are blocked before large fibres

Sensory fibres are blocked before motor fibres

"Adrenaline should not go to Five Fingers"
  • F = Fingers
  • F = Feet (toes)
  • F = Flap of ear
  • F = Face tip of nose
  • F = Phallus (penis)
"Bupivacaine is BUPI-dangerous"
  • B = Bradycardia and arrhythmia
  • U = Unstable circulation
  • P = Potent cardiotoxicity
  • I = Increased risk of cardiovascular collapse
"PRILOCAINE makes the Patient Pale"

Prilocaine can produce methaemoglobinaemia

Blood becomes chocolate-brown

Patient appears pale or cyanosed

"LIGNOCAINE = Local anaesthetic and Irregular beats Gone"

Lignocaine is the most commonly used local anaesthetic

It is also useful in ventricular arrhythmias

"COCAINE Creates Confidence and Constriction"

Cocaine causes:

FLOWCHART: Cocaine -> [Euphoria | Tachycardia | Rise in blood pressure | Addiction]
"ROPI Runs with labour"

Ropivacaine gives sensory block more than motor block

Therefore it is useful in labour analgesia

"EMLA = Easy Mixture for Local Anaesthesia"
  • E = Eutectic mixture
