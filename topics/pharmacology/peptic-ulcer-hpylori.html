ULCER PROTECTIVES

Ulcer protectives are drugs that protect the ulcer base and gastric mucosa from acid-pepsin injury without markedly reducing acid secretion.
  • Main ulcer protective drugs include:
    • Sucralfate
    • Colloidal bismuth subcitrate (CBS)

SUCRALFATE

INTRODUCTION

  • Sucralfate is a complex of:
    • Aluminium hydroxide
    • Sulphated sucrose
  • It acts mainly as a mucosal protective agent.

MECHANISM OF ACTION

FLOWCHART: Sucralfate enters acidic stomach environment (pH < 4) -> Polymerization occurs -> Formation of sticky viscous polymer -> Adheres firmly to ulcer base -> Protective barrier formed against acid and pepsin

CYTOPROTECTIVE ACTIONS

FLOWCHART: Sucralfate -> ↑ Prostaglandin release -> ↑ Epidermal growth factor release -> ↑ Mucus secretion -> ↑ Bicarbonate secretion -> Enhanced mucosal defense and repair

IMPORTANT PHARMACOLOGICAL FEATURES

  • Requires acidic pH (<4) for activation
  • Acts locally over ulcer base
  • Produces mucosal protection
  • Does not significantly inhibit acid secretion

ADMINISTRATION

  • Given orally
  • Taken on empty stomach
  • Administered at least 1 hour before meals

DRUG INTERACTIONS

FLOWCHART: Sucralfate forms protective coating in stomach -> ↓ Drug absorption

DRUGS WHOSE ABSORPTION IS REDUCED

  • Digoxin
  • Tetracyclines
  • Ketoconazole
  • Fluoroquinolones

IMPORTANT CLINICAL POINT

FLOWCHART: Sucralfate requires acidic pH for activation -> Concurrent use of: Antacids H2 blockers PPIs -> Should be avoided

THERAPEUTIC USES

  • Peptic ulcer
  • Prevention of bleeding from stress ulcers
  • Reduction of aspiration pneumonia risk
  • GERD with oesophagitis
  • Oral mucositis
  • Radiation proctitis
  • Rectal ulcers
  • Burns
  • Bed sores

ADVERSE EFFECTS

  • Constipation (most common)
  • Nausea
  • Aluminium toxicity in renal failure patients

CURRENT STATUS

FLOWCHART: Introduction of PPIs -> Marked reduction in use of sucralfate

COLLOIDAL BISMUTH SUBCITRATE (CBS)

INTRODUCTION

  • Colloidal bismuth subcitrate is a mucosal protective agent with additional anti-H. pylori activity.

MECHANISM OF ACTION

FLOWCHART: CBS -> Precipitates proteins over ulcer base -> Protective coating formed FLOWCHART: CBS -> ↑ PGE2 secretion -> ↑ Mucus secretion -> ↑ Bicarbonate secretion -> Enhanced mucosal protection FLOWCHART: CBS -> Direct antimicrobial effect -> Action against H. pylori

THERAPEUTIC USES

  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Part of anti-H. pylori regimens

ADVERSE EFFECTS

  • Black discoloration of tongue
  • Black stools
  • These effects may reduce patient compliance.

ANTI–H. PYLORI AGENTS

INTRODUCTION

  • Helicobacter pylori is a:
    • Gram-negative
    • Rod-shaped bacterium
  • It is associated with:
    • Gastritis
    • Duodenal ulcer
    • Gastric ulcer
    • Gastric carcinoma

PATHOGENESIS

FLOWCHART: H. pylori produces urease -> Urea converted into ammonia (NH3) -> Direct mucosal injury and inflammation

PRINCIPLES OF H. PYLORI THERAPY

  • Combination therapy is always recommended.

OBJECTIVES OF COMBINATION THERAPY

FLOWCHART: Combination therapy -> ↓ Drug resistance -> ↓ Relapse -> Rapid ulcer healing -> Eradication of H. pylori

IMPORTANT POINTS

  • Triple or quadruple therapy is used
  • Duration may be 1–2 weeks
  • 14-day therapy is most effective

COMMON ANTI-H. PYLORI DRUGS

  • Amoxicillin
  • Tetracycline
  • Clarithromycin
  • Metronidazole
  • Tinidazole
  • PPIs
  • H₂ blockers
  • CBS

IMPORTANT RESISTANCE PATTERN

  • Resistance develops rapidly to:
    • Metronidazole
    • Clarithromycin
  • Resistance develops slowly to:
    • Amoxicillin

IMPORTANT PRECAUTION

  • Avoid amoxicillin in penicillin-allergic patients

TRIPLE THERAPY (LAC REGIMEN)

DURATION

  • 14 days

COMPONENTS

  • Lansoprazole 30 mg twice daily
  • Clarithromycin 500 mg twice daily
  • Amoxicillin 1 g twice daily

QUADRUPLE THERAPY

DURATION

  • 14 days

COMPONENTS

  • Omeprazole 20 mg twice daily
  • CBS 120 mg four times daily
  • Tetracycline 500 mg four times daily
  • Metronidazole 400 mg three times daily

AFTER COMPLETION OF THERAPY

FLOWCHART: Completion of eradication regimen -> Continue PPI therapy -> Enhanced ulcer healing

DRUGS USED IN GASTROESOPHAGEAL REFLUX DISEASE (GERD)

INTRODUCTION

GERD is a condition in which gastric contents reflux into the oesophagus.

PATHOGENESIS

FLOWCHART: Reflux of acidic gastric contents -> Irritation of oesophageal mucosa -> Oesophagitis and ulceration

COMPLICATIONS

  • Oesophagitis
  • Oesophageal ulcers
  • Oesophageal carcinoma

SYMPTOMS

  • Retrosternal burning sensation
  • Regurgitation
  • Chest pain
  • Cough
  • Dysphagia (long-standing disease)

LIFESTYLE AND DIETARY MODIFICATIONS

  • Weight reduction
  • Elevation of head end during sleep
  • Avoid spicy foods
  • Avoid late-night meals
  • Avoid caffeine

DRUGS USED IN GERD

1. PROTON PUMP INHIBITORS (PPIs)

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: PPIs -> ↓ Gastric acid secretion -> ↑ Gastric pH -> Symptom relief + healing of oesophageal lesions

IMPORTANT FEATURES

  • Drug of choice in GERD
  • More effective than H₂ blockers
  • Heal erosive oesophagitis
  • Do not affect LES tone

2. H₂ RECEPTOR BLOCKERS

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: H2 blockers -> ↓ Acid secretion -> Reduced acid exposure to oesophagus

ROLE

  • Effective in GERD
  • Less effective than PPIs

3. ANTACIDS

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Antacids neutralize gastric acid -> ↓ Acid exposure to oesophagus -> Rapid symptom relief

ROLE

  • Used occasionally
  • Rapid acting

4. PROKINETIC DRUGS

DRUGS

  • Metoclopramide
  • Mosapride

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Prokinetic drugs -> ↑ Lower oesophageal sphincter (LES) tone -> ↑ Gastric emptying -> Reduced reflux

IMPORTANT POINT

  • Do not reduce acid secretion

5. SODIUM ALGINATE

MECHANISM

FLOWCHART: Sodium alginate -> Forms froth over gastric contents -> Prevents gastroesophageal reflux
