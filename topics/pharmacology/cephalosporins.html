Cephalosporins are B-lactam antibiotics containing a 7-aminocephalosporanic acid nucleus because they were originally obtained from the fungus Cephalosporium acremonium. Later, semisynthetic cephalosporins were developed. Like penicillins, cephalosporins inhibit synthesis of the bacterial cell wall and therefore produce a bactericidal effect.

Mechanism of Action

FLOWCHART: Cephalosporin -> Acts as a structural analogue of D-alanine -> Binds to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) -> Inhibits transpeptidase enzyme -> Prevents cross-linking of peptidoglycan strands -> Inhibits synthesis of the bacterial cell wall -> Produces cell wall-deficient bacteria -> Bacteria undergo lysis -> Therefore cephalosporins produce a bactericidal effect.

The mechanism of development of bacterial resistance is similar to that of penicillins because bacteria may produce B-lactamases, alter their PBPs or reduce the ability of the drug to reach its site of action.

Generations of Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: Cephalosporins -> Divided into five generations -> Each successive generation generally shows greater activity against gram-negative organisms and greater resistance to B-lactamases.

1. GENERATIONS OVERVIEW

  • Cephalosporins are divided into generations based on antibacterial spectrum, β-lactamase resistance and pharmacokinetic properties.
  • With increasing generation, gram-negative activity increases and CNS penetration improves.

2. ANTIBACTERIAL SPECTRUM

TABLE: | Feature | FIRST GENERATION | SECOND GENERATION | THIRD GENERATION | FOURTH GENERATION | | Gram-positive organisms (except enterococci and MRSA) | +++ | ++ | + | + | | Gram-negative organisms | + (E. coli, K. pneumoniae) | ++ (E. coli, K. pneumoniae, Proteus, H. influenzae) | +++ | +++ | | Anaerobes | Effective against oral cavity anaerobes except B. fragilis | Effective against some anaerobes (cefuroxime, cefaclor) | Effective against anaerobes including B. fragilis (cefoperazone, ceftizoxime) | Not effective against B. fragilis | | Pseudomonas | Not effective | Not effective | Effective (cefoperazone, ceftazidime) | Effective (cefepime) | | Salmonella | Not effective | Not effective | Effective (ceftriaxone, cefoperazone) | Not effective |

3. DRUGS

TABLE: | Generation | Oral Drugs | Parenteral Drugs | | FIRST | Cephalexin, Cefadroxil | Cefazolin (i.m., i.v.) | | SECOND | Cefaclor, Cefuroxime axetil, Cefprozil | Cefuroxime (i.m., i.v.), Cefoxitin | | THIRD | Cefixime, Cefpodoxime proxetil, Cefdinir, Ceftibuten, Ceftamet pivoxil | Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Cefoperazone, Ceftazidime, Ceftizoxime | | FOURTH | — | Cefepime (i.v.), Cefpirome |

4. β-LACTAMASE

  • Among the first-generation agents, cefazolin is highly susceptible to staphylococcal β-lactamases
  • Cefuroxime is resistant to β-lactamases produced by gram-negative organisms
  • Most third-generation cephalosporins are resistant to most β-lactamases (except cefoperazone) produced by gram-negative organisms
  • Fourth-generation cephalosporins show similar resistance pattern as third generation

5. BLOOD–BRAIN BARRIER

  • First generation → Do not cross BBB
  • Second generation → Some drugs (cefuroxime) cross BBB
  • Third generation → Cefotaxime, Ceftriaxone → Cross BBB → Reach high concentration in CSF
  • Fourth generation → Cross BBB

6. USES

FIRST GENERATION

  • Skin and soft tissue infections due to streptococci and Staphylococcus aureus
  • Surgical prophylaxis
  • Cefazolin is preferred because of its longer duration of action and produces high concentration in blood and bile

SECOND GENERATION

  • Respiratory tract infections
  • Otitis media and sinusitis
  • Oral cefuroxime axetil can be used
  • Cefuroxime is useful for urinary and soft tissue infections due to gram-negative bacteria

THIRD GENERATION

TABLE: | Condition / Infection | Drug / Detail | | Severe gram-negative infections | Used alone or with aminoglycosides | | Pyelonephritis (gram-negative) | Ceftriaxone | | Community-acquired pneumonia | Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime (parenteral), Cefpodoxime (oral) | | Gonorrhoea | Ceftriaxone is drug of choice (250 mg IM single dose) | | Typhoid fever | Ceftriaxone, Cefoperazone (multidrug-resistant Salmonella) | | Meningitis (meningococci, H. influenzae) | Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime | | Mixed aerobic + anaerobic infections (malignancy patients) | Third generation cephalosporins | | Septicaemia (gram-negative) | Third generation cephalosporins | | Nosocomial infections | Third generation drugs useful | | Syphilis | Ceftriaxone is alternative drug |

FOURTH GENERATION

  • Same uses as third generation
  • Cross BBB
  • Used as reserve drugs for hospital-acquired resistant infections
  • Effective against Pseudomonas (cefepime)

PHARMACOKINETICS

  • Cephalosporins are administered either orally or parenterally.

Administration

FLOWCHART: Pharmacokinetics of Cephalosporins -> Administration -> Oral or Parenteral routes

Distribution and Metabolism

FLOWCHART: Distribution and metabolism -> Some cephalosporins are metabolized before excretion -> Cefotaxime -> Deacetylated in the body before excretion

Excretion

FLOWCHART: Excretion -> Mainly excreted unchanged through kidney -> By glomerular filtration or tubular secretion

Exception

FLOWCHART: Exception -> Cefoperazone -> Excreted mainly through bile

Drug Interaction

FLOWCHART: Drug interaction -> Probenecid blocks active tubular secretion -> Therefore blood levels increase -> Duration of action prolongs

ADVERSE EFFECTS OF CEPHALOSPORINS

Cephalosporins are generally safe antibiotics, but they can produce adverse effects involving:
  • Skin
  • GIT
  • Kidneys
  • Blood coagulation system
  • Injection sites
  • Many adverse effects occur because:
    • Cephalosporins alter normal flora
    • Some drugs directly irritate tissues
    • Some share structural similarity with penicillins

1. HYPERSENSITIVITY REACTIONS

  • This is the:
    • Most common adverse effect

MANIFESTATIONS INCLUDE

  • Skin rashes
  • Urticaria
  • Rarely anaphylaxis

WHY HYPERSENSITIVITY OCCURS

FLOWCHART: Cephalosporin enters body -> Immune system recognizes drug as foreign -> Allergic reaction develops -> Skin rash / urticaria / anaphylaxis may occur

CROSS-REACTIVITY WITH PENICILLINS

  • Cephalosporins and penicillins share:
    • Similar β-lactam ring structure
  • So patients allergic to penicillin may also react to cephalosporins.
FLOWCHART: Previous penicillin allergy -> Immune system already sensitized to β-lactam structure -> Cephalosporin administered -> Cross-reactive allergic response may occur
Cephalosporins should be avoided in patients with:
  • History of severe penicillin hypersensitivity
Reason:

Severe allergic reaction or anaphylaxis may occur.

2. GASTROINTESTINAL DISTURBANCES

COMMON SYMPTOMS

  • Diarrhoea
  • Vomiting
  • Anorexia

WHY GIT SIDE EFFECTS OCCUR

  • Normally intestine contains:
    • Protective normal bacterial flora
  • Cephalosporins disturb this flora.
FLOWCHART: Cephalosporins kill normal intestinal bacteria -> Intestinal microbial balance disturbed -> GIT irritation develops -> Diarrhoea and vomiting occur

3. PAIN AT INTRAMUSCULAR INJECTION SITE

  • Seen especially with:
    • Cephalothin

WHY PAIN OCCURS

FLOWCHART: Intramuscular cephalothin injection -> Local tissue irritation occurs -> Pain develops at injection site

4. THROMBOPHLEBITIS WITH IV ADMINISTRATION

  • Intravenous cephalosporins may irritate veins.
  • This can lead to:
    • Thrombophlebitis

HOW THROMBOPHLEBITIS OCCURS

FLOWCHART: IV cephalosporin administered -> Venous wall irritation occurs -> Inflammation of vein develops -> Thrombophlebitis occurs

5. NEPHROTOXICITY

  • Seen especially with:
    • Cephaloridine
  • This drug can:
    • Directly damage kidneys
  • Because of severe nephrotoxicity:
    • Cephaloridine has been withdrawn

MECHANISM OF NEPHROTOXICITY

FLOWCHART: Cephaloridine reaches kidney -> Direct tubular toxicity occurs -> Renal damage develops -> Nephrotoxicity occurs

IMPORTANT DRUG INTERACTION

  • Cephalothin + Gentamicin
  • Both drugs may damage kidneys.
  • So combined use increases risk.
FLOWCHART: Cephalothin + gentamicin used together -> Combined renal toxicity increases -> Risk of nephrotoxicity rises markedly

6. DISULFIRAM-LIKE REACTION

  • Seen with:
    • Cefotetan
    • Cefoperazone
  • Patients develop alcohol intolerance.

WHY THIS REACTION OCCURS

  • Normally alcohol metabolism requires:
    • Aldehyde dehydrogenase
  • These cephalosporins interfere with alcohol metabolism.
FLOWCHART: Alcohol consumed during therapy -> Alcohol metabolism blocked -> Acetaldehyde accumulates -> Disulfiram-like reaction develops

SYMPTOMS OF DISULFIRAM-LIKE REACTION

  • Flushing
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Tachycardia
  • Hypotension

7. BLEEDING COMPLICATIONS

  • Severe bleeding may occur because cephalosporins can cause:
    • Hypoprothrombinaemia
    • Thrombocytopenia
    • Platelet dysfunction
  • Risk is higher in:
    • Renal failure patients

HOW BLEEDING OCCURS

FLOWCHART: Cephalosporins interfere with coagulation -> Clotting ability decreases -> Bleeding tendency increases

HYPOPROTHROMBINAEMIA

FLOWCHART: Prothrombin level decreases -> Blood clot formation impaired -> Bleeding risk increases

TREATMENT OF HYPOPROTHROMBINAEMIA

  • Vitamin K is useful because:
    • It restores clotting factor synthesis
FLOWCHART: Vitamin K administered -> Coagulation factor synthesis improves -> Bleeding tendency decreases
