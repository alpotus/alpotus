Cephalosporins are B-lactam antibiotics containing a 7-aminocephalosporanic acid nucleus because they were originally obtained from the fungus Cephalosporium acremonium. Later, semisynthetic cephalosporins were developed. Like penicillins, cephalosporins inhibit synthesis of the bacterial cell wall and therefore produce a bactericidal effect.

Mechanism of Action

FLOWCHART: Cephalosporin -> Acts as a structural analogue of D-alanine -> Binds to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) -> Inhibits transpeptidase enzyme -> Prevents cross-linking of peptidoglycan strands -> Inhibits synthesis of the bacterial cell wall -> Produces cell wall-deficient bacteria -> Bacteria undergo lysis -> Therefore cephalosporins produce a bactericidal effect.

The mechanism of development of bacterial resistance is similar to that of penicillins because bacteria may produce B-lactamases, alter their PBPs or reduce the ability of the drug to reach its site of action.

Generations of Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: Cephalosporins -> Divided into five generations -> Each successive generation generally shows greater activity against gram-negative organisms and greater resistance to B-lactamases.

First-Generation Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: First-Generation Cephalosporins -> Cephalexin + Cefadroxil + Cefazolin
  • First-generation cephalosporins are mainly active against gram-positive organisms because they are especially effective against streptococci and methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus.
  • They are only weakly active against gram-negative organisms such as Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae because their spectrum against gram-negative bacteria is limited.
  • They are not effective against enterococci and MRSA because these organisms possess resistance mechanisms.
  • They are not effective against anaerobes, except for some activity against oral cavity anaerobes excluding Bacteroides fragilis.
  • They are not effective against Pseudomonas because they do not possess antipseudomonal activity.
  • Among the first-generation drugs, cefazolin is highly susceptible to staphylococcal B-lactamases because it is easily destroyed by these enzymes.
  • First-generation cephalosporins do not cross the blood-brain barrier because they penetrate poorly into the cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Cephalexin and cefadroxil are useful in skin and soft-tissue infections because they are effective against streptococci and Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Cefazolin is preferred for surgical prophylaxis because it has a longer duration of action and produces high concentrations in blood and bile.

Second-Generation Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: Second-Generation Cephalosporins -> Cefaclor + Cefuroxime axetil + Cefuroxime + Cefprozil
  • Second-generation cephalosporins possess greater gram-negative activity because they act against Escherichia coli, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Proteus and Haemophilus influenzae.
  • They retain moderate activity against gram-positive organisms, although they are less active than first-generation cephalosporins.
  • Some second-generation drugs such as cefuroxime and cefaclor are effective against certain anaerobes because they act against oral cavity anaerobes.
  • They are not effective against Bacteroides fragilis because this organism remains resistant.
  • Cefuroxime is resistant to B-lactamases produced by gram-negative organisms because its structure protects it from enzymatic destruction.
  • Some second-generation drugs, particularly cefuroxime, cross the blood-brain barrier because they can penetrate into the cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Oral cefuroxime axetil is useful in respiratory tract infections such as otitis media and sinusitis because it acts against common respiratory pathogens.
  • Cefuroxime is also useful in urinary tract infections and soft-tissue infections due to gram-negative bacteria because of its broader spectrum.

Third-Generation Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: Third-Generation Cephalosporins -> Cefixime + Cefpodoxime proxetil + Ceftriaxone + Cefotaxime + Cefoperazone + Ceftazidime + Ceftizoxime + Cefdinir + Ceftibuten + Ceftamet pivoxil
  • Third-generation cephalosporins possess marked gram-negative activity because they are highly effective against Enterobacteriaceae and other resistant gram-negative organisms.
  • They retain less activity against gram-positive organisms than first-generation drugs, although they remain effective against many streptococci.
  • Most third-generation cephalosporins are resistant to most B-lactamases produced by gram-negative organisms, although cefoperazone is less resistant.
  • Cefotaxime and ceftriaxone cross the blood-brain barrier because they reach high concentrations in cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Cefoperazone and ceftizoxime are effective against anaerobes including Bacteroides fragilis because they possess significant anaerobic activity.
  • Ceftazidime and cefoperazone are effective against Pseudomonas aeruginosa because they possess antipseudomonal activity.
  • Ceftriaxone and cefoperazone are effective against Salmonella because they are useful in multidrug-resistant typhoid fever.
  • Third-generation cephalosporins alone or combined with aminoglycosides are used in severe gram-negative infections because they possess strong activity against resistant organisms.
  • Ceftriaxone is useful in pyelonephritis caused by gram-negative organisms because it produces high blood and urinary concentrations.
  • Ceftriaxone, cefotaxime and oral cefpodoxime are useful in community-acquired pneumonia because they are highly active against common respiratory pathogens.
  • Ceftriaxone is the drug of choice for gonorrhoea because a single intramuscular dose of 250 mg is highly effective.
  • Ceftriaxone and cefoperazone are useful in typhoid fever because they are effective against multidrug-resistant Salmonella infections.
  • Ceftriaxone and cefotaxime are the preferred drugs for meningitis caused by meningococci and Haemophilus influenzae because they cross the blood-brain barrier and reach high concentrations in cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Cefoperazone and ceftizoxime are useful in mixed aerobic and anaerobic infections because they are effective against Bacteroides fragilis.
  • Third-generation cephalosporins are useful in septicaemia and nosocomial gram-negative infections because of their broad gram-negative activity.

Fourth-Generation Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: Fourth-Generation Cephalosporins -> Cefepime + Cefpirome
  • Fourth-generation cephalosporins possess very broad-spectrum activity because they are effective against both gram-positive and gram-negative organisms.
  • They are highly resistant to B-lactamases because their structure protects them from enzymatic destruction.
  • Cefepime is effective against Pseudomonas because it possesses strong antipseudomonal activity.
  • Fourth-generation cephalosporins cross the blood-brain barrier because they penetrate into the cerebrospinal fluid.
  • They have therapeutic uses similar to those of third-generation cephalosporins, but they are reserve drugs because they are mainly used for hospital-acquired resistant infections.

Fifth-Generation Cephalosporins

FLOWCHART: Fifth-Generation Cephalosporins -> Ceftaroline fosamil + Ceftobiprole medocaril
  • Ceftaroline fosamil and ceftobiprole medocaril are given intravenously because both drugs are prodrugs requiring activation in the body.
  • Unlike earlier cephalosporins, these drugs are effective against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) because they retain activity even when resistance has developed due to alteration in penicillin-binding proteins.
  • They are indicated for complicated skin and soft-tissue infections, hospital-acquired pneumonia and community-acquired pneumonia caused by resistant organisms.

Pharmacokinetics

  • Cephalosporins are administered either orally or parenterally because different drugs in the group vary in their absorption and route of administration.
FLOWCHART: Most cephalosporins -> Excreted unchanged through the kidney -> Excretion occurs by glomerular filtration or tubular secretion -> Therefore dose modification is required in renal impairment.
  • Some cephalosporins undergo metabolism before excretion because cefotaxime is deacetylated in the body before it is eliminated.
  • Cefoperazone is unusual because it is excreted mainly through bile rather than through the kidney.
  • Ceftriaxone is eliminated by both urine and bile because it undergoes dual routes of excretion.
FLOWCHART: Probenecid -> Blocks active tubular secretion of cephalosporins -> Blood level of cephalosporins increases -> Duration of action becomes longer.

Important Pharmacokinetic Features

  • First-generation cephalosporins do not cross the blood-brain barrier because they penetrate poorly into the cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Cefuroxime, ceftriaxone and cefotaxime are useful in central nervous system infections because they penetrate into the cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Cefotaxime and ceftriaxone reach high concentrations in cerebrospinal fluid because they cross the blood-brain barrier effectively.
  • Fourth-generation cephalosporins also cross the blood-brain barrier because they possess excellent penetration into the central nervous system.

Antibacterial Spectrum

TABLE: | Generation | Main Activity | Important Organisms | | First-generation | More active against gram-positive organisms | Streptococci + Methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus + Weak activity against Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae | | Second-generation | Greater gram-negative activity | Escherichia coli + Klebsiella pneumoniae + Proteus + Haemophilus influenzae + Oral cavity anaerobes | | Third-generation | Marked activity against gram-negative organisms | Enterobacteriaceae + Resistant gram-negative organisms + Bacteroides fragilis | | Fourth-generation | Broad-spectrum with strong gram-negative activity | Pseudomonas + Gram-positive organisms + Gram-negative organisms | | Fifth-generation | Active against resistant gram-positive organisms | MRSA |

Ceftazidime and cefoperazone are particularly useful against Pseudomonas aeruginosa because they possess specific antipseudomonal activity.

Fourth-generation cephalosporins such as cefepime are also effective against Pseudomonas because they combine strong gram-negative activity with high resistance to B-lactamases.

Ceftriaxone and cefoperazone are useful against Salmonella because they are effective in multidrug-resistant typhoid fever.

B-Lactamase Resistance

  • Among first-generation cephalosporins, cefazolin is highly susceptible to staphylococcal B-lactamases because it is easily hydrolyzed by these enzymes.
  • Cefuroxime is resistant to B-lactamases produced by gram-negative organisms because its structure protects it from enzymatic destruction.
  • Most third-generation cephalosporins are resistant to most gram-negative B-lactamases, although cefoperazone is less resistant.
  • Fifth-generation cephalosporins are unique because they remain active against bacteria that have developed resistance due to altered penicillin-binding proteins.

Therapeutic Uses

  • First-generation cephalosporins are useful in skin and soft-tissue infections because they are effective against streptococci and Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Cefazolin is preferred for surgical prophylaxis because it has a longer duration of action and produces high concentrations in blood and bile.
  • Oral cefuroxime axetil is useful in respiratory tract infections such as otitis media and sinusitis because it acts against common respiratory pathogens.
  • Cefuroxime is also useful in urinary tract and soft-tissue infections caused by gram-negative organisms because of its broader spectrum.
  • Third-generation cephalosporins alone or combined with aminoglycosides are useful in severe gram-negative infections because they possess strong activity against resistant gram-negative bacteria.
  • Ceftriaxone is preferred in pyelonephritis caused by gram-negative organisms because it produces high blood and urinary concentrations.
  • Ceftriaxone, cefotaxime and oral cefpodoxime are useful in community-acquired pneumonia because they are highly active against common respiratory pathogens.
  • Ceftriaxone is the drug of choice for gonorrhoea because a single intramuscular dose of 250 mg is highly effective.
  • Ceftriaxone and cefoperazone are useful in typhoid fever because they are effective against multidrug-resistant Salmonella infections.
  • Ceftriaxone and cefotaxime are preferred drugs for meningitis caused by meningococci and Haemophilus influenzae because they cross the blood-brain barrier and reach high concentrations in cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Cefoperazone and ceftizoxime are useful in mixed aerobic and anaerobic infections because they are effective against Bacteroides fragilis.
  • Third-generation cephalosporins are useful in septicaemia and nosocomial gram-negative infections because of their broad gram-negative activity.
  • Fourth- and fifth-generation cephalosporins are reserve drugs for hospital-acquired resistant infections because they are effective against multidrug-resistant organisms.
  • Cefadroxil is useful in streptococcal pharyngitis and tonsillitis because it is highly active against streptococci.

Adverse Effects

  • Hypersensitivity reactions are the most common adverse effects because cephalosporins may produce skin rashes, urticaria and rarely anaphylaxis.
  • Cross-reactivity with penicillin occurs in some patients because cephalosporins and penicillins share a similar B-lactam structure.
  • Gastrointestinal disturbances such as diarrhoea, vomiting and anorexia may occur because cephalosporins alter normal intestinal flora.
  • Pain may occur at the site of intramuscular injection, especially with cephalothin, because the injection is locally irritating.
  • Intravenous cephalosporins may produce thrombophlebitis because they irritate the vein.
  • Nephrotoxicity may occur, especially with cephaloridine, because this drug can directly damage the kidney and therefore has been withdrawn.
  • The combination of cephalothin and gentamicin increases the risk of nephrotoxicity because both drugs may damage the kidney.
  • Cefotetan and cefoperazone may produce intolerance to alcohol because they cause a disulfiram-like reaction.
  • Severe bleeding may occur because cephalosporins can produce hypoprothrombinaemia, thrombocytopenia or platelet dysfunction, particularly in patients with renal failure.
  • Hypoprothrombinaemia responds to vitamin K therapy because vitamin K corrects the coagulation defect.
⚠️
Cephalosporins should be avoided in patients with a history of hypersensitivity to penicillins because cross-reactivity may precipitate a severe allergic reaction.

Key Points

  • Cephalosporins produce a bactericidal effect because they inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis.
  • Most cephalosporins are excreted mainly by tubular secretion and therefore require dose modification in renal impairment.
  • Cefoperazone is excreted in bile, whereas ceftriaxone is excreted in both urine and bile.
  • Probenecid increases the duration of action of cephalosporins because it blocks their tubular secretion.
  • First-generation cephalosporins do not cross the blood-brain barrier, whereas cefuroxime, ceftriaxone and cefotaxime enter the cerebrospinal fluid.
  • Fifth-generation cephalosporins are effective against bacteria with altered penicillin-binding proteins and therefore are useful against MRSA.
  • Ceftazidime, cefoperazone and cefepime are important antipseudomonal cephalosporins because they are effective against Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
