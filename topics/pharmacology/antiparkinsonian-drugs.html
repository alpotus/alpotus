ANTIPARKINSONIAN DRUGS

Parkinson's disease (PD) was first described by Sir James Parkinson.

It is characterized by:

  • Tremor
  • Rigidity
  • Bradykinesia (slowness of movements)
  • Loss of postural reflexes

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY OF PARKINSONISM

Normally there is a balance between dopamine (DA) and acetylcholine (ACh) in the striatum.

FLOWCHART: Normal state -> Dopamine activity = Acetylcholine activity -> Normal movement FLOWCHART: Idiopathic Parkinsonism -> Degeneration of dopamine-containing neurons in substantia nigra -> Dopamine deficiency develops in striatum -> Relative cholinergic overactivity occurs -> [Tremor | Rigidity | Bradykinesia | Loss of postural reflexes]
Mnemonic
"Parkinson = DA Down, ACh Dominates"

CLASSIFICATION OF ANTIPARKINSONIAN DRUGS

1. Drugs Influencing Brain Dopaminergic System

TABLE: | Group | Drugs | | Dopamine precursor | Levodopa (L-Dopa) | | Dopamine agonists | Bromocriptine, Pramipexole, Ropinirole | | NMDA-receptor antagonist | Amantadine | | MAO-B inhibitors | Selegiline (Deprenyl), Rasagiline | | COMT inhibitors | Tolcapone, Entacapone |

2. Drugs Influencing Brain Cholinergic System

TABLE: | Group | Drugs | | Centrally acting anticholinergics | Benztropine, Benzhexol (Trihexyphenidyl), Procyclidine, Biperiden | | Antihistaminics with anticholinergic activity | Promethazine, Diphenhydramine, Orphenadrine |

Main aim of treatment:

FLOWCHART: Treatment of Parkinsonism -> [Increase dopamine activity | Decrease cholinergic activity in the striatum]

LEVODOPA (L-DOPA)

Levodopa is the main drug for treatment of idiopathic Parkinsonism.

Dopamine itself does not cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB), therefore its precursor levodopa is used.

Mechanism of Action

FLOWCHART: Levodopa is administered orally -> Levodopa crosses BBB -> In dopaminergic neurons of striatum: Levodopa is converted to dopamine by dopa decarboxylase -> Dopamine acts on D2 receptors in basal ganglia -> Rigidity, bradykinesia and tremor improve
Mnemonic
"Levodopa = Leads dopamine into the brain"

Why Levodopa is Combined with Carbidopa/Benserazide

A large amount of levodopa is converted to dopamine in peripheral tissues. Only 2–3% enters the brain.

FLOWCHART: Levodopa given alone -> Peripheral decarboxylase converts much of it into dopamine outside brain -> Less levodopa reaches CNS -> More peripheral side effects occur FLOWCHART: Levodopa + Carbidopa/Benserazide -> Peripheral decarboxylase inhibited -> More levodopa reaches the brain -> More dopamine formed in basal ganglia -> Better antiparkinsonian effect with fewer peripheral adverse effects

PHARMACOKINETICS OF LEVODOPA

FLOWCHART: Levodopa is absorbed from small intestine by active transport -> Dietary amino acids compete for absorption -> Absorption decreases if taken with food

Therefore: Levodopa should be taken 30–60 minutes before meals

Important metabolites:

  • Homovanillic acid (HVA)
  • 3,4-Dihydroxyphenylacetic acid (DOPAC)

Metabolites are excreted in urine.

Mnemonic
"Levodopa before Lunch"

ADVERSE EFFECTS OF LEVODOPA

1. Gastrointestinal Effects

FLOWCHART: Peripheral dopamine stimulates CTZ -> Nausea, vomiting and anorexia occur

2. Cardiovascular Effects

FLOWCHART: Dopamine formed in periphery -> [β-receptor stimulation | Vasodilatation] -> [Tachycardia | Palpitation | Postural hypotension]

Levodopa should be used cautiously in ischemic heart disease.

3. Dyskinesias

FLOWCHART: Excess dopamine in CNS -> Abnormal involuntary movements develop -> Tics, tremors and choreoathetoid movements occur

Tolerance does not develop. Dose reduction decreases severity.

4. Mental Changes

  • Insomnia
  • Confusion
  • Delusions
  • Euphoria
  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Hallucinations
  • Nightmares

5. Alteration in Taste Sensation

Mnemonic
"Levodopa gives the 4 D problems"
  • D = Dyskinesia
  • D = Delusions
  • D = Dreams (nightmares)
  • D = Drop in BP

ON–OFF PHENOMENON AND WEARING OFF

FLOWCHART: After 3–5 years of treatment -> Disease progresses -> Control becomes poor -> Patient fluctuates between -> ["ON" phase = relief of symptoms + dyskinesia | "OFF" phase = loss of drug effect and return of symptoms]

This is called the ON/OFF phenomenon.

FLOWCHART: Towards end of dose interval -> Plasma level of levodopa falls -> Symptoms reappear

This is called WEARING OFF phenomenon.

Improvement: Give levodopa in smaller and more frequent doses

Mnemonic
"ON = Overactive with dyskinesia
OFF = Old Parkinson symptoms return"

PERIPHERAL DECARBOXYLASE INHIBITORS

Drugs:

  • Carbidopa
  • Benserazide

These drugs do not cross the BBB.

Mechanism

FLOWCHART: Peripheral decarboxylase inhibited -> Less levodopa converted to dopamine in periphery -> More levodopa enters brain

Common combinations

TABLE: | Combination | Name | | L-Dopa + Carbidopa (4:1 or 10:1) | Co-careldopa | | L-Dopa + Benserazide (4:1) | Fixed combination |

Advantages

  1. Dose of levodopa reduced by 75%
  2. Plasma half-life of levodopa prolonged
  3. Nausea and vomiting reduced
  4. Tachycardia, hypotension and arrhythmias reduced
  5. Pyridoxine no longer abolishes effect of levodopa
  6. Sustained-release preparation reduces ON/OFF phenomenon

However, these drugs do NOT reduce:

  • Postural hypotension
  • Dyskinesias
  • Behavioural changes
Mnemonic
"CARBIDOPA CARES for levodopa"
  • C = CNS availability increased
  • A = Adverse effects reduced
  • R = Reduced dose needed
  • E = Effect prolonged

DOPAMINE RECEPTOR AGONISTS

Drugs:

  • Bromocriptine
  • Ropinirole
  • Pramipexole

These drugs directly stimulate dopamine receptors.

FLOWCHART: Direct dopamine receptor stimulation -> Relieve symptoms of Parkinsonism -> Longer duration than levodopa -> Useful in patients with ON/OFF fluctuations

BROMOCRIPTINE

Bromocriptine is an ergot derivative.

FLOWCHART: Strong D2 agonist + Partial D1 agonist -> Improves Parkinson symptoms

Adverse Effects

  • Anorexia
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Constipation
  • Postural hypotension
  • Cardiac arrhythmias
  • Digital vasospasm
  • Dyskinesias
  • Headache
  • Confusion
  • Hallucinations
  • Nasal congestion

Contraindications

  • Mental illness
  • Recent MI
  • Peptic ulcer
  • Peripheral vascular disease
Mnemonic
"Bromocriptine = Bad Ergot Effects"

ROPINIROLE AND PRAMIPEXOLE

These are non-ergoline derivatives.

TABLE: | Drug | Action | | Ropinirole | D2 agonist | | Pramipexole | More selective for D3 |

Advantages

  • No ergot-related adverse effects
  • Neuroprotective effect
  • Less dyskinesia than levodopa
  • Less ON/OFF fluctuations

Ropinirole is also useful in restless leg syndrome.

Adverse Effects

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Confusion
  • Fatigue
  • Somnolence
  • Hallucinations
  • Postural hypotension
  • Dyskinesia
  • Sudden irresistible sleep attacks during daytime
Mnemonic
"Pramipexole and Ropinirole may make the patient Sleep Suddenly"

COMT INHIBITORS

Drugs:

  • Tolcapone
  • Entacapone

These are reversible COMT inhibitors.

FLOWCHART: COMT normally converts levodopa to 3-O-methyldopa -> Tolcapone/Entacapone inhibit COMT -> More levodopa remains available -> More levodopa enters brain -> ON time is prolonged TABLE: | Drug | Site of Action | | Tolcapone | Peripheral + Central | | Entacapone | Peripheral only |

These drugs are used as adjuncts with levodopa-carbidopa.

Available combination:

Levodopa + Carbidopa + Entacapone

Adverse Effects

  • Dyskinesia
  • Nausea
  • Diarrhoea
  • Confusion
  • Hypotension
  • Hallucinations
Tolcapone may rarely cause fulminant hepatitis. Therefore it should be avoided in liver disease.

Entacapone does not cause hepatotoxicity.

Mnemonic
"Tolcapone Troubles the liver"

MAO-B INHIBITORS

Drugs:

  • Selegiline (Deprenyl)
  • Rasagiline

These drugs selectively and irreversibly inhibit MAO-B in the brain.

FLOWCHART: MAO-B in brain normally breaks down dopamine -> Selegiline/Rasagiline inhibit MAO-B -> Dopamine breakdown decreases -> Dopamine level in brain increases -> Symptoms improve and progression slows

Additional benefits

  • Neuroprotective effect
  • Prolong action of levodopa
  • Reduce ON/OFF and wearing-off phenomena

Used:

  • Alone in mild disease
  • With levodopa in advanced disease

Rasagiline is:

  • More potent
  • Longer acting
  • Single daily dose sufficient

Selegiline metabolites:

  • Amphetamine
  • Methamphetamine

These may cause:

  • Insomnia
  • Anxiety
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
Mnemonic
"Selegiline stimulates like amphetamine"

AMANTADINE

Amantadine is an antiviral drug used in influenza A.

It is also useful in Parkinsonism.

FLOWCHART: Amantadine increases synthesis and release of dopamine in brain -> Also blocks NMDA receptors -> Glutamate neurotransmission decreases -> Parkinson symptoms improve

It is less effective than levodopa. Therefore it is mainly used in mild Parkinsonism.

It may be combined with levodopa.

Adverse Effects

  • Headache
  • Heart failure
  • Hypotension
  • Hallucinations
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Constipation
  • Dry mouth
  • Insomnia
  • Livedo reticularis
Mnemonic
"Amantadine causes A-MAN-TI problems"
  • A = Anxiety/insomnia
  • M = Mottled skin (livedo reticularis)
  • A = Anticholinergic effects
  • N = Nausea
  • T = Tired heart (heart failure)
  • I = Illusions (hallucinations)

CENTRAL ANTICHOLINERGICS

Drugs:

  • Benzhexol (Trihexyphenidyl)
  • Benztropine
  • Procyclidine
  • Biperiden

These drugs are especially useful in:

  • Drug-induced Parkinsonism
  • Idiopathic Parkinsonism with predominant tremor and rigidity
FLOWCHART: Relative cholinergic overactivity in striatum -> Central anticholinergic drug blocks cholinergic activity -> Balance between DA and ACh restored -> Tremor and rigidity improve

These drugs have little effect on hypokinesia.

Adverse Effects

  • Dry mouth
  • Confusion
  • Constipation
  • Blurring of vision
  • Drowsiness
  • Hallucinations
  • Urinary retention
Mnemonic
"Anticholinergics Dry and Dim the patient"

ANTIHISTAMINICS WITH ANTICHOLINERGIC ACTION

Drugs:

  • Promethazine
  • Diphenhydramine
  • Orphenadrine

These drugs decrease cholinergic overactivity in basal ganglia.

WHY LEVODOPA DOES NOT WORK IN DRUG-INDUCED PARKINSONISM

FLOWCHART: Drug-induced Parkinsonism -> Dopamine receptors are blocked by drugs -> There is no true dopamine deficiency -> Levodopa cannot produce effect

Reasons:

  1. Dopamine receptors are blocked in basal ganglia.
  2. There is no deficiency of dopamine in striatum.

DRUG INTERACTIONS

1. Levodopa + Nonselective MAO Inhibitors

Hypertensive crisis may occur.
FLOWCHART: MAO inhibited -> Dopamine metabolism decreases -> Plasma dopamine rises greatly -> Hypertensive crisis may occur

2. Levodopa + Pyridoxine

FLOWCHART: Pyridoxine increases peripheral conversion of levodopa to dopamine -> Less levodopa reaches brain -> Therapeutic effect decreases

3. Levodopa + Antihypertensive Drugs

FLOWCHART: Both cause fall in BP -> Postural hypotension becomes worse

4. Levodopa + Metoclopramide / Phenothiazines

FLOWCHART: Metoclopramide or phenothiazine crosses BBB -> Blocks D2 receptors in basal ganglia -> Opposes effect of levodopa -> Drug-induced Parkinsonism may occur

Domperidone poorly crosses BBB. Therefore it does not interfere with levodopa.

Mnemonic
"Domperidone Does not Disturb dopamine in brain"

SUMMARY OF MECHANISM OF ACTION OF ANTIPARKINSONIAN DRUGS

TABLE: | Drug | Mechanism of Action | | Levodopa | Converted to dopamine in basal ganglia → acts on D2 receptors | | Ropinirole, Pramipexole | Dopamine receptor agonists | | Selegiline, Rasagiline | Inhibit MAO-B in brain → decrease dopamine breakdown | | Entacapone, Tolcapone | Inhibit COMT → increase levodopa availability | | Amantadine | Increases dopamine release + NMDA antagonist | | Benzhexol, Benztropine | Central anticholinergics → reduce cholinergic activity |

"L-Dopa Leads dopamine
Agonists Act directly
MAO-B inhibitors Maintain dopamine
COMT inhibitors Continue levodopa
Anticholinergics Control ACh"
