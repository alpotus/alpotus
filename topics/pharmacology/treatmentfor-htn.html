Treatment of hypertension includes both lifestyle modification and drug therapy. Lifestyle measures should be advised to every patient, even when antihypertensive drugs are prescribed.

Lifestyle Modification

Lifestyle modification is the first step in the management of hypertension.

TABLE: | Lifestyle Measure | Benefit | | Weight reduction | Reduces blood pressure and cardiovascular risk | | Restriction of dietary sodium | Decreases sodium and water retention | | Restriction of alcohol intake | Prevents rise in blood pressure | | Regular exercise | Improves vascular function and reduces blood pressure | | Mental relaxation and stress reduction | Decreases sympathetic overactivity | | Smoking cessation | Reduces cardiovascular risk | | Potassium-rich diet | Helps counteract sodium and lowers blood pressure |

The effect of sodium restriction is particularly important.

FLOWCHART: Excess dietary sodium causes sodium and water retention -> Blood volume increases -> Peripheral resistance and blood pressure rise FLOWCHART: Dietary sodium intake is restricted -> Sodium and water retention decrease -> Blood volume falls -> Blood pressure decreases

A potassium-rich diet also contributes to blood pressure reduction.

FLOWCHART: Diet contains more potassium-rich foods -> Renal sodium excretion increases -> The effect of excess sodium decreases -> Blood pressure falls

The DASH diet is recommended in hypertension.

DASH Diet Includes │ ├── Fruits ├── Vegetables ├── Whole grains ├── Nuts ├── Fish ├── Lean meat └── Low-fat dairy products
FLOWCHART: The DASH diet provides less sodium and more potassium, calcium and fibre -> Vascular function improves -> Blood pressure decreases -> Long-term cardiovascular risk becomes lower

Drug Treatment of Hypertension

The selection of antihypertensive drugs depends on multiple patient-related factors.

TABLE: | Factor Affecting Drug Selection | Importance | | Comorbid disease | Determines which drug is preferred or avoided | | Associated complications | Helps choose drugs that improve prognosis | | Age | Younger and older patients respond differently | | Sex | Some drugs are contraindicated in pregnancy | | Cost of the drug | Influences long-term adherence | | Concomitant drugs | Important because of drug interactions |

First-Line Antihypertensive Drugs

TABLE: | First-Line Drug Group | Examples | | ACE inhibitors | Enalapril, Ramipril, Lisinopril | | ARBs | Losartan, Telmisartan, Valsartan | | Calcium channel blockers | Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Diltiazem | | Thiazide diuretics | Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone |

Treatment is usually started with a single drug.

FLOWCHART: Patient is diagnosed with hypertension -> A single first-line antihypertensive drug is started -> The patient is reassessed after several weeks -> If blood pressure becomes controlled, the same treatment is continued -> If blood pressure remains high, the dose is increased or combination therapy is started

Age is important in choosing the initial drug.

TABLE: | Age Group | Preferred Initial Drug | | Younger than 55 years | ACE inhibitor or ARB | | 55 years or older | Calcium channel blocker or thiazide diuretic | FLOWCHART: Patient is younger than 55 years -> The renin-angiotensin system contributes more strongly to hypertension -> ACE inhibitor or ARB is preferred FLOWCHART: Patient is 55 years or older -> Peripheral vascular resistance contributes more strongly to hypertension -> Calcium channel blocker or thiazide diuretic is preferred

Combination Therapy

Combination therapy is used when a single drug does not adequately control blood pressure. It may also be started initially in patients with markedly elevated blood pressure.

TABLE: | Preferred Combination | Reason | | ACE inhibitor or ARB + Calcium channel blocker | Produces strong and complementary reduction in blood pressure | | ACE inhibitor or ARB + Thiazide diuretic | Enhances antihypertensive effect and balances potassium | | ACE inhibitor or ARB + Calcium channel blocker + Thiazide | Used when two-drug therapy is inadequate | FLOWCHART: A single antihypertensive drug is started -> Blood pressure remains above target -> A second antihypertensive drug with a different mechanism is added -> Blood pressure falls more effectively FLOWCHART: ACE inhibitor or ARB decreases angiotensin II activity -> Calcium channel blocker relaxes vascular smooth muscle -> Both drugs act through different mechanisms -> The antihypertensive effect becomes greater

If blood pressure still remains uncontrolled, additional drugs such as clonidine or beta blockers may be added.

Certain combinations should be avoided.

TABLE: | Combination to be Avoided | Reason | | Beta blocker + Thiazide diuretic | Increased risk of diabetes mellitus | | Verapamil or diltiazem + Beta blocker | Excessive cardiac depression, bradycardia and AV block | FLOWCHART: Verapamil or diltiazem is combined with a beta blocker -> Both drugs depress cardiac conduction and contractility -> Heart rate falls excessively -> Severe bradycardia or atrioventricular block may occur
⚠️
Verapamil or diltiazem combined with a beta blocker may produce severe bradycardia or atrioventricular block.

Drugs Preferred in Specific Comorbid Conditions

TABLE: | Comorbid Condition | Preferred Drugs | | Angina or post-myocardial infarction | Beta blockers, ACE inhibitors, ARBs | | Congestive cardiac failure or left ventricular failure | ACE inhibitors, ARBs, loop diuretics | | Diabetes mellitus and diabetic nephropathy | ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers | | Post-stroke secondary prevention | ACE inhibitors, ARBs, thiazides | | Bronchial asthma or COPD | Calcium channel blockers | | Benign prostatic hyperplasia | Selective α1-blockers | | Pregnancy | Sustained-release nifedipine, labetalol, α-methyldopa, hydralazine | | Hypertensive emergency | Sodium nitroprusside, labetalol, nitroglycerin | FLOWCHART: Patient has hypertension with diabetes mellitus -> There is a high risk of diabetic nephropathy -> ACE inhibitor or ARB is selected -> Blood pressure decreases and kidney damage slows FLOWCHART: Patient has hypertension with benign prostatic hyperplasia -> An α1-blocker is selected -> Blood pressure decreases -> Urinary obstruction due to enlarged prostate also improves

Drugs to be Avoided in Specific Conditions

TABLE: | Clinical Condition | Drug to be Avoided | Reason | | Bronchial asthma or COPD | Beta blockers | May precipitate bronchospasm | | Peripheral vascular disease | Nonselective beta blockers | May worsen peripheral circulation | | Diabetes mellitus | Beta blocker + thiazide combination | Increases risk of diabetes and masks hypoglycaemia | | Hyperlipidaemia | Thiazides and some beta blockers | May worsen lipid profile | | Gout | Thiazides | Increase serum uric acid | | Sexually active males | Some beta blockers and thiazides | May cause impotence and decreased libido |

Hypertensive Urgency and Hypertensive Emergency

TABLE: | Condition | Definition | | Hypertensive urgency | Severe elevation of blood pressure without progressive end-organ damage | | Hypertensive emergency | Blood pressure greater than 180/120 mm Hg with progressive end-organ damage |

Examples of end-organ damage include retinopathy, renal dysfunction, myocardial infarction and hypertensive encephalopathy.

FLOWCHART: Blood pressure rises above 180/120 mm Hg -> There is no evidence of end-organ damage -> The condition is called hypertensive urgency FLOWCHART: Blood pressure rises above 180/120 mm Hg -> The patient develops retinal damage, renal failure, myocardial infarction or encephalopathy -> The condition is called hypertensive emergency -> Immediate treatment is required

Treatment of Hypertensive Urgency

Hypertensive urgency is treated with oral antihypertensive drugs.

TABLE: | Oral Drug Used in Hypertensive Urgency | Examples | | Central sympatholytic | Clonidine | | Combined α and β blocker | Labetalol | | Dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker | Amlodipine | FLOWCHART: Patient has severe hypertension without end-organ damage -> An oral antihypertensive drug is administered -> Blood pressure gradually decreases over several hours -> The patient is stabilized without rapid fall in blood pressure

Treatment of Hypertensive Emergency

In hypertensive emergency, blood pressure should be reduced gradually.

TABLE: | Time Period | Target Reduction in Blood Pressure | | First 1 hour | Not more than 25% reduction | | Next 2–6 hours | Reduce to approximately 160/100 mm Hg | | Next 48 hours | Gradually reduce to normal | FLOWCHART: Blood pressure is lowered too rapidly -> Blood flow to the brain, heart and kidneys suddenly falls -> Ischaemic damage may occur

Therefore blood pressure is reduced gradually in hypertensive emergency.

Intravenous drugs are used because they act rapidly and can be carefully controlled.

TABLE: | Intravenous Drug | Special Situation or Important Point | | Labetalol | Commonly used; avoided in asthma, COPD and heart failure | | Nicardipine | Rapid acting; may cause reflex tachycardia | | Nitroglycerin | Particularly useful when myocardial ischaemia is present | | Sodium nitroprusside | Very potent; monitor for cyanide toxicity | | Furosemide | Used when hypertensive emergency is associated with acute pulmonary oedema | | Clevidipine | Short-acting calcium channel blocker | | Esmolol | Preferred in aortic dissection | | Hydralazine | Useful especially in pregnancy | | Fenoldopam | Useful in hypertensive emergency with renal dysfunction | | Enalaprilat | Intravenous ACE inhibitor | | Phentolamine | Used in hypertensive crisis due to pheochromocytoma |

Nicardipine

TABLE: | Feature | Description | | Initial infusion rate | 5 mg/hour | | Maximum infusion rate | 15 mg/hour | | Important property | Rapid onset and short duration | | Important adverse effect | Reflex tachycardia | FLOWCHART: Intravenous nicardipine is started at 5 mg/hour -> Blood pressure decreases rapidly -> The infusion rate may be increased gradually -> A maximum rate of 15 mg/hour may be used if required

Labetalol

TABLE: | Feature | Description | | Initial dose | 20 mg intravenous bolus | | Repeated dose | 20–80 mg every 10 minutes | | Maximum total dose | 300 mg | | Infusion rate | 0.5–2 mg/min | | Contraindications | Bronchial asthma, COPD, heart failure | FLOWCHART: Intravenous labetalol is administered -> Alpha blockade produces vasodilatation -> Beta blockade prevents reflex tachycardia -> Blood pressure falls in a controlled manner

Nitroglycerin

TABLE: | Feature | Description | | Initial dose | 5 mcg/min | | Maximum dose | 100 mcg/min | FLOWCHART: Nitroglycerin generates nitric oxide in vascular smooth muscle -> Veins dilate predominantly -> Venous return and preload decrease -> Cardiac workload falls -> Blood pressure decreases

Sodium Nitroprusside

Sodium nitroprusside is one of the most powerful intravenous vasodilators used in hypertensive emergency.

TABLE: | Feature | Description | | Initial infusion rate | 0.25–0.5 mcg/kg/min | | Maximum dose | 10 mcg/kg/min | | Important precaution | Monitor for cyanide toxicity |

Sodium nitroprusside acts by releasing nitric oxide.

FLOWCHART: Sodium nitroprusside enters the circulation -> Nitric oxide is released -> Nitric oxide relaxes vascular smooth muscle -> Both arterioles and veins dilate -> Peripheral vascular resistance and venous return decrease -> Afterload and preload decrease -> Blood pressure falls rapidly FLOWCHART: Sodium nitroprusside is given for a prolonged period or at a high dose -> Cyanide ions accumulate in the body -> Cyanide toxicity may develop -> The patient may develop metabolic acidosis, arrhythmia and neurological symptoms
⚠️
Sodium nitroprusside given for a prolonged period or at a high dose may cause cyanide toxicity with metabolic acidosis, arrhythmia and neurological symptoms.
