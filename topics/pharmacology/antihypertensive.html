Antihypertensives

🩺
Hypertension is a common cardiovascular disease affecting populations worldwide. Persistent and sustained elevation of blood pressure gradually damages the heart, brain, kidneys and eyes.

Introduction

Hypertension is broadly divided into two types:

TABLE: | Type of Hypertension | Explanation | | Primary (Essential) Hypertension | This is the most common type. No specific underlying cause can be identified. | | Secondary Hypertension | This develops due to an identifiable cause such as renal disease, vascular disease or endocrine disorder. |

Blood Pressure

Normal blood pressure according to recent guidelines is:

TABLE: | Parameter | Normal Value | | Systolic Blood Pressure (SBP) | <120 mm Hg | | Diastolic Blood Pressure (DBP) | <80 mm Hg |

Systolic blood pressure is the maximum pressure generated during ventricular systole, whereas diastolic blood pressure is the minimum pressure present during ventricular diastole.

FLOWCHART: Ventricular contraction occurs -> The highest pressure generated in arteries is called systolic blood pressure -> Ventricular relaxation occurs -> The lowest pressure remaining in arteries is called diastolic blood pressure

Pulse pressure and mean arterial pressure are calculated from SBP and DBP.

TABLE: | Parameter | Formula | | Pulse Pressure (PP) | PP = SBP − DBP | | Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) | MAP = DBP + 1/3(PP) |

Classification of Antihypertensive Drugs

TABLE: | Major Group | Drugs | | ACE Inhibitors | Captopril, Enalapril, Lisinopril, Perindopril, Ramipril, Benazepril, Fosinopril, Imidapril | | Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs) | Losartan, Candesartan, Irbesartan, Valsartan, Telmisartan, Olmesartan, Eprosartan | | Direct Renin Inhibitor | Aliskiren | | Calcium Channel Blockers | Diltiazem, Verapamil, Nifedipine, Amlodipine, Cilnidipine, Nicardipine, Benidipine, Isradipine, Felodipine, Lacidipine, Lercanidipine | | Thiazide Diuretics | Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone, Indapamide | | Loop Diuretics | Furosemide, Bumetanide, Torsemide | | Potassium-Sparing Diuretics | Amiloride, Triamterene, Spironolactone, Eplerenone | | Centrally Acting Sympatholytics | Clonidine, α-Methyldopa | | β-Blockers | Atenolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol, Betaxolol, Propranolol, Timolol | | β-Blockers with Additional α-Blocking Activity | Labetalol, Carvedilol, Nebivolol | | α-Blockers | Prazosin, Terazosin, Doxazosin, Phenoxybenzamine, Phentolamine | | Vasodilators | Hydralazine, Minoxidil, Diazoxide, Fenoldopam, Nitroglycerin, Sodium Nitroprusside |

The major antihypertensive drugs act at different sites in the cardiovascular system.

FLOWCHART: Kidney releases renin -> Renin converts angiotensinogen into angiotensin I -> ACE converts angiotensin I into angiotensin II -> Angiotensin II causes vasoconstriction and aldosterone release -> Peripheral resistance and sodium-water retention increase -> Blood pressure rises
  • ACE inhibitors stop conversion of angiotensin I into angiotensin II.
  • ARBs block the action of angiotensin II on its receptor.
  • Aliskiren blocks renin itself.
  • Diuretics reduce sodium and water content.
  • β-blockers reduce cardiac output and renin release.
  • Calcium channel blockers relax blood vessels and reduce cardiac activity.
  • Vasodilators directly dilate arteries and veins.

Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitors

ACE inhibitors are frequently used as first-line antihypertensive drugs.

TABLE: | ACE Inhibitors | Examples | | Active drugs | Captopril, Lisinopril | | Prodrugs | Enalapril, Perindopril, Ramipril, Fosinopril, Benazepril, Quinapril, Trandolapril, Imidapril |

Mechanism of Action of ACE Inhibitors

ACE inhibitors prevent the formation of angiotensin II and also prevent degradation of bradykinin. Both effects together reduce blood pressure.

FLOWCHART: Angiotensin I is normally converted into angiotensin II by angiotensin-converting enzyme -> ACE inhibitor blocks this conversion -> Angiotensin II levels fall -> Arterioles dilate -> Peripheral vascular resistance decreases -> Blood pressure falls

At the same time, reduction in angiotensin II also decreases aldosterone secretion.

FLOWCHART: Angiotensin II normally stimulates aldosterone release from adrenal cortex -> ACE inhibitor decreases angiotensin II formation -> Aldosterone secretion decreases -> Less sodium and water are retained in the body -> Blood volume decreases -> Blood pressure falls further

ACE inhibitors also decrease sympathetic nervous system activity.

FLOWCHART: Reduced angiotensin II formation occurs -> Sympathetic stimulation produced by angiotensin II becomes less -> Heart rate and vascular tone reduce slightly -> Additional reduction in blood pressure occurs

Another important action is due to bradykinin.

FLOWCHART: ACE normally breaks down bradykinin -> ACE inhibitor prevents bradykinin degradation -> Bradykinin levels rise -> Bradykinin stimulates prostaglandin synthesis and vasodilation -> Blood vessels relax further -> Blood pressure decreases

These effects gradually develop over about 2 weeks. ACE inhibitors also reverse ventricular hypertrophy and vascular hypertrophy.

Pharmacokinetics of ACE Inhibitors

Most ACE inhibitors are given orally. Enalaprilat is the only ACE inhibitor that can be administered intravenously during hypertensive emergency.

Food reduces absorption of captopril, therefore captopril should be taken 1 hour before meals.

TABLE: | Drug | Active Drug or Prodrug | Effect of Food | Duration of Action | Route of Excretion | | Captopril | Active | Food decreases absorption, therefore given 1 hour before meals | 8–12 h | Kidney | | Enalapril | Prodrug | Food does not significantly affect absorption | 24 h | Kidney | | Lisinopril | Active | Food does not affect absorption | >24 h | Kidney | | Perindopril | Prodrug | Food does not significantly affect absorption | >24 h | Kidney | | Ramipril | Prodrug | Food affects rate of absorption | >24 h | Kidney and bile | | Fosinopril | Prodrug | Food does not significantly affect absorption | 24 h | Kidney and stools | | Benazepril | Prodrug | Food does not significantly affect absorption | 24 h | Kidney | | Trandolapril | Prodrug | Food does not significantly affect absorption | >24 h | Kidney and stools | | Quinapril | Prodrug | Food decreases absorption | >24 h | Kidney | | Imidapril | Prodrug | Food decreases absorption, therefore given before meals | >24 h | Kidney |

ACE inhibitors poorly cross the blood-brain barrier. Most are metabolized in the liver and excreted through the kidneys.

Adverse Effects of ACE Inhibitors

The most important adverse effect is dry cough.

FLOWCHART: ACE inhibitor is administered -> Bradykinin degradation in the lungs decreases -> Bradykinin accumulates in respiratory passages -> Persistent dry cough develops -> The cough disappears after stopping the drug

Angioedema is another serious adverse effect.

FLOWCHART: ACE inhibitor increases bradykinin levels -> Excessive vascular permeability develops in lips, face, tongue and airway -> Angioedema occurs -> Airway obstruction may develop -> The drug must be stopped immediately and airway should be protected

Mnemonic for Adverse effects - CAPTOPRIL

TABLE: | Adverse Effect | Explanation | | Dry cough | Due to increased bradykinin in lungs | | Angioedema | Swelling of lips, tongue, mouth and larynx due to bradykinin accumulation | | Proteinuria | Rare; drug should be stopped | | First-dose hypotension | More common in volume depletion or congestive heart failure | | Hyperkalaemia | More common in renal insufficiency or with potassium-sparing diuretics | | Neutropenia | Rare | | Rash and itching | Usually mild | | Dysgeusia (loss of taste) | Especially with captopril | | Teratogenicity | Causes fetal hypotension, growth retardation, renal failure and neonatal death |
⚠️
ACE inhibitors are contraindicated during pregnancy.

ACE Inhibitors in Renal Artery Stenosis

Normally, when renal blood flow decreases, angiotensin II constricts the efferent arteriole and maintains glomerular filtration rate.

FLOWCHART: Renal artery stenosis decreases blood supply to kidney -> The kidney produces more angiotensin II -> Angiotensin II constricts the efferent arteriole -> Glomerular filtration pressure is maintained

When an ACE inhibitor is given, this protective mechanism is lost.

FLOWCHART: ACE inhibitor blocks angiotensin II formation -> Efferent arteriole cannot constrict -> Glomerular filtration pressure falls -> Acute renal failure may occur

Therefore ACE inhibitors are contraindicated in:

TABLE: | Contraindication | Reason | | Bilateral renal artery stenosis | Can precipitate acute renal failure | | Single functioning kidney with renal artery stenosis | Can precipitate severe renal failure | | Pregnancy | Teratogenic |

Drug Interactions of ACE Inhibitors

TABLE: | Combination | Result | | ACE inhibitors + Potassium-sparing diuretics | Dangerous hyperkalaemia may occur | | ACE inhibitors + Lithium | Lithium toxicity increases because renal elimination decreases | | ACE inhibitors + NSAIDs | Antihypertensive effect decreases because NSAIDs cause sodium and water retention | | ACE inhibitors + Thiazide diuretics | Antihypertensive effect increases and potassium balance is maintained |

The interaction with thiazides is often beneficial.

FLOWCHART: Thiazide diuretic causes sodium and water loss -> ACE inhibitor prevents compensatory sodium retention -> Both drugs act together -> A greater fall in blood pressure is produced

Therapeutic Uses of ACE Inhibitors

ACE inhibitors are especially useful in patients with hypertension associated with diabetes, nephropathy, heart failure or myocardial infarction.

TABLE: | Clinical Condition | Why ACE Inhibitors are Useful | | Hypertension | They reduce cardiovascular and cerebrovascular morbidity and mortality | | Young hypertensive patients (<55 years) | They are particularly effective in this age group | | Hypertension with diabetes | They delay progression of diabetic nephropathy | | Hypertension with chronic kidney disease | They reduce intraglomerular pressure and proteinuria | | Congestive heart failure | They reduce preload, afterload and ventricular remodeling | | Acute myocardial infarction | They improve short-term and long-term survival if started within 24 hours | | Left ventricular hypertrophy | They reverse ventricular remodeling | | Scleroderma renal crisis | They improve survival by blocking angiotensin II effects in renal vessels |

The benefit in diabetic nephropathy is especially important.

FLOWCHART: Diabetes causes increased intraglomerular pressure -> The renal glomerulus gradually becomes damaged -> ACE inhibitor dilates the efferent arteriole -> Intraglomerular pressure decreases -> Microalbuminuria decreases and kidney damage slows

Similarly, after myocardial infarction, ACE inhibitors prevent harmful remodeling.

FLOWCHART: Myocardial infarction damages a part of the left ventricle -> The ventricle enlarges and remodels abnormally -> ACE inhibitor reduces angiotensin II mediated remodeling -> Cardiac function improves and survival increases
